# 第001章：在叫出魔鬼名字的那一刻，魔鬼就被消解。

## 原文

### 第1页
道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名天地之始，有名万物之母。

——《道德经》第一章

### 第2页
耶和华神用土所造成的野地各样走兽和空中各样飞鸟都带到那人面前，看他叫什么。那人怎样叫各样的活物，那就是它的名字。那人便给一切牲畜和空中飞鸟、野地走兽都起了名。

《创世记》第2章第19-20节


## 释义

### 第3页
“名”不是简单的标签，它是对事物形貌的限定，是一种世界进入秩序的方式。《创世记》中的命名故事揭示：当人类给世界起名，事情便从无限的可能性坍缩为一个确定的形态。

“道”则与此相反：它是未可名、不可道的根源。它既不是某一种事物，也不是某一个名字所能涵盖的存在，而是背后那一片无限流动的无名场域。

### 第4页
埃及神话里，真名（Ren）就是神祇权力的根本。伊西斯通过获得太阳神拉的真名，获得了对拉的控制——这说明，一旦名字被捕获，它就赋予掌握者某种支配力量。

日本“言灵”信仰则进一步强调：语言本身携带灵力，真名若泄露，事物的自由便会受限。因此，许多年长的日本人会拥有一个隐秘的真名和一个公开的假名。

### 第5页
现代社会的命名行为更加复杂。科学家用命名来分类自然，艺术家用名字来传递情感，政治家用称谓来建立权威。但无论命名铺展开来多么细密，人们仍常常幻想能通过“最终的名”把握宇宙的本体。

这正是与《道德经》所说“名可名，非常名”相悖的地方。将“道”当成可被固定称呼的对象，便失去了它本该保持的无名之质。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第6页
- 真名（Ren）：埃及神话中神灵力量的本源，名字本身即意味着对该神的控制。
- 言灵：日本文化中的语言神秘论，认为言语能够生成现实、改变命运。
- 坍缩：量子力学隐喻，观测使叠加态变为确定状态，类似命名使可能性收敛。
- 无名：指《道德经》中道之本体，超越语言与名字的存在。

### 第7页
- 《创世记》2:19-20：亚当给万物命名，成为对世界秩序的一种主宰。
- 《道德经》第一章：名与无名并列，提醒人们语言与存在之间的界限。
- 伊西斯与拉：古埃及神话中通过真名获取主权的故事，体现名字与权力的关系。
- 《阴阳师》引用：强调名字对事物形貌的束缚力。

## 正文

### 第8页
当亚当为所有生灵命名时，他并非仅在给它们贴标签。他同时在为世界建立一套可理解的秩序：每一个名字都意味着一种区分，一种归属，也是一种对该物的可控性。

在这张图景中，名字是力量的入口。它让混沌有了边界，使无数潜在的形态落定为一种可说的存在。

### 第9页
反观古埃及，真名直接成为掌权的钥匙。伊西斯知晓拉的真名之后，不再只是崇拜者，而是掌握者。名字的泄露使神圣之物从隐秘转为可支配。

这种观念并不只是神话的修辞，而是从一种更古老的世界观出发：语言本身能够参与现实的生成。

### 第10页
在日本，言灵信仰把这种力量再次压缩为个体经验。名字被分为“真名”和“假名”，其中隐秘的真名保留着一个人真正的核心，而公开的假名则成为社会关系中的符号。

正是在这种文化里，名字的不完全披露成为对自由的守护；真名若被揭开，事物便失去它本来的流动性。

### 第11页
现代科学与政治把命名扩展到了整个社会结构。我们给元素、病症、思想体系、运动、组织命名，让它们进入可被讨论、可被管理的领域。

同时，命名也带来了权力：谁来命名，往往决定了谁在叙事中占据主体位置。

### 第12页
但是，不论命名体系怎样增厚，仍有一个更深的悬念存在：无名的那一面。

如果“道”是万物背后的生成之源，那么它并不等于任何一个名字所指的对象。它是那种在命名之前、在秩序之外流动的“无名”。

### 第13页
这不是否认名字的价值，而是提醒我们：名字本身有界限。它们是理解世界的工具，而非最终的世界。

当我们把“名”当作终极解释，就会把“道”关在一个狭小的语义笼子里，错过了它作为先于命名的存在。

### 第14页
在语言的边界之外，存在着一种无法被具体形象化的状态。旧约中的上帝、古代神话中的至高神，乃至《道德经》中的无名之道，都是这种超越形态的投影。

这种存在不是可被直接命名的对象，而是一个可供我们通过否定性语言接近的本质：它更像是一片“未说的场域”。

### 第15页
因此，我们对名字的态度应当更为谦卑。命名让我们走近世界，但它同时也告诉我们：世界不仅仅是那些被我们说出来的事物。

在这两者之间，真正的智慧不是抛弃名字，而是看清名字与无名的关系，既使用名字，又不被名字囚禁。

## 回扣

### 第16页
《创世记》的命名与《道德经》的无名并非完全对立，而是同一个问题的两面：当我们给事物命名，意味着我们开始把它纳入一个人造的秩序；但在命名之前，仍有一种更根本的存在不受任何名字限制。

这正是本章要说的：名是方法，而道是境界；名可名非常名，名只是通向道的门槛，而不是道本身。

### 第17页
对一个真正成熟的心智来说，名字应该是一种工具，而不是终极的真理。

我们可以尊重名字给予秩序的力量，同时也要保留对无名之道的敬畏。这样，名字才不会把我们锁进一个狭窄的世界，而道才会继续在不确定与开放中展开。

# Chapter 001: When the Demon’s Name Is Spoken, the Demon Dissolves.

## Original Text

### Page 1
The Dao that can be spoken of is not the eternal Dao; the name that can be named is not the eternal name.

The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth; the named is the mother of ten thousand things.

### Page 2
The LORD God formed out of the ground every beast of the field and every bird of the air, and brought them to the man to see what he would call them. Whatever the man called each living creature, that was its name. The man gave names to all cattle, to the birds of the air, and to every beast of the field.

Genesis 2:19-20


— Dao De Jing, Chapter 1

## Interpretation

### Page 3
A “name” is not a simple label. It is a way to define the form of something and let the world enter order. The naming story in Genesis shows that when humanity names the world, events collapse from infinite possibility into a determinate shape.

The Dao is the opposite: it is the unnamed and unspeakable source. It is not any one thing, and it cannot be contained by a single name. It is the infinitely flowing field of the nameless behind everything.

### Page 4
In Egyptian myth, a true name (Ren) is the foundation of a deity’s power. Isis gains control of Ra by learning his true name — showing that once a name is captured, it grants dominion to the possessor.

The Japanese belief in kotodama emphasizes this further: language itself carries spiritual power, and if a true name is revealed, the freedom of the thing becomes limited. For that reason, many older Japanese people keep a hidden true name and a public false name.

### Page 5
Naming in modern society is even more complex. Scientists name natural phenomena to classify them; artists use names to convey emotion; politicians use titles to establish authority. Yet no matter how detailed the web of names becomes, people still often imagine that a “final name” can grasp the essence of the universe.

That is precisely what contradicts the Dao De Jing’s saying: the named that can be named is not the eternal named. Treating Dao as something that can be fixed by words loses its essential quality of being nameless.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 6
- True Name (Ren): In Egyptian myth, the source of a god’s power; the name itself implies control over the deity.
- Kotodama: A Japanese belief in the spiritual power of words, where speech can produce reality and alter fate.
- Collapse: A quantum metaphor; observation causes a superposed state to become determinate, similar to how naming causes possibilities to converge.
- Nameless: In the Dao De Jing, the fundamental nature of Dao, which transcends language and names.

### Page 7
- Genesis 2:19-20: Adam names the creatures, taking on a form of sovereign order over the world.
- Dao De Jing Chapter 1: Pairs the named with the nameless, reminding us of the boundary between language and being.
- Isis and Ra: In ancient Egyptian myth, the story of gaining sovereignty through the true name, showing the link between names and power.
- Quote from Onmyoji: Emphasizes that a name can bind the essential shape of a thing.

## Main Text

### Page 8
When Adam named all living beings, he was not merely attaching labels. He was building a comprehensible order for the world: each name implies a distinction, a belonging, and a degree of control over that thing.

In this picture, a name becomes an entry to power. It gives chaos boundaries and causes countless potential forms to settle into a speakable existence.

### Page 9
In ancient Egypt, the true name becomes the key to sovereignty. After Isis learns Ra’s true name, she is no longer merely a worshiper but a possessor. The revelation of the name turns the sacred from hidden into governable.

This idea is not mere mythic rhetoric. It comes from an older worldview in which language itself participates in the making of reality.

### Page 10
In Japan, the belief in kotodama compresses this power into personal experience. Names are divided into a true name and a false name; the hidden true name preserves a person’s core, while the public false name functions as a symbol in social relations.

In that culture, the incomplete disclosure of a name becomes a way to protect freedom. If a true name is exposed, the thing loses its original fluidity.

### Page 11
Modern science and politics have extended naming into the structure of society. We name elements, diseases, systems of thought, movements, and organizations, bringing them into a sphere where they can be discussed and managed.

At the same time, naming confers power: whoever names often determines who occupies the central position in the narrative.

### Page 12
Yet no matter how dense the naming system becomes, a deeper question remains: the side of the nameless.

If Dao is the source behind all things, then it is not equivalent to any object that a name points to. It is the nameless that flows before naming and outside of order.

### Page 13
This is not to deny the value of names. It is to remind us that names have limits. They are tools for understanding the world, not the world itself.

If we take “name” as the ultimate explanation, we confine Dao within a narrow semantic cage and miss its existence prior to naming.

### Page 14
Beyond the boundaries of language lies a condition that cannot be concretely pictured. The God of the Old Testament, the supreme deities of ancient myth, and the nameless Dao of the Dao De Jing are all projections of this transcendent form.

Such an existence is not an object that can be directly named; it is a reality we approach through negative language. It is more like an “unsaid field.”

### Page 15
Therefore, our attitude toward names should be humbler. Naming brings us closer to the world, but it also tells us that the world is not only what we can say.

Between the two, true wisdom is not to abandon names, but to see the relationship between names and the nameless — to use names without being imprisoned by them.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16
The naming in Genesis and the namelessness in the Dao De Jing are not completely opposed; they are two sides of the same issue. When we name things, we begin to place them within a human-made order. Yet before naming, there is a more fundamental existence that is not limited by any name.

That is what this chapter seeks to say: a name is a method, while Dao is a realm. The named that can be named is not the eternal named. A name is only a threshold to Dao, not Dao itself.

### Page 17
For a truly mature mind, a name should be a tool, not an ultimate truth.

We can respect the order names provide while also retaining reverence for the nameless Dao. In this way, names will not lock us into a narrow world, and Dao can continue unfolding in uncertainty and openness.
