# 第001-3章：轮匠放下凿子

## 典故引入

### 第1页

堂上有书，堂下有木。

齐桓公坐在高处读古人的话，轮匠轮扁在下面斫车轮。故事一开始就把两种权威放进同一个房间：一种有君位和经典的光，另一种沾着木屑、汗和多年失败后的分寸。

### 第2页

这个故事出自《庄子》。

它不是为了羞辱读书人，而是要把一个尖锐问题推到面前：文字能把经验送到多远？当古人的生命已经消失，书页留下的是道路本身，还是道路被走过之后的痕迹？

### 第3页

齐桓公读的是古人的书，轮扁做的是眼前的轮。

一个面对已经留下的文字，一个面对还没有成形的木头。故事的张力正在这里：被保存下来的智慧，和正在发生的判断，究竟能不能彼此替代？

### 第4页

书页沙沙，凿声笃笃。

一个人在读圣人的话，一个人在听木头的纹理。书页保存的是前人的声音，凿子面对的是眼前的材料；两者都需要敬意，但敬意的方向不同。

### 第5页

轮扁忽然停手，抬头问：“君上读的是什么？”

齐桓公说：“圣人的话。”这不是随便一句问话，而是把读书人最深的自信放到光里：我们读到的，是圣人本身，还是圣人留下的痕迹？

### 第6页

轮扁又问：“那些圣人还在吗？”

齐桓公说：“早已不在了。”

### 第7页

轮扁说：“那么君上所读的，恐怕只是古人留下的迹。”

堂上一冷。齐桓公命他说明白，否则这句话就太放肆了。轮扁的危险在于，他不是用另一套理论挑战经典，而是用一个工匠的身体经验，挑战文字的自满。

## 正文

### 第8页

轮扁举起手里的车轮。

他说：“我斫轮时，手慢一点，木就松；手急一点，木就死。”这里没有玄妙词汇，只有材料的反应：松了，轮不稳；紧了，木会裂。道理不是飘在天上，而是在手下一次次失败里变得清楚。

### 第9页

“不松不紧的那一点，在手里，也在心里。”

“嘴可以说出大概，却说不出那一瞬间。”那一瞬不是反知识，而是知识进入身体之后的判断：眼看、手试、心定，三者同时抵达，才有真正的分寸。

### 第10页

他说自己不能完全传给儿子。

儿子也不能完全从他这里接走。所以他到了老年，仍要亲手斫轮。不是父亲吝啬，也不是儿子愚钝；有些东西可以教到门口，却必须由学习者自己在门内跌倒、调整、再站稳。

### 第11页

这个故事不是叫人轻视书。

没有书，古人的门也许早已关上；没有文字，许多声音早就散在风里。轮扁真正反对的不是书，而是把书当成已经完成的生命，把痕迹当成奔跑本身。

### 第12页

可是书不是屋子，门不是屋子，路标不是道路。

它们能带人靠近，却不能替人抵达。若读者停在门口赞美门的雕刻，却不肯进入屋内，那么最美的门也会变成障碍。

### 第13页

轮匠量木、看弧、试松紧，这是“有欲”。

他必须看见边界、材料、工具和手上的分寸。没有这种有目的的观看，手艺只会变成空谈；没有对“徼”的把握，所谓“妙”也会漂浮得没有落点。

### 第14页

轮匠放下成见，等手、心和木纹在同一刻相遇，这是“无欲”。

那一点“妙”，不是不要技术，而是技术熟到不再僵硬。无欲不是没有意志，而是不让意志抢在材料之前；它让木头、手和心都有说话的余地。

### 第15页

齐桓公没有再急着翻页。

书还在，凿子也还在。屋里只剩木头被慢慢听懂的声音。那一刻，读书不再是占有古人的话，而是学习如何让话重新进入当下的生命。

## 回扣

### 第16页

第一章让每一句话记得自己的边界。

能说出的道，是门、是迹、是车轮压过的浅痕；真正的道，还要在人亲自看、亲自做、亲自安静时发生。语言把我们带到门前，经验让我们知道门后有风。

### 第17页

读书的人合上书，轮匠放下凿子。

车轮向前滚去，留下的痕迹很浅；真正的路，在痕迹之外。痕迹值得珍惜，因为它说明有人走过；也必须越过，因为活着的人不能只住在别人的痕迹里。

# Chapter 001-3: The Wheelwright Sets Down the Chisel

## Allusion

### Page 1

There were books above, and wood below.

Duke Huan of Qi sat in the hall reading the words of the ancients, while a wheelwright named Pian shaped a wheel beneath him. From the first moment, the story places two kinds of authority in one room: the brightness of rule and canon, and the measure earned through wood dust, sweat, and years of failure.

### Page 2

The story comes from the Zhuangzi.

It is not told to humiliate readers. It brings a sharper question forward: how far can writing carry experience? When the life of the ancients is gone, does the page preserve the road itself, or only the trace left after someone passed?

### Page 3

Duke Huan read an ancient book. The wheelwright worked on a wheel still taking shape.

One faced words already left behind; the other faced wood not yet finished. The tension of the story begins here: can preserved wisdom and present judgment ever take each other’s place?

### Page 4

Pages whispered. The chisel answered.

One person read the words of sages. One listened to the grain of wood. The page preserved voices from before; the chisel faced the material at hand. Both deserved respect, but not the same kind.

### Page 5

The wheelwright stopped and looked up.

“What is my lord reading?” he asked. “The words of sages,” said the duke. The question was not casual. It brought into the open a reader’s deepest confidence: are we meeting the sages themselves, or only the traces they left?

### Page 6

“Are those sages still alive?” asked the wheelwright.

“Long gone,” said the duke.

### Page 7

“Then what my lord reads,” said the wheelwright, “may be only the traces they left behind.”

The hall grew cold. Duke Huan demanded an explanation. The danger of the wheelwright’s answer was that he did not oppose books with another theory. He opposed the self-satisfaction of words with bodily knowledge.

## Main Text

### Page 8

The wheelwright lifted the rim in his hands.

“When I shape a wheel, too soft a hand makes it loose; too hard a hand kills the wood.” There are no mystical terms here, only the response of material: loose, the wheel fails; forced, the wood splits. Insight becomes clear through repeated failure under the hand.

### Page 9

“The point between loose and tight lives in the hand and in the heart.”

“The mouth can speak around it, but not become that instant.” This is not anti-knowledge. It is knowledge ripened into embodied judgment: eye, hand, and heart arriving together at measure.

### Page 10

He said he could not fully pass it to his son.

His son could not fully take it from him. So even in old age, he still shaped wheels with his own hands. The father was not stingy, and the son was not dull. Some things can be taught only to the doorway; inside the door, the learner must stumble, adjust, and stand.

### Page 11

The story does not ask us to despise books.

Without books, many ancient doors would have closed; without writing, many voices would have scattered into wind. What the wheelwright resists is not the book, but the belief that a book has already completed the life it points toward.

### Page 12

But a book is not the room. A door is not the room. A sign is not the road.

They can bring us near, but they cannot arrive for us. If a reader remains outside praising the carving on the door, even the most beautiful door becomes an obstacle.

### Page 13

The wheelwright measures wood, reads the curve, and tests loose and tight. This is desire.

He must see edges, material, tools, and the exact measure in his hand. Without this purposeful seeing, craft becomes empty talk. Without the outer fringe, mystery has no place to land.

### Page 14

The wheelwright releases his fixed idea and waits until hand, heart, and grain meet in one moment. This is desirelessness.

That subtle point is not the absence of skill. It is skill ripened beyond stiffness. Desirelessness is not a lack of will; it is the refusal to let will speak before the material has spoken.

### Page 15

Duke Huan did not hurry to turn the page again.

The book remained; the chisel remained. The room held only the sound of wood being slowly understood. In that moment, reading was no longer possession of ancient words, but a training in letting words enter present life again.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Chapter One asks every sentence to remember its edge.

The Dao that can be spoken is a door, a trace, a shallow track made by a wheel. The real Dao still happens when a person sees, acts, and grows quiet. Language brings us to the threshold; experience tells us there is wind beyond it.

### Page 17

The reader closes the book. The wheelwright sets down the chisel.

The wheel rolls on, leaving a shallow track; the real road lies beyond the trace. The trace is worth honoring, because someone has passed this way. It must also be passed beyond, because the living cannot dwell only in another person’s track.
