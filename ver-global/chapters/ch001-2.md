# 第001-2章：帛书里的恒道

## 考据引入

### 第1页

1973 年，湖南长沙东郊，考古人员打开马王堆三号汉墓。

墓中没有宏大的宣告，只有封闭在湿暗里的漆器、帛画、兵书、医方，以及写在丝上的文字。两千多年的沉睡之后，一部早已被读熟的经典，忽然有了更早、更陌生的面孔。

### 第2页

出土帛书中有《老子》甲、乙两种抄本。

它们写在柔软而脆弱的丝帛上，早于许多后世定本。它们的意义不在于替代我们手中的书，而在于让读者看见：经典不是天上掉下的一块石头，而是一条经过抄写、传诵、避讳和整理的河。

### 第3页

这些帛书不是“唯一原版”，也不能简单说成“最正确的老子”。

它们更像时间深处打开的一扇门，让我们看见《老子》在定型以前曾有过的早期面貌。考据的意义不在于制造一个新的偶像，而在于让旧偶像松动，让我们重新学会谨慎。

### 第4页

我们熟悉的开篇，在帛书里带着另一种字形与节奏出现。

同一句话，一旦进入历史的光里，就不再只是抽象的格言。思想进入文字之后，也属于抄写者的手、时代的禁忌、材料的脆弱和传承中的选择。

### 第5页

传世本第一章说：“道可道，非常道。”

帛书相应处常见更舒缓的句式：“道，可道也，非恒道也。”多出来的停顿和语气，像是把一句被后世读熟的格言，还原成一个人正在思考、正在开口的声音。

### 第6页

传世本说“常”，帛书多作“恒”。

这不是一个字把另一个字推翻，而是两个字一起指向同一个问题：什么东西经久不尽，不能被一时的言说耗尽？如果读者只为“到底哪个字才对”而兴奋，就仍然停在名相之争；第一章要我们问的，是那个名字试图指向、却无法穷尽的深处。

### 第7页

帛书还常多“也”这样的语气。

一句古老的话，忽然不再像碑上的铁句，而像有人在很久以前缓缓开口。我们听见的不只是内容，也听见文本的呼吸：思想在变成经典以前，曾经也有语调、迟疑和现场。

### 第8页

更奇妙的是，马王堆帛书多见“德经”在前，“道经”在后。

我们熟悉的书，也曾经有别的排列方式。章序的差异让人惊讶，却并不必然摧毁意义；它让我们知道，经典的权威不是来自版式永远不变，而是来自它在不同形态中仍然能够发问。

## 正文

### 第9页

想象一间安静的整理室。

灯光落在丝帛上，工作人员戴着手套，连呼吸都放轻，因为字和丝都太脆弱。这里没有神秘的仪式，只有极慢的动作、极小的工具，以及对时间的敬畏。

### 第10页

那些字没有像石刻一样硬。

它们会折，会裂，会在时间里发黄，也会把一个熟悉的开篇变得陌生。陌生感不是损失；它使读者从背诵中醒来，重新面对一句话原本的锋利和不安。

### 第11页

读者原本以为自己已经知道第一章。

可是“常”旁边出现了“恒”，“道经”之前出现了“德经”，熟悉的门忽然多出一条侧廊。那条侧廊不一定通向另一个结论，却会改变我们站在门口的姿势：少一点占有，多一点倾听。

### 第12页

这时，第一章像是在解释自己。

凡是被我们固定成唯一形态的文本，都还不是那部在历史中活过来的文本。所谓“非常道”，不只适用于宇宙和人生，也适用于我们读到的文字：一旦被封成绝对，它就离活的传统远了。

### 第13页

版本差异不是经典的裂缝。

它更像树的年轮，让我们看见一部书怎样经过手抄、避讳、传承、校订，仍然继续生长。年轮不是树的伤口，而是时间在树身上的记录；异文也不是必然的错误，而是传统曾经呼吸过的证据。

### 第14页

如果只想抓住“唯一正确的字”，我们会把经典变成标本。

如果承认文字有来路，我们反而更接近它的生命。真正严肃的阅读不是放弃判断，而是知道判断必须带着证据、限度和谦卑；它不把不确定当成敌人。

### 第15页

考据让人谦卑。

它告诉我们：手中的版本是渡口，不是整条河；眼前的一句，是入口，不是全部的道。渡口很重要，没有渡口就难以下水；但若把渡口当成河本身，就再也不会真正渡过去。

## 回扣

### 第16页

“名可名，非常名”在这里变得更深。

连“常”与“恒”这样的名字，也只是从不同方向指向那个不能被名字耗尽的深处。考据不是把玄妙消灭，而是让玄妙不再漂浮；它给深处一条可查的岸线，又承认岸线之外还有水。

### 第17页

帛书合上，门没有关。

它把我们带回第一章：说得出的，是入口；说不尽的，才是道。一个成熟的读者，会珍惜入口，也不会跪在入口处不再前行。

# Chapter 001-2: The Enduring Dao in Silk

## Textual-Historical Opening

### Page 1

In 1973, archaeologists opened Tomb Three at Mawangdui, in the eastern suburbs of Changsha, Hunan.

Inside were no grand proclamations, but lacquerware, silk paintings, military texts, medical writings, and words written on silk. After more than two thousand years underground, a classic that many readers thought they knew returned with an earlier, stranger face.

### Page 2

Among the finds were two manuscript copies of the Laozi.

They were written on soft, fragile silk, and they are earlier than many received versions of the text. Their importance is not that they replace the book in our hands, but that they show a classic is not a stone dropped from heaven. It is a river passing through copying, recitation, taboo, editing, and care.

### Page 3

These manuscripts are not “the only original,” nor simply “the correct Laozi.”

They are better understood as a door opened in deep time, showing an earlier face of the text before later forms became familiar. The work of textual study is not to build a new idol, but to loosen the old one, so that reverence can become more careful.

### Page 4

The opening many readers know appears in the silk manuscripts with another written shape and rhythm.

Under historical light, the sentence is no longer only an abstract saying. Once thought enters writing, it belongs not only to an abstract heaven, but also to scribal hands, political taboos, fragile materials, and the choices of transmission.

### Page 5

The received text begins: “Dao ke dao, fei chang dao.”

The silk manuscripts often preserve a slower rhythm: “Dao, if it can be spoken as dao, is not the enduring Dao.” The added pauses make a line long memorized as doctrine feel again like a thought being formed in a human mouth.

### Page 6

The received text says chang, “constant.” The silk often has heng, “enduring.”

One word does not simply defeat the other. Together they ask: what cannot be used up by a moment of speech? If we care only about which word wins, we remain trapped in the contest of names; Chapter One asks us to look toward what the names cannot exhaust.

### Page 7

The silk text also often carries particles like ye.

An ancient sentence suddenly feels less like an iron inscription, and more like a voice speaking slowly from long ago. We hear not only meaning, but breath: before a thought becomes a classic, it has tone, hesitation, and occasion.

### Page 8

Even more striking, the Mawangdui manuscripts often place the De chapters before the Dao chapters.

The book we know once had another order. This need not destroy meaning; it changes the posture of reading. Authority does not lie in a layout that never moved, but in a text still able to question us across its changing forms.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Imagine a quiet conservation room.

Light falls on silk. Gloved hands move slowly. Even breathing seems careful, because both silk and words are fragile. There is no theatrical mystery here, only small tools, slow gestures, and a disciplined reverence for time.

### Page 10

The characters are not hard like stone.

They can fold, split, yellow with time, and make a familiar opening unfamiliar again. That unfamiliarity is not a loss. It wakes the reader from recitation and returns the old sentence to its edge.

### Page 11

The reader thought Chapter One was already known.

Then chang stands beside heng, De stands before Dao, and a familiar door gains a side passage. That passage may not lead to a different conclusion, but it changes how we stand before the door: with less possession, and more listening.

### Page 12

At that moment, Chapter One seems to explain itself.

Any text fixed by us into one final shape is not yet the text that has lived through history. “Not the enduring Dao” applies not only to cosmos and life, but also to our own reading: when a living tradition is sealed as absolute, it begins to lose its life.

### Page 13

Variant readings are not cracks in a classic.

They are more like tree rings, showing how a book has grown through copying, taboo, transmission, and editing. Tree rings are not wounds in the tree; they are time recorded in the body. Variants can be records of a tradition breathing.

### Page 14

If we hunt only for the one final word, we turn the classic into a specimen.

If we admit that words have histories, we come closer to their life. Serious reading does not abandon judgment; it learns that judgment must travel with evidence, limits, and humility.

### Page 15

Philology teaches humility.

The version in our hands is a ferry landing, not the whole river; the sentence before us is an entrance, not the whole Dao. A landing matters: without it, we may never reach the water. But if we mistake the landing for the river, we never cross.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

“The name that can be named is not the enduring name” becomes deeper here.

Even names like chang and heng point, from different directions, toward what names cannot exhaust. Textual study does not destroy mystery; it gives mystery a shoreline that can be walked, while admitting that water continues beyond the shore.

### Page 17

The silk is folded away, but the door does not close.

It returns us to Chapter One: what can be spoken is an entrance; what cannot be exhausted is Dao. A mature reader cherishes the entrance, but does not kneel there forever.
