# 第001-1章：地图与未命名海岸

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名，天地之始；有名，万物之母。

### 第2页

故常无欲，以观其妙；常有欲，以观其徼。

此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄。玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第3页

老子开篇不是要我们停止言说。

他提醒我们：凡是能被一句话说尽、被一种理论占有、被一个体系封口的“道”，都已经不是那个恒久、活泼、不可穷尽的道。语言不是敌人，但语言一旦自以为完成了真实，就会变成最精致的遮蔽物。

### 第4页

名字很有用。它像路标、门牌、星图上的连线，让人能够分辨、寻找、记忆和彼此相认。

但名字也有危险。名字会带来边界，边界会带来秩序，也会带来占有、排除和误判。一旦我们把标签当作全部，活的真实就被遮住了；我们看见的不再是事物，而是自己已经贴上去的解释。

### 第5页

“无名”不是一片空白，而是万物还没有形状、分类和归属之前的源头。

“有名”也不是错误，而是万物显形之后，可以被照料、被呼唤、被共同生活的世界。老子在这里没有把“无”和“有”分成高低贵贱；他让我们看见：生成的深处和显现的世界，本来互相依存。

### 第6页

“无欲”是暂时放下占有和急于定论的心，以观看深处的“妙”。

“有欲”是带着目的去看见边界、道路、用途和抵达的方法，也就是“徼”。一个成熟的观看者，不是永远没有目的，也不是永远被目的牵着走；他知道什么时候让心空下来，什么时候让眼睛沿着边界工作。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**道**：本义是路，也可以指方法、原则、运行方式。第一章里的“道”，不能只缩小成普通规则。

它更像万物生成和运行的根本方式：可以趋近，可以体会，却不能被一句定义封住。王弼解释这一章时说，可说可名的东西已经落在“指事造形”之中；换句话说，一旦被说出，它已经成为某个角度、某个形状，而不是那个未被限定的常道本身。

### 第8页

**名**：名字、称谓、分类和概念。名让世界可交流，也让世界被切分。

好的名字像灯，照见一处；坏的执着像墙，把灯外的真实挡住。第一章不是要熄灯，而是要我们记得：灯光之外还有夜色，夜色不是错误，它保留着事物尚未被我们耗尽的深度。

## 正文

### 第9页

一个旅行者来到一片尚未画完的海岸。

向导把一张空白地图铺在木桌上，请他先不要急着把所有地方都写满。旅行者受过良好训练，习惯把未知变成名称，把名称变成线路，把线路变成可管理的世界；留白让他不安。

### 第10页

旅行者写下岬角、礁石、港湾、市集小路。

地图立刻变得可用。陌生人找到水井，船夫找到避风处，孩子找到回家的路。名字开始发挥慈悲的一面：它让分散的经验可以共享，让后来者不必从零开始摸索。

### 第11页

到了黄昏，潮水上涨。

白天露出的礁石沉入海里，一条从未画过的水路在月光下打开。白天准确的线，到了夜里并没有变成谎言；它只是显出自己的时限。世界不是背叛了地图，而是从来就比地图更会变化。

### 第12页

旅行者拿起笔，急着补上新的名字。

向导说：“先看。不要一看见门，就急着把门改成自己的标签。”有些时刻，命名太快不是理解，而是防御；人用一个词挡住惊讶，好让自己仍然像是掌握了一切。

### 第13页

旅行者停住了。

他看见潮线在移动，看见船影在水里变形，看见地图的边缘比地图本身更像一扇门。他第一次意识到，边界并不只是用来封闭的；边界也可能是邀请，邀请人走到知识的尽头，再保持观看。

### 第14页

他终于明白：地图不是错的。

错的是以为地图可以替海岸呼吸，可以替潮水改变，可以替黑暗里尚未显形的路作证。地图是人类智慧的一种美德，却也最容易变成傲慢：它把曾经走过的路保存下来，却不能保证未来只照着旧路展开。

### 第15页

他把地图的一角留白。

那块空白不是失败，而是诚实：它承认世界还在生长，承认名字之外仍有深处。留白不是放弃知识，而是给知识留出呼吸；不是反对命名，而是让命名记得自己只是临时的桥。

## 回扣

### 第16页

回到第一章，老子不是叫我们丢掉名字。

他是让名字保持谦卑：名字可以指向道，却不能成为道；地图可以带路，却不能成为大地。无名让我们接近生成的深处，有名让我们照料显现的世界；两者同出一处，彼此不是敌人。

### 第17页

旅行者带着名字继续行走，也带着沉默继续聆听。

他不再把沉默看成知识的空缺，而把它看成知识的边界。更深的门，在没有写满的地方打开。

# Chapter 001-1: The Map and the Unnamed Coast

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Tao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao.

The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name.

### Page 2

(Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth; (conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

Always without desire we must be found, if its deep mystery we would sound; but if desire always within us be, its outer fringe is all that we shall see.

Under these two aspects, it is really the same. Together we call them the Mystery. Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Laozi does not begin by asking us to stop speaking.

He reminds us that any Way fully captured by a sentence, possessed by a theory, or sealed inside a system is no longer the enduring, living, inexhaustible Way. Language is not the enemy. But when language believes it has completed reality, it becomes one of the most elegant forms of concealment.

### Page 4

Names are useful. They work like signposts, doorplates, and lines between stars.

They help us distinguish, find, remember, and recognize one another. Yet every name draws a boundary, and every boundary can become a form of possession, exclusion, or error. If we mistake the label for the whole, we no longer meet the thing itself; we meet our own interpretation.

### Page 5

But names can also become hard.

They are tools that can quietly become prisons. A name begins as a lamp, then becomes a wall when we forget how much remains outside its light.

### Page 6

“Nameless” is not blank nothingness. It is the source before things take shape.

“Named” is the world after things become visible, where they can be called, tended, remembered, and shared. Laozi does not rank these two as pure and fallen. He shows them as two ways one reality becomes available to us: depth before distinction, and care after distinction.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**Dao**: A way, road, method, or mode of unfolding. In Chapter One, it cannot be reduced to an ordinary rule.

It is the fundamental way by which things arise and move. Once it is spoken, it has already entered a certain angle, use, and shape. That spoken form may be helpful, but it is no longer the unbounded source itself.

### Page 8

**name**: A name, label, category, or concept.

A good name is a lamp: it lights one place. But clinging to a name can turn the lamp into a wall. Chapter One does not ask us to put out the lamp. It asks us to remember the night around it.

## Main Text

### Page 9

A traveler arrived at a coast no map had finished.

The guide laid a blank map on a wooden table and asked him not to fill every corner too quickly. The traveler was well trained: he was used to turning the unknown into names, names into routes, and routes into a manageable world. The blankness unsettled him.

### Page 10

The traveler named the headland, the reef, the harbor, and the road to the market.

The map became useful. Strangers found the well; sailors found shelter; children found the way home. Here names showed their mercy: they made experience shareable, so that those who came later did not have to begin from nothing.

### Page 11

At dusk, the tide rose.

The reef sank under water, and a moonlit passage opened where no line had been drawn. The line that had been accurate by day did not become false by night; it simply revealed its limit. The world had not betrayed the map. It had always been more changeable than the map.

### Page 12

The traveler lifted his pen, eager to add another name.

The guide said, “Look first. Do not turn every door into a label of your own.” Sometimes naming too quickly is not understanding but defense. A word can be used to block wonder, so that the mind may continue pretending it has mastered what stands before it.

### Page 13

The traveler stopped.

He saw the tide line moving, the boat shadows changing shape, and the margin of the map becoming more like a doorway than an edge. He realized that a boundary does not only close. It can also invite us to stand at the end of what we know and keep looking.

### Page 14

He understood at last: the map was not wrong.

What was wrong was believing a map could breathe for the coast, change for the tide, or testify for paths still hidden in the dark. A map is a human virtue, but also a temptation: it preserves where we have been, then tempts us to believe the future must obey the old line.

### Page 15

He left one corner of the map blank.

The blank was not failure, but honesty: the world was still growing, and depth remained beyond names. The blank did not reject knowledge. It gave knowledge room to breathe.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Chapter One does not ask us to throw names away.

It asks names to stay humble: a name can point toward Dao, but it cannot become Dao. The nameless lets us approach the depth from which things arise; the named lets us care for the visible world. They are not enemies.

### Page 17

The traveler walked on with names, and listened on with silence.

He no longer treated silence as a failure of knowledge, but as the edge where knowledge becomes honest. The deeper door opened where the page was not filled.
