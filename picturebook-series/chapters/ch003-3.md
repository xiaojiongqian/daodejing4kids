# 第003-3章：金手指和放下的权杖

## 典故引入

### 第1页

希腊神话里有位迈达斯王，已经有许多财宝，却还想要更多。他许愿：碰到什么，什么就变成黄金。

愿望实现时，他高兴极了。石头、树枝、桌子，都在他手下变得金光闪闪。

### 第2页

可是他拿起面包，面包变成金子；端起水，水也变成金子。传说里，连他最爱的孩子跑来拥抱他，也变成金色雕像。

他这才发现：最想要的东西，可能会把最需要的东西拿走。

### 第3页

另一个故事来自古罗马。辛辛纳图斯本来在田里耕作，国家有急难，人们请他出来掌权救城。

他做完大事后，没有抱住权力不放，而是回到自己的农田。

### 第4页

很久以后，华盛顿也做过相似的事。他带兵打赢战争后交还军权，后来任期满了，也回到自己的庄园。

能拿起权力不稀奇，能在该放下时放下，才很难。

## 正文

### 第5页

阿禾听完迈达斯的故事，做了一个游戏。他把纸片剪成金币，贴在杯子、馒头和小椅子上。

一开始大家都笑。没过多久，小兔说：“金馒头不能吃呀。”

### 第6页

小鸟也说：“金杯子里没有水。”小石头抱着金椅子，却发现谁也坐不上去。

阿禾看着满屋金光，忽然觉得眼睛累。太亮的东西，也会让人看不见真正需要。

### 第7页

老人端来一碗热汤。没有金边，也没有花纹，只冒着白白的热气。

孩子们一口一口喝下去，肚子暖起来。金纸片在桌边静静卷起了角。

### 第8页

下午，他们又玩“村长椅”的游戏。谁坐上椅子，谁就可以发号施令。

小兔坐上去不肯下来，小石头也想坐，小鸟在旁边急得拍翅膀。

### 第9页

老人讲了辛辛纳图斯的故事。那个农夫拿起权力，是为了救人；事情一成，就回去扶犁。

阿禾摸摸椅背，觉得它忽然不那么香了。

### 第10页

他坐上去，只说了一句话：“我们先修水沟。”说完，他就把椅子让出来，去拿小铲子。

小兔眨眨眼，也从椅子上跳下来。大家跟着去挖泥、搬石头、引水。

### 第11页

水沟通了，菜地喝到了水。没有人再问谁当了多久村长。

阿禾发现，权杖如果只是拿来挥舞，会让人争。若把它当工具，用完放下，事情就清楚了。

### 第12页

傍晚，孩子们把金币纸片收进盒子，把村长椅搬回屋檐下。

桌上只剩热汤、馒头和清水。它们不闪亮，却让大家安安稳稳。

## 回扣

### 第13页

第三章说“不见可欲，使民心不乱”。迈达斯的金手指告诉阿禾：欲望太亮，会把面包、水和拥抱都变远。

所以不是不能喜欢美好，而是不要让“想要”坐到心的王位上。

### 第14页

第三章也说“为无为”。辛辛纳图斯和华盛顿让阿禾看见：有时最好的做，是做完就退一步。

不把权力和功劳抓在手里，事情反而能安稳地留给大家。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：金子不能替我喝水，椅子不能替我修沟。

我愿意让心空一点，肚子暖一点，骨头有力一点。这样，手里没有金光，也能把今天过好。

# Chapter 003-3: The Golden Touch and the Laid-Down Staff

## Allusion

### Page 1

In Greek myth, King Midas already had many treasures, but wanted more. He wished that everything he touched would turn to gold.

When the wish came true, he was delighted. Stones, branches, and tables shone under his hand.

### Page 2

But when he picked up bread, it became gold; when he lifted water, it became gold too. In the story, even the child he loved became a golden statue when running to embrace him.

Then he learned that what we most want may take away what we most need.

### Page 3

Another story comes from ancient Rome. Cincinnatus was farming when the city faced danger, and the people asked him to take power and save it.

When the work was done, he did not cling to power. He returned to his field.

### Page 4

Much later, George Washington did something similar. After winning the war, he returned military power; after his terms as president, he went back to his estate.

To take up power is not so rare. To lay it down at the right time is harder.

## Main Text

### Page 5

After hearing the Midas story, Ahe made a game. He cut paper into coins and pasted them on cups, buns, and little chairs.

At first everyone laughed. Soon Little Rabbit said, "A golden bun cannot be eaten."

### Page 6

Little Bird said, "A golden cup has no water." Little Stone hugged a golden chair, then found no one could sit on it.

Ahe looked at the shining room and felt his eyes grow tired. Things that glitter too much can hide what we truly need.

### Page 7

The old man brought a bowl of hot soup. It had no golden edge and no pattern, only white steam rising.

The children drank it slowly, and their bellies warmed. The paper coins curled quietly at the edge of the table.

### Page 8

In the afternoon, they played "village-chief chair." Whoever sat on the chair could give orders.

Little Rabbit sat and would not get down. Little Stone wanted a turn, and Little Bird flapped in worry.

### Page 9

The old man told the story of Cincinnatus. The farmer took power to save people; once the matter was finished, he went back to the plow.

Ahe touched the chair. Suddenly it did not seem so delicious.

### Page 10

He sat down and said only one thing: "Let us mend the water ditch first." Then he left the chair and picked up a small shovel.

Little Rabbit blinked, then jumped down too. Everyone went to dig mud, move stones, and guide the water.

### Page 11

The ditch opened, and the vegetable beds drank. No one asked who had been chief the longest.

Ahe saw that if a staff is only waved, people fight. If it is used as a tool and then put down, the work becomes clear.

### Page 12

At dusk, the children put the paper coins in a box and carried the chief's chair back under the eaves.

On the table were only soup, buns, and clear water. They did not shine, but they made everyone steady.

## Return to the Original

### Page 13

Chapter Three says, "not to show them what is likely to excite their desires." Midas's golden touch shows Ahe that desire can shine so brightly that bread, water, and embraces move away.

This does not mean we cannot love good things. It means wanting should not sit on the throne of the heart.

### Page 14

Chapter Three also teaches action without forcing. Cincinnatus and Washington show Ahe that sometimes the best doing is to step back when the work is done.

When power and credit are not clutched, the result can remain steady for everyone.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: Gold cannot drink water for me, and a chair cannot mend a ditch for me.

I will keep my heart a little emptier, my belly warmer, and my bones stronger. Then even without glitter in my hands, I can live this day well.
