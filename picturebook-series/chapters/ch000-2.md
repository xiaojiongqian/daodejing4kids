# 第000-2章：一本书走了两千五百年

## 第1页

第二天一早，阿禾就坐在村口大树下等着了。

他翻开本子，把昨天写的字又看了一遍：*老子——爱看水的老人。耳朵很大（可能）。骑青牛往西走。不逃，只是走。*

"老师！"老人刚出现，阿禾就跑过去，"你昨天说，书上的那些字——老子的那些话——走了两千五百年才走到我们这里。它们是怎么走的？"

老人坐下来，把拐杖靠在树干上。

"这个问题问得好。那些字走的路，比老子当年骑青牛走的路还要长——也更有意思。"

小兔弟弟、小石头和小鸟陆陆续续来了，围坐在树下。晨光从树叶缝里漏下来，在地上画了很多小光圈。

"今天，我给你们讲一本书的旅程。"

## 第2页

"昨天讲到老子骑着青牛往西边走了。这是'传说部分'的开始。"

老人从怀里掏出一个小布袋，倒出几粒豆子在手里。

"传说部分是后来的人一层一层加上去的光，不能当作历史，但很有意思。喜欢听吗？"

"喜——欢——"大家拖长了声音。

"好。传说老子骑青牛到了函谷关——这是周朝西边的一个重要关口，走出去就离开了周朝的地界。守关的官员叫尹喜——也有人叫他关尹子。"

"'关尹子'是名字吗？"

"'关'是他管的地方，'尹'是他的官职，'子'是尊敬——合起来就是'守关的尹先生'。"

## 第3页

老人放下一粒豆子。

"这个尹喜，不是一般的守门人。传说他懂得'望气'——看天上的云气，就能知道有没有特别的人要经过。"

"望气？"小鸟抬起头看天，"云气能看出什么？"

"古时候有些人相信，天空的云气颜色和形状，可以透露出将要发生的事情。这一天，尹喜往东边望去——"

老人停了一下，让大家想象。

"他看到东方有一片紫色的云气，正慢慢往西边飘过来。他心里一动：有圣人要过关了。"

"紫气东来！"阿禾喊了出来，"我听过这个词！"

"对，'紫气东来'后来成了一个成语，意思是'有好事要来了，有贵人要到了'。直到今天，过年的时候还有人贴这四个字。"

## 第4页

老人放下一粒豆子。

"果然，没过多久，一位白发白须的老人，骑着一头青牛缓缓走来。"

"真的是老子！"

"尹喜拦住了他。不是要为难他——而是要请他留下一些东西。"

"尹喜说：老人家，您就要出关去了，以后再也见不到您了。能不能把您的道理写下来？不用多，把最重要的写一写就行。"

"老子答应了没有？"小兔紧张地问。

老人笑了。"古书上说，老子本来不想写。他相信'知道的人不说，说的人不完全知道'——真正的道理不是嘴能讲清的。但现在不讲就要走了，人家又这么诚恳，那就讲一讲吧。"

"于是，他坐下来，写了一篇文字。"

"写了多久？"

"不知道。可能几天，也可能几个月。他写下来的字数——"

老人伸出五根手指。

## 第5页

"五千字？"小石头最近在学数数。

"对，大约五千个字——差不多是你们听过的几个故事加起来的长度。分成了八十一章。写完之后，老子就骑上青牛，出了函谷关，往西边去了。"

"后来呢？"

"没有人知道后来。"老人看着远山，"有人说他去了更西边的地方继续修行；有人说他去了印度，教化那里的人；还有人说他的事迹融进了天上的云。各种说法都有——但没有一样能证实。"

"他再也没有回来过？"

"没有。他只留下了那五千个字。就像一个人往湖里投了一颗石子，然后转身走了。涟漪一圈一圈荡开——这一荡，就是两千五百年。"

## 第6页

风吹过来，树叶沙沙响。大家好像都在想象那个画面：关门、黄昏、青牛远去、一堆写满字的竹简留在了守关人的手里。

"这才是开始。"老人把豆子推了推，"接下来才是书的旅程。"

"老子留下的竹简——这是第一份《道德经》。可是竹简这种东西，过几十年上百年，绳子会断，竹片会烂、会被虫蛀。古时候又没有印刷机——每多一本，都得靠人用手一个字一个字地抄。"

"那不是很容易抄错吗？"阿禾问。

"正是。抄的时候，有人看花了眼，有人多抄一笔，有人少抄一句。同一个字，这个抄本写'常'，那个抄本写'恒'——因为后来有个皇帝名字里有'恒'字，大家抄书时不敢用这个字，就改成了'常'。"

"因为一个人的名字，就把书里的字改了？"小兔觉得不可思议。

"那时候就是这样。皇帝的名字不能随便写。所以，这本书传着传着，就传出了不同的'样子'。"

## 第7页

"你看，"老人在地上画了两条线，像一条河的两岸，"《道德经》从老子手里写出来，到今天——就像一条河。河水流过不同的地方，带着不同的泥沙，颜色也不完全一样。但河的大致流向，从来没有变过。"

他在第一条线上做了个标记。

"最早的《道德经》是什么样的？很长一段时间，没有人确切知道。直到——"

他用力点了一下地面。"——大约五十年前，中国考古人员挖出了两样非常古老的东西。"

"挖出什么了？"小石头最爱听"挖土"的事。

"1973年，在湖南长沙的马王堆，人们从一座西汉古墓里挖出了写在丝绸上的《道德经》——叫'帛书'。已经埋在地下两千多年了。"

## 第8页

"丝绸上的字？"阿禾在本子上画了一小块方形的布。

"对。写在帛上的字，用的词跟后来流传的版本有很多不一样。比如现在很多版本说'道可道，非常道'，帛书上写的是'道可道也，非恒道也'——多了一个'也'字，'常'变成了'恒'。还有，帛书把'德经'放在前面，'道经'放在后面，顺序也是倒过来的。"

"那哪个才是真的？"小兔糊涂了。

老人缓缓摇头。"不是'哪个是真的，哪个是假的'——它们都是真的。它们是同一条河在不同年代的样子。河的上游和下游，水是不一样的，但水道是一样的。"

"还有更早的发现：1993年，在湖北郭店，人们从一座战国古墓里挖出了更老的竹简——比帛书还要早一百多年。但这些竹简不完整，只保留了《道德经》的一部分章节。"

"考古真神奇，"小石头说，"像从土里往外翻时间。"

## 第9页

老人又把一颗豆子往前推。

"在考古挖出帛书和竹简之前，人们读的《道德经》，是靠一代代人用手抄、后来用木板刻、再后来用机器印——这样传下来的。"

他伸出两根手指。"有两个版本最出名。"

"一个是河上公本。河上公是汉朝的一位隐士，住在河边的小屋里。据说汉文帝亲自去请教他——皇帝去了，他不出来迎接，只叫皇帝自己进屋。皇帝有点不高兴，说：'普天之下，莫非王土，你知不知道我是谁？'河上公一下子飞到半空中，说：'我现在上不挨天，下不着地，你说这地方还是你的吗？'皇帝赶紧恭敬行礼。"

"哇！他会飞！"小鸟瞪圆了眼睛。

"这大概也是传说。但河上公的注解本确确实实传下来了——他把每一章都解释得很仔细，字字句句都讲。"

## 第10页

"另一个更重要的版本，是王弼本。"

老人放下一颗稍大一点的豆子。

"王弼是三国时代的人——比诸葛亮稍晚一点。他非常非常聪明，十几岁就能跟大学者讨论最深奥的道理。"

"十几岁？比我们只大一点点？"阿禾眼睛亮了。

"对。可惜他只活了二十三岁就去世了。但他留下的《老子注》，成了后来一千多年全世界读《道德经》'标准本'的基础。"

"二十三岁就写出了最重要的注解？"

"是的。有些人活得长，贡献不一定多；有些人很短，却留下了很深的东西。王弼就是后者。"

老人顿了顿："你们现在听到的每章原文——中文的——主要是根据王弼本。只有少数地方参考了帛书来修正。"

## 第11页

阿禾在本子上画了一条河，河两岸画了很多小点——代表不同的版本。他好像慢慢明白了：同一条河，在不同年代、不同地方，看过去的样子不完全一样。

"老师，"他放下笔，"这本书这么老了。每个年代的人读它，都读到一样的东西吗？"

"好问题。"老人把地上一颗豆子转了转。"每个年代的人，读到的东西不完全一样。"

"打仗那年，有人从中读到了'不要靠武力逞强'。天下大乱那年，有人从中读到了'安静下来，先守住自己'。当官的人读到'不要贪得无厌'。读书人读到'最厉害的智慧往往是安静的'。"

"每个人都在里面找自己需要的那一句？"

"差不多。一本书，如果像钥匙一样只能开一把锁，它就只活那一个年代。一本书像光一样照进不同的困境，它就活两千五百年。"

## 第12页

小兔举手："那英文版呢？世界上其他地方的人读的是什么？"

"第十七、十八世纪，《道德经》被传教士带到了欧洲，开始被翻译成各种语言。到现在，它是全世界被翻译最多的书之一——仅次于《圣经》。"

"英文最早的翻译，是一个叫 James Legge 的英国人——他的中文名叫理雅各——在 1891 年完成的。他一字一字地翻，尽量忠实于原来中文的意思。我们这本书里英文的原文部分，用的就是他的翻译。"

阿禾在本子上写：*Legge = 理雅各，1891*。

"不过，英文翻译《道德经》特别难。比如'道'这个字——翻成 Way，漏掉了它里面的生命感；翻成 Path，又轻了一点。最后大多数翻译索性不翻了，直接用拼音——Dao。让读者自己去感受它是什么。"

"就像'象'——"阿禾慢慢说，"叫它'粗柱子怪'不对，叫'大象'才对——但'大象'也只是个名字。"

"完全对。有些东西，任何语言里的任何一个词都装不下。"

## 第13页

老人把豆子都收了起来。

"现在你们知道这本书的来历了。一个爱看水的老人，在一个很乱的年代，安安静静地活了很久、看了很多。他不争、不急、不强。最后，他骑一头青牛往西边走了。走之前，他把五千个字留给了守关的年轻人。那个年轻人抄了一份又一份，后来的人又抄，刻，印——一代传一代，一直传到今天，传到我口里，传进你们耳朵里。"

他站起来，手杖轻轻点着地面。

"那我们现在该做什么？"

"该读它。"阿禾说。

"对。但读它不是为了把它背下来，不是为了记住'正确答案'。"

"那是为了什么？"小石头问。

"为了像老子看水那样看世界。看事情不只看一面，做事情不急着抢第一，心里不乱、不赌气、不急着骂人——慢慢观察，悄悄地想，轻轻地做。做成了呢——"他看着阿禾。

"做成了也不一定站到最中间去。"阿禾接上。

老人点点头。

## 第14页

他们走在田埂上，夕阳把几个人的影子排成一排。最前面是小石头的圆脑袋影子，接着是小兔的长耳朵影子，中间是小鸟飞在影子之上的影子，最后是老人弯曲的背影和手杖的长影子。阿禾走在老人旁边，手里捧着本子。

"明天，"老人说，"我们正式开始第一章。"

"就是大象那一章！"小兔说。

"对，'道可道，非常道'。我们看见的每一片都是真的，但没有一片是全部。所以要一直好奇，一直往前走。"

他停了一下，手杖点在泥土小路上。

"阿禾，"他侧过头，"你知道你自己像什么吗？"

"像什么？"

"像关尹。你也在请老人把话说出来。你的'名字本'，就是关尹当年铺在老子面前的那卷竹简。"

阿禾低头看了看自己写满了字的旧本子——封面已经有点破了，纸边卷起来，有些页被水打湿过，有些页上面画了好多圈和叉，有些页还贴着被风吹过的纸条。他忽然很想一直写下去。

## 第15页

晚上，阿禾一个人坐在窗前。月光照在本子的封面上。

他翻开最后一页——那是他特地为今天留出来的空白纸。他把今天听到的事写了下来：

*1. 老子骑青牛到了函谷关。一个叫尹喜的守关人请他把道理写下来。*
*2. 老子写了五千个字。然后走了。没有人知道他去了哪里。*
*3. 这些字被人抄了又抄——写在竹子上、写在丝绸上、刻在木板上、印在纸上。*
*4. 不同年代的抄本有一点点不一样——像同一条河在不同的地方看起来颜色不同。但河还是那条河。*
*5. 王弼二十三岁就写出了最重要的注解。*
*6. 现在全世界都在读这本书。用很多种语言。*
*7. 老师让我像关尹——请老人把话说出来，再写下来。*

他放下笔，又拿起笔，在下面加了一行：

*也许，老子不是那个"把道理讲完"的人。老子是那个"把门推开"的人。推开之后，他自己先走了。门开着。我们——每一个捧着这本书的人——都是后来走进门的人。*

风从窗外吹过来，翻动本子里的纸页。月光下，阿禾已经趴在桌上睡着了。笔还握在手里。

本子上最后一行字，被月光照着。

*门开着。*

# Chapter 000-2: A Book That Traveled Two Thousand Five Hundred Years

## Page 1

Early the next morning, Ahe was already waiting beneath the big tree at the village entrance.

He opened his notebook and read yesterday's words once more: *Laozi — an old man who loved to watch water. His ears were very big (probably). Rode a blue-green ox westward. Was not fleeing — was simply leaving.*

"Teacher!" The moment the old man appeared, Ahe ran over. "Yesterday you said that those words in the book — Laozi's words — traveled two thousand five hundred years to reach us here. How did they travel?"

The old man sat down, leaning his stick against the tree trunk.

"That is a fine question. The road those words traveled is even longer than the road Laozi rode on his blue-green ox — and far more interesting."

Little Rabbit, Little Stone, and Little Bird arrived one after another and settled around the tree. Morning light leaked through the leaves, painting tiny circles of brightness on the ground.

"Today, I will tell you the journey of a book."

## Page 2

"Yesterday we spoke of Laozi riding his blue-green ox westward. This is where the 'legend portion' begins."

The old man took a small cloth pouch from inside his robe and poured a few beans into his palm.

"The legend portion is the extra light that later generations added, layer by layer. It cannot be taken as history, but it is wonderful to hear. Would you like to hear it?"

"YESSS —" everyone stretched the word out.

"Good. Legend says Laozi, riding his blue-green ox, arrived at Hangu Pass — an important checkpoint on the western border of the Zhou domain. Pass through it, and you had left Zhou territory. The officer guarding the pass was named Yin Xi — sometimes called Guan Yinzi."

"'Guan Yinzi' — is that a name?"

"'Guan' is the place he guarded — the pass. 'Yin' was his official rank. 'Zi' is a respectful suffix — so put together, it means 'Master Yin, Keeper of the Pass.'"

## Page 3

The old man set down one bean.

"This Yin Xi was no ordinary gatekeeper. Legend says he understood 'cloud-watching' — how to read the sky's vapors — and could tell from them whether an extraordinary person was about to pass through."

"Cloud-watching?" Little Bird looked up at the sky. "What can you tell from clouds?"

"In ancient times, some people believed that the color and shape of the clouds could reveal what was about to happen. That day, Yin Xi gazed eastward —"

The old man paused, letting them imagine.

"He saw a plume of purple vapor rising in the east, slowly drifting westward. His heart stirred: a sage was about to pass through the gate."

"Zi qi dong lai — 'Purple vapor from the east'!" Ahe cried out. "I've heard that phrase!"

"Yes. 'Purple vapor from the east' later became a set expression — it means 'something wonderful is on its way, a noble guest is arriving.' Even today, people still paste those four characters up at New Year."

## Page 4

The old man set down a second bean.

"And sure enough — before long, an old man with white hair and a white beard came riding slowly toward the pass on a blue-green ox."

"It really was Laozi!"

"Yin Xi stopped him. Not to make trouble — but to ask him to leave something behind."

"Yin Xi said: Respected elder, you are about to go beyond the pass, and we will never see you again. Could you please write down what you know? Not a lot — just the most important parts."

"Did Laozi agree?" Little Rabbit asked anxiously.

The old man smiled. "The ancient texts say that at first, Laozi did not want to write. He believed that 'those who know do not speak; those who speak do not fully know' — true understanding is not something the mouth can explain clearly. But now he was about to leave without speaking, and the man was so earnest — all right, he would speak a little."

"And so he sat down and wrote a text."

"How long did it take him?"

"No one knows. Perhaps a few days. Perhaps a few months. The number of characters he wrote —"

The old man held up five fingers.

## Page 5

"Five thousand characters?" Little Stone had been learning to count recently.

"That's right — about five thousand characters. Roughly the length of all the stories you have heard put together. He divided them into eighty-one sections. After he finished writing, Laozi climbed onto his blue-green ox, passed through Hangu Pass, and headed west."

"And after that?"

"No one knows what happened afterward." The old man looked toward the far mountains. "Some say he went farther west to continue his practice. Some say he traveled all the way to India and taught people there. Others say his story dissolved into the clouds in the sky. There are many versions — but not one of them can be confirmed."

"He never came back?"

"No. He left behind only those five thousand characters. Like someone who tossed a pebble into a lake — and then turned and walked away. The ripples spread outward, ring after ring — and they have been spreading for two thousand five hundred years."

## Page 6

The wind came through, the leaves rustling. Everyone seemed to be imagining the scene: the pass gate, the dusk, the blue-green ox disappearing into the distance, a pile of bamboo slips covered in writing left in the hands of the pass-keeper.

"But this was only the beginning." The old man pushed the beans forward. "The journey of the book was just starting."

"The bamboo slips that Laozi left — that was the very first copy of the *Dao De Jing*. But bamboo slips — give them a few decades or a century, and the cords snap, the strips rot, the worms eat them. And in ancient times there were no printing presses. Every extra copy had to be made by a person, writing character by character by hand."

"Wouldn't it be easy to make mistakes copying?" Ahe asked.

"Exactly. While copying, someone's eyes would blur, someone would add an extra stroke, someone would miss a whole phrase. The same character — this copy writes *chang* 常, that copy writes *heng* 恒 — because later, an emperor had the character *heng* in his personal name. People copying the book did not dare use that character, so they changed it to *chang*."

"Changed a word in the book just because of one person's name?" Little Rabbit found this incredible.

"That was how things worked then. The emperor's name could not be written casually. And so this book, as it was passed down, naturally developed different 'faces.'"

## Page 7

"Look," the old man drew two lines on the ground, like the banks of a river. "From the moment Laozi set it down until today — the *Dao De Jing* is like a river. The river flows through different places, carries different silt, and its color is never exactly the same. But the river's broad course has never changed."

He made a mark at the first line.

"What did the earliest *Dao De Jing* look like? For the longest time, no one knew for certain. Until —"

He jabbed the ground. " — about fifty years ago, Chinese archaeologists unearthed two very ancient things."

"What did they dig up?" Little Stone loved anything about digging.

"In 1973, at Mawangdui near Changsha in Hunan, people excavated a Western Han tomb and found the *Dao De Jing* written on silk — called the 'silk manuscript.' It had been buried underground for more than two thousand years."

## Page 8

"Characters on silk?" Ahe drew a small square of cloth in his notebook.

"Yes. The words on the silk manuscript differ in many places from the version passed down through later centuries. For instance, where most versions now say 'Dao ke dao, fei chang dao,' the silk manuscript reads 'Dao ke dao ye, fei heng dao ye' — it has an extra *ye*, and *chang* is *heng*. Also, the silk manuscript places the 'De' section first and the 'Dao' section second — the order is reversed."

"So which one is the real one?" Little Rabbit was baffled.

The old man slowly shook his head. "It is not a matter of 'this one is real and that one is fake.' They are both real. They are the same river at different points in time. Upstream and downstream — the water is not identical, but the riverbed is the same."

"And there is an even earlier discovery: in 1993, at Guodian in Hubei, people dug up even older bamboo slips from a Warring States tomb — more than a hundred years older than the silk manuscript. But these bamboo slips were incomplete — they preserve only parts of the *Dao De Jing*."

"Archaeology is amazing," Little Stone said. "It's like flipping time out of the dirt."

## Page 9

The old man pushed another bean forward.

"Before archaeology unearthed the silk manuscripts and bamboo slips, the *Dao De Jing* that people read was the version handed down by generations of hand-copying, then later by woodblock carving, then by machine printing."

He held up two fingers. "Two versions became the most famous."

"One is the Heshang Gong version. Heshang Gong was a hermit of the Han dynasty, who lived in a little hut by a river. It is said that Emperor Wen of Han went in person to seek his teaching. The emperor arrived, but the hermit did not come out to greet him — he just told the emperor to come inside himself. The emperor was a little displeased and said: 'All under heaven belongs to the king. Do you know who I am?' Heshang Gong suddenly levitated into mid-air and said: 'Right now, I touch neither the sky above nor the earth below — would you say this place is still yours?' The emperor hurriedly bowed with respect."

"WHOA! He could fly!" Little Bird's eyes went perfectly round.

"This is probably also legend. But Heshang Gong's annotated text was unquestionably passed down — he explained each chapter in great detail, phrase by phrase, character by character."

## Page 10

"The other, more important version is the Wang Bi version."

The old man set down a slightly larger bean.

"Wang Bi lived during the Three Kingdoms period — a little later than Zhuge Liang. He was extraordinarily brilliant. In his early teens he could already debate the deepest philosophy with the greatest scholars of the age."

"Early teens? Only a little older than us?" Ahe's eyes lit up.

"Yes. The pity is — he died at only twenty-three. But the *Laozi Zhu* — his commentary on Laozi — that he left behind became the foundation of the 'standard text' used by the whole world for more than a thousand years after."

"At twenty-three — he wrote the most important commentary?"

"Indeed. Some people live long but leave little; some people live briefly but leave something very deep. Wang Bi was the latter."

The old man paused. "The original Chinese text you hear in each chapter — it is mainly based on the Wang Bi version. Only in a few places has it been corrected by consulting the silk manuscripts."

## Page 11

Ahe drew a river in his notebook, with many small dots along both banks — representing the different versions. He was beginning to understand: the same river, seen at different times and different places, does not look exactly the same.

"Teacher," he set down his pen, "this book is so old. Has every generation that read it found the same thing in it?"

"Good question." The old man turned one of the beans on the ground. "No — every generation has found something slightly different."

"In a year of war, someone read in it: 'Do not rely on force and violence.' In a year of chaos, someone read in it: 'Be still. Guard yourself first.' Officials read: 'Do not be greedy without end.' Scholars read: 'The deepest wisdom is usually quiet.'"

"Everyone finds the one line they need?"

"More or less. If a book is like a key that opens only one lock, it lives for only one age. If a book is like light that shines into many different troubles — it lives for two thousand five hundred years."

## Page 12

Little Rabbit raised a hand. "What about English? What do people in other parts of the world read?"

"In the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the *Dao De Jing* was carried to Europe by missionaries and began to be translated into many languages. Today, it is one of the most translated books in the world — second only to the Bible."

"The earliest complete English translation was done by an Englishman named James Legge — his Chinese name was Li Yage — completed in 1891. He translated character by character, staying as faithful as he could to the original Chinese meaning. In our book, the English sections at the start of each chapter — the 'Original Text' — come directly from his translation."

Ahe wrote in his notebook: *Legge = Li Yage, 1891*.

"Still, translating the *Dao De Jing* into English is especially difficult. Take the word *Dao* — translated as Way, it loses its living, growing feel. Translated as Path, it feels a bit too light. In the end, most translations do not translate it at all but use the pinyin directly — *Dao*. Let the reader feel for themselves what it means."

"It's like the elephant," Ahe said slowly. "Calling it 'Thick-Pillar Monster' is wrong, but calling it 'Elephant' is right — and yet 'Elephant' is still just a name."

"Exactly. Some things — no single word in any language can hold them completely."

## Page 13

The old man gathered up the beans.

"Now you know where this book came from. An old man who loved to watch water, in a time of great upheaval, lived quietly for many years and observed much. He did not struggle, did not rush, did not force. In the end, he rode a blue-green ox westward. Before he left, he handed five thousand characters to the young keeper of the pass. That young man copied them, and copied them again. Later people copied, carved, printed — generation passing to generation — all the way to today, into my mouth, and from my mouth into your ears."

He stood, tapping his stick lightly on the ground.

"So — what should we do now?"

"Read it," Ahe said.

"Yes. But we do not read it in order to memorize it. We do not read it to remember 'the right answer.'"

"Then what for?" Little Stone asked.

"To look at the world the way Laozi looked at water. To see things from more than one side. To do things without always rushing to be first. To keep the heart uncluttered — not sulking, not quarreling in haste. Observe slowly. Think quietly. Act gently. And once something is finished —" he looked at Ahe.

"Even when it's done, you don't have to stand in the middle," Ahe finished.

The old man nodded.

## Page 14

They walked along the raised path between the fields. The setting sun stretched their shadows into a row. First came Little Stone's round-headed shadow, then Little Rabbit's long-eared shadow, then — in between — Little Bird's shadow flying above the shadows. Last was the old man's bent silhouette and the long shadow of his stick. Ahe walked beside the old man, notebook cradled in his hands.

"Tomorrow," the old man said, "we officially begin Chapter One."

"The elephant chapter!" Little Rabbit said.

"That's right: 'The Dao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Dao.' Every piece we saw was real — but no single piece was the whole. So we must always stay curious. Always keep walking."

He stopped. His stick tapped the dirt path.

"Ahe," he said, tilting his head, "do you know who you remind me of?"

"Who?"

"Guan Yin — Yin Xi. You too keep asking an old man to speak what he knows. That 'book of names' of yours — it is the very same thing as the scroll of bamboo slips that Yin Xi spread before Laozi all those years ago."

Ahe looked down at his old notebook — the cover a little torn, the edges of the pages curling, some pages stained with water, some covered in circles and crosses, some still holding the label the wind had once blown away. He suddenly wanted to keep writing — forever.

## Page 15

That night, Ahe sat alone by the window. Moonlight fell across the cover of his notebook.

He turned to the very last page — a blank sheet he had saved especially for today. He wrote what he had learned:

*1. Laozi rode a blue-green ox to Hangu Pass. A pass-keeper named Yin Xi asked him to write down his teachings.*
*2. Laozi wrote five thousand characters. Then he left. No one knows where he went.*
*3. Those characters were copied and copied again — onto bamboo slips, onto silk, carved onto woodblocks, printed onto paper.*
*4. Copies from different ages differ in small ways — just as the same river may look different in color when seen in different places. But the river is still the river.*
*5. Wang Bi, at only twenty-three, wrote the most important commentary.*
*6. Now the whole world reads this book. In many, many languages.*
*7. Teacher says I am like Guan Yin — asking the old man to speak his words, and writing them down.*

He set down his pen. Then picked it up again and added one more line:

*Perhaps Laozi was not the one who "finished explaining the truth." Laozi was the one who "pushed the door open." After pushing it open, he left first. The door stands open. We — each of us who holds this book — are the ones who walk through afterward.*

Wind came through the window, stirring the pages of the notebook. In the moonlight, Ahe had fallen asleep at his desk. The pen was still in his hand.

The last line of the notebook was lit by the moon.

*The door stands open.*
