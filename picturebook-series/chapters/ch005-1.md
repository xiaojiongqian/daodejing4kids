# 第005-1章：空空的风箱

## 原文

### 第1页

天地不仁，以万物为刍狗；
圣人不仁，以百姓为刍狗。
天地之间，其犹橐籥乎？
虚而不屈，动而愈出。
多言数穷，不如守中。

## 释义

### 第2页

这里的“不仁”，不是冷酷，也不是叫人没爱心。王弼说，天地任自然，让万物按本性生长和归去。

它不用人的爱憎做尺子。雨落下来，不先查谁值得被淋湿。

### 第3页

“刍狗”是古礼里的草扎狗。祭礼前被敬重，祭礼后完成了用处，就回到草木。

老子借这个比喻说：天地不把任何形状永远捧在手心，也不按人的喜欢安排万物。

### 第4页

圣人也学天地，不把“我多仁慈”挂在嘴边。该扶就扶，该止就止，不让偏爱遮住全局。

真正的慈悲不是情绪泛滥，而是看清规律后，把人放回能活的秩序里。

### 第5页

“橐籥”像古代风箱，中间空空的。越推拉，风越出来，炉火越旺。

河上公把这一章叫“虚用”。空不是没有用，空让风有路，让力量不停被生出来。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**仁**：这里不是日常的善良，而是带着人情偏爱的“我要怎样对你”。

**刍狗**：用草扎成的祭礼之物。它不是被恨，只是不用人情去偏爱。

**橐籥**：古代风箱，靠中空和推拉送出风。

### 第7页

**虚而不屈**：空着，却不穷尽。像风箱空，才不会被堵住。

**动而愈出**：一动，作用反而更多。

**守中**：守住中心和分寸，不被话语、情绪和偏见拉偏。

## 正文

### 第8页

铁匠铺里，有一只旧风箱。它肚子空空，木板发亮，手柄被摸得很光。

小徒弟问：“师父，空东西怎么能帮炉火变旺？”

### 第9页

师父拉一下，风从管口冲进炉膛。火苗先是一低，马上又亮起来。

他再推一下，炭火红得像星。空肚子里，竟然送出这么大的风。

### 第10页

小徒弟想把棉布塞进风箱。“塞满了，风就不会乱跑。”

师父笑着摇头：“塞满了，它就不再会呼吸。”

### 第11页

傍晚下雨，雨点落在屋顶，也落在泥地和石头上。它没有挑一朵最乖的花，只给那朵花水。

阿禾站在门口，觉得天地的“不偏心”，有时比偏爱更深。

### 第12页

第二天，孩子们争着解释风箱。有人说它神奇，有人说它普通，越说越吵。

阿禾把手放在风箱边，等风吹过，再轻轻说：“它中间空，所以风能过。”

### 第13页

老人点点头：“话太多，会把意思挤没。话说到中心，心里反而有空间。”

孩子们安静下来，只听见风箱一推一拉，像天地在呼吸。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第五章不是说天地无情到伤人。它说天地不是奖惩账本，不按私心给谁加糖、给谁下雨。

万物在规律里来去。懂这一点，人就少一点抱怨，多一点敬畏。

### 第15页

也不是叫圣人不爱人。真正的爱，不忙着标榜自己，也不乱许愿、乱干预。

像风箱守着中空，该动时动，该停时停；心中不被偏私塞满，风才不断。

# Chapter 005-1: The Empty Bellows

## Original Text

### Page 1

Heaven and earth do not act from (the impulse of) any wish to be benevolent; they deal with all things as the dogs of grass are dealt with.

The sages do not act from (any wish to be) benevolent; they deal with the people as the dogs of grass are dealt with.

### Page 2

May not the space between heaven and earth be compared to a bellows?

'Tis emptied, yet it loses not its power;
'Tis moved again, and sends forth air the more.

Much speech to swift exhaustion lead we see;
Your inner being guard, and keep it free.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Here, "not benevolent" does not mean cruel, and it does not tell us to have no love. Wang Bi explains that heaven and earth follow what is natural, letting things grow and pass according to their own nature.

They do not measure with human liking and dislike. Rain does not first ask who deserves to be wet.

### Page 4

"Straw dogs" were grass figures used in old rites. Before the rite, people treated them with care; after the rite, their use was finished, and they returned to grass.

Laozi uses this image to say: heaven and earth do not hold any form forever in their hands, and they do not arrange the world by human preference.

### Page 5

The sage learns from heaven and earth: help what should be helped and stop what should be stopped, without making kindness into a signboard or letting favoritism cover the whole.

True care is not emotional overflow. It sees the pattern and helps people return to an order in which life can continue.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**not benevolent** — Here this means not sentimental, not partial, and not ruled by favoritism.

**straw dogs** — Grass figures used in ancient rites. They are not hated; they are simply not favored by personal feeling.

**bellows** — An old tool that sends air into a fire.

### Page 7

**empty, yet not used up** — Empty space lets breath keep moving.

**moving, more comes out** — When the bellows moves, more air comes.

**keep the center** — Stay steady and balanced, without being pulled off course by words, fear, or bias.

## Main Text

### Page 8

In the blacksmith's shop, there was an old bellows. Its belly was hollow, its wood shone from years of touch, and its handle was smooth.

The apprentice asked, "Master, how can an empty thing make the fire stronger?"

### Page 9

The master pulled once, and wind rushed into the furnace. The flame dipped, then rose brighter.

He pushed again, and the coals glowed like stars. From an empty belly came a great wind.

### Page 10

The apprentice wanted to stuff cloth into the bellows. "If we fill it," he said, "the wind will not run around."

The master smiled and shook his head. "If we fill it, it will no longer breathe."

### Page 11

At dusk, rain fell on the roof, the mud, and the stones. It did not choose the best flower and water only that one.

Ahe stood at the door and felt that heaven's "not taking sides" could be deeper than favoritism.

### Page 12

The next day, the children argued about the bellows. One called it magic, another called it ordinary, and their voices rose.

Ahe placed a hand beside it, waited for the wind, and said softly, "It is hollow in the middle, so the wind can pass."

### Page 13

The old man nodded. "Too many words can crowd the meaning away. When words reach the center, the heart has space."

The children grew quiet. They only heard the bellows push and pull, like heaven and earth breathing.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Five does not say that heaven and earth are cruel. It says they are not a reward-and-punishment ledger, adding sugar here and rain there by private feeling.

All things come and go within patterns. Seeing this, we complain less and respect more.

### Page 15

It also does not tell the sage to stop loving people. True care does not rush to display itself, make wild promises, or interfere blindly.

Like a bellows keeping its hollow center, it moves when needed and rests when needed. When the heart is not stuffed with favoritism, the wind keeps coming.
