# 第004-1章：不满的井

## 原文

### 第1页

道冲而用之或不盈，渊兮似万物之宗；

挫其锐，解其纷，和其光，同其尘，湛兮似或存。

吾不知谁之子，象帝之先。

## 释义

### 第2页

“冲”不是普通的空洞，而是能通、能容、能生用的空。老子说，道像深器，正因不被填死，万物才有路从中出入。

满得太实，就只剩自己；留着空，反而能继续生出用处。

### 第3页

“渊兮似万物之宗”是说，道深得像不见底的水。万物好像都从那里来，又靠它继续生长。

它不站出来说“我是祖先”。它只是深深地在。

### 第4页

“挫其锐”是把太尖、太冲的劲收一收。“解其纷”是把缠在一起的结慢慢松开。

“和其光”不是熄灭光，而是让光不刺人。“同其尘”是愿意走进普通尘土里。

### 第5页

最后老子说：我不知道道是谁的孩子，仿佛在天帝之前。

意思是：道不是万物中的一个东西，也不是某个答案能抓住的来源；它更像让一切来源得以出现的深处。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**冲（chōng）**：空而通，不满不塞。像井口空着，水才能被打上来。

**不盈（bù yíng）**：不满出来，不把自己塞到没有空处。

**渊（yuān）**：很深的水。这里形容道深远难尽。

### 第7页

**宗（zōng）**：根本、祖先、源头。

**挫锐（cuò ruì）**：把尖锐的锋芒磨柔一点。不是没力量，而是不刺伤别人。

**解纷（jiě fēn）**：解开混乱和纠缠，像慢慢松开打结的线。

### 第8页

**和光（hé guāng）**：让自己的光柔和一些，不用亮光逼人。

**同尘（tóng chén）**：和普通人、普通生活在一起，不把自己摆得很高。

**湛（zhàn）**：清深、安静，好像一直在那里。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村有一口老井。井沿不高，井口黑黑的，看起来什么也没有。

小兔问：“里面空空的，为什么大家每天都来？”

### 第10页

老人把木桶放下去，绳子吱呀吱呀响。过了一会儿，桶上来了，里面晃着清水。

阿禾低头看井，才明白：井口看起来空，正因为空，水才能出来。

### 第11页

小石头想把井填满，好让它看起来“有东西”。他搬来石块，刚放到井边，就被老人拦住。

“填满了，它就不再是井了。”老人说，“有些东西，是靠不满来成全用处的。”

### 第12页

后来，孩子们吵着抢水桶。你拉我扯，绳子打成一个大结。

老人没有用力猛拽，只把结放在膝上，一圈一圈松开。越急，结越紧；越稳，结越开。

### 第13页

太阳快落山时，井水照出一片柔光。光不刺眼，尘土也在光里慢慢飞。

阿禾忽然觉得，道也许就像这口井：深深的，静静的，不说话，却每天给村子水。

## 回扣

### 第14页

回看第四章：道冲而不盈，不是空无一物，而是不被塞成固定东西，所以用不尽。

渊深如宗，是说万物有根；挫锐解纷，是说真正的力量先减少伤害和纠缠。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：井不满，水才能来；光不刺眼，尘土才能被照见。

道像深处的空，不占满世界，却让世界有路、有水、有光。

# Chapter 004-1: The Well That Is Not Full

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Tao is (like) the emptiness of a vessel; and in our employment of it we must be on our guard against all fulness. How deep and unfathomable it is, as if it were the Honoured Ancestor of all things!

### Page 2

We should blunt our sharp points, and unravel the complications of things; we should attemper our brightness, and bring ourselves into agreement with the obscurity of others. How pure and still the Tao is, as if it would ever so continue!

I do not know whose son it is. It might appear to have been before God.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

"Chong" is not ordinary emptiness. It is an opening that can hold, pass, and give rise to use. Laozi says Dao is like a deep vessel: because it is not stuffed full, things can move through it.

When something is packed too full, only itself remains. When it keeps emptiness, use can keep arising.

### Page 4

"Deep, as if the ancestor of all things" means Dao is like water with no visible bottom. The ten thousand things seem to come from it and keep growing through it.

It does not stand up and announce, "I am the ancestor." It is simply, deeply there.

### Page 5

"Blunt sharp points" means softening the urge to stab forward; "unravel complications" means loosening tangled knots slowly.

"Temper brightness" does not put out the light; it makes light gentle. "Join the dust" means entering ordinary life.

Laozi finally says he does not know whose child Dao is: Dao is not one thing among things, but the depth that lets every source appear.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**emptiness of a vessel** — Legge's image for 道冲. An open hollow can hold and give.

**fulness** — Being so packed that nothing more can move. Too much fullness spills over.

**unfathomable** — Too deep to measure.

### Page 7

**Honoured Ancestor** — Legge's rendering for 万物之宗: the deep source or root of all things.

**blunt our sharp points** — Soften sharpness. Not weakness, but strength that does not wound.

**unravel the complications** — Loosen tangles, like a knot in a string.

### Page 8

**attemper our brightness** — Make one's light gentle and balanced.

**obscurity of others** — Legge's way of saying 同尘: living among ordinary dust and ordinary people.

**pure and still** — Clear, deep, and quiet, as if always there.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Kongkong Village had an old well. Its stone rim was low, and its mouth looked dark and empty.

Little Rabbit asked, "If it is empty inside, why does everyone come here every day?"

### Page 10

The old man lowered a wooden bucket, and the rope creaked. After a while, the bucket came up with clear water rocking inside.

Ahe looked down and understood: the well seemed empty, and because it was empty, water could come.

### Page 11

Little Stone wanted to fill the well so it would "have something in it." He carried stones over, but the old man stopped him.

"If you fill it, it will no longer be a well," he said. "Some things work because they are not full."

### Page 12

Later, the children argued over the water bucket. Pulling this way and that, they tied the rope into a hard knot.

The old man did not yank. He laid the knot on his knee and loosened it loop by loop. The faster you pull, the tighter it gets; the steadier you are, the more it opens.

### Page 13

At sunset, the well water held a soft light. The light did not sting the eyes, and dust floated slowly inside it.

Ahe felt that Dao might be like this well: deep, quiet, speaking no words, yet giving water to the village every day.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Looking back at Chapter Four: Dao is open and not full. This does not mean blank nothingness; it means not being fixed and stuffed, so its use does not run out.

Deep as a root, it lets all things have a source. To blunt sharpness and untie tangles means true strength first reduces harm and complication.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: A well is not full, so water can come. Light does not sting, so even dust can be seen.

Dao is like depth with room inside. It does not fill the world with itself, yet it lets the world have roads, water, and light.
