# 第001章：名字不是全部

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道。

名可名，非常名。

### 第2页

无名天地之始；有名万物之母。

故常无欲，以观其妙；常有欲，以观其徼。

此两者同出而异名，同谓之玄。

玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第3页

有些东西，可以说出来；但说出来以后，它就变小了。

比如“路”。我们可以指着地上的小路说：“这是一条路。”可是《道德经》里的“道”，不只是脚下的路。它也像万物生长的办法，像水往低处流的方向，像种子知道什么时候发芽，像人心里慢慢明白的那个“应该怎样走”。

### 第4页

名字也是这样。

我们说“树”，可每一棵树都不一样。有的树高，有的树歪，有的树春天开花，有的树秋天落叶。名字能帮我们认识它，却不能把它全部装进去。

### 第5页

“无名天地之始”可以理解为：在我们给万物起名字以前，世界已经在了。风、雨、光、黑暗，都还没有被我们分成一个一个词。

“有名万物之母”可以理解为：当我们开始起名字，就能分辨万物，知道这是山，那是水，这是门，那是路。

名字不是坏东西。它像一扇门，能带我们走近世界。可是门不是屋子，名字也不是全部。

## 正文

### 第6页

阿禾有一本新本子。

封面是空的，里面也是空的。阿禾想了想，在第一页写下两个大字：名字。

“我要把空空村所有东西都写进去。”他说，“这样，我就懂得整个村子了。”

### 第7页

他跑到路边。

一块石头蹲在那里。阿禾写下“石头”，把小纸条贴上去。石头没有说话，只在太阳底下暖暖地亮着。

一丛草长在墙角。阿禾写下“草”，把小纸条贴上去。草叶轻轻摇了一下，好像还有别的话要说。

一口井在院子里。阿禾写下“井”。

一扇门在风里开着。阿禾写下“门”。

### 第8页

本子很快热闹起来。

石头。草。井。门。路。树。

阿禾越写越高兴。

后来，他跑到小溪边。溪水从山坡上下来，绕过石头，穿过草根，亮晶晶地往前流。

阿禾写下“水”，把纸条放在岸边。

### 第9页

可是水没有停下来。

它弯了一下，绕过一块石头，继续往前走。

“等等！”阿禾追着喊，“你的名字在这里！”

溪水没有回头。它只是哗啦啦，哗啦啦，好像还有很远的地方要去。

### 第10页

这时，一阵风从树上跑下来。

阿禾赶紧写下“风”。

可是风在哪里呢？

贴在树叶上？树叶摇起来。

贴在衣角上？衣角鼓起来。

贴在脸上？脸上凉了一下，风又不见了。

### 第11页

阿禾踮起脚，把纸条往空中一举。纸条飞起来，转了一个圈，落到村口老人的脚边。

老人正在修一只旧木盒。他捡起纸条，看了看。

“你在追什么？”老人问。

“我在追风的名字。”阿禾说，“可是风不肯停下来。”

### 第12页

老人笑了笑，把木盒打开。

盒子里空空的。阳光落进去，像一小块安静的池塘。

“名字像盒子。”老人说，“可以帮你收东西。”

阿禾看着盒子。

老人又说：“可是有些东西，盒子装不下。”

“那名字没用吗？”阿禾问。

“有用。”老人说，“名字也像门。你从门进去，才看见里面。”

### 第13页

阿禾抱着本子，回到小溪边。

这一次，他没有急着写。

他蹲下来，看水。

水里有光。水里有树影。水碰到石头，就弯一弯；碰到落叶，就带它走一小段。

他又听水。

近处是哗啦啦，远处是轻轻的沙沙声。水声有时候像笑，有时候像悄悄说话。

阿禾忽然觉得，“水”这个字很小。

真正的水，比这个字多得多。

可是，他还是在本子上写下：水。

这一次，他没有把字写满整页。他只写在角落里，又在旁边画了一条弯弯的小线。

剩下的地方，他留给水光，留给水声，也留给明天还会变化的小溪。

## 回扣

### 第14页

晚上，阿禾坐在门槛上。

风又来了，吹动他的本子。

第一页上，阿禾新写了一句话：

名字是门，不是屋子。

阿禾一开始以为，只要写下名字，就算真正懂了。可是小溪继续流，风继续跑，世界没有被名字关住。

这就是“名可名，非常名”。名字很有用，它能帮我们分辨、寻找、记住。可是名字不是事物的全部。

### 第15页

这也是“道可道，非常道”。

我们可以用语言靠近“道”，但不能用一句话把它说完。真正的“道”，还在万物的生长里，在水的流动里，在风的来去里。

门开了。

门外还有路。

路的远处，还有很多很多说不完的奇妙。

所以，读第一章时，可以先记住阿禾写下的那句话：名字是门，不是屋子。门打开以后，我们还要继续往里看。

# Chapter 001: Names Are Not Everything

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Dao that can be spoken is not the constant Dao.

The name that can be named is not the constant name.

### Page 2

The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth.

The named is the mother of all things.

So, without desire, we may see its wonder.

With desire, we may see its edges.

These two come from the same source, but they have different names.

Together, they are called mystery.

Mystery upon mystery, the gate of all wonders.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Some things can be spoken.

But once we say them, they become smaller.

For example, a road. We can point to the road under our feet and say, "This is a road." But the "Dao" in the Dao De Jing is not only this road. It is also like the way all things grow, like the direction water knows as it flows downward, like the seed knowing when to sprout, and like the quiet sense inside us that slowly learns which way to go.

### Page 4

Names are like that too.

We say "tree," but every tree is different. Some trees are tall. Some trees lean to one side. Some bloom in spring. Some drop their leaves in autumn.

A name can help us know something, but it cannot hold everything inside it.

### Page 5

"The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth" can mean this: before we gave names to things, the world was already here. Wind, rain, light, and darkness had not yet been divided into separate words.

"The named is the mother of all things" can mean this: when we begin to name things, we can tell them apart. This is a mountain. That is water. This is a door. That is a road.

Names are not bad. A name is like a door. It can help us walk closer to the world. But a door is not the whole house, and a name is not the whole thing.

## Main Text

### Page 6

Ahe had a new notebook.

The cover was blank. The pages were blank too. Ahe thought for a moment, then wrote two big words on the first page:

Names.

"I am going to write down everything in Kongkong Village," he said. "Then I will understand the whole village."

### Page 7

He ran to the roadside.

A stone crouched there. Ahe wrote "stone" and stuck a small label on it. The stone did not speak. It only shone warmly in the sun.

A patch of grass grew by the wall. Ahe wrote "grass" and stuck a label on it. The blades of grass moved a little, as if they still had something else to say.

There was a well in the yard. Ahe wrote "well."

There was a door open in the wind. Ahe wrote "door."

### Page 8

The notebook soon became lively.

Stone. Grass. Well. Door. Road. Tree.

Ahe grew happier and happier.

Then he ran to the little stream. The stream came down from the hillside, curved around stones, passed through grass roots, and flowed forward, shining brightly.

Ahe wrote "water" and placed the label by the bank.

### Page 9

But the water did not stop.

It bent around a stone and kept going.

"Wait!" Ahe called, chasing after it. "Your name is here!"

The stream did not turn back. It only went on, shuala-la, shuala-la, as if it still had somewhere far away to go.

### Page 10

Just then, a gust of wind ran down from the tree.

Ahe quickly wrote "wind."

But where was the wind?

Should he stick the label on the leaves? The leaves shook.

Should he stick it on his sleeve? His sleeve puffed out.

Should he stick it on his face? His face felt cool for a moment, and then the wind was gone again.

### Page 11

Ahe stood on tiptoe and lifted the label into the air. The label flew up, spun once, and landed by the feet of an old man at the village gate.

The old man was fixing an old wooden box. He picked up the label and looked at it.

"What are you chasing?" the old man asked.

"I am chasing the name of the wind," Ahe said. "But the wind will not stop."

### Page 12

The old man smiled and opened the wooden box.

The box was empty. Sunlight fell inside it, like a small quiet pond.

"Names are like boxes," the old man said. "They can help you hold things."

Ahe looked at the box.

The old man said again, "But some things cannot fit inside a box."

"Then are names useless?" Ahe asked.

"They are useful," the old man said. "Names are also like doors. You go through the door, and then you begin to see what is inside."

### Page 13

Ahe carried his notebook back to the stream.

This time, he did not hurry to write.

He crouched down and watched the water.

There was light in the water. There were tree shadows in the water. When water met a stone, it curved around it. When it met a fallen leaf, it carried the leaf for a little while.

Then he listened.

Nearby, the water went shuala-la. Farther away, it made a soft rustling sound. Sometimes the water sounded like laughter. Sometimes it sounded like whispering.

Ahe suddenly felt that the word "water" was very small.

The real water was much more than that word.

Still, he wrote in his notebook:

Water.

This time, he did not fill the whole page with the word. He wrote it in one corner and drew a small curving line beside it.

He left the rest of the page for the light on the water, for the sound of the water, and for the stream that would be different again tomorrow.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

At night, Ahe sat on the threshold.

The wind came again and turned the pages of his notebook.

On the first page, Ahe had written a new sentence:

Names are doors, not houses.

At first, Ahe thought that if he wrote down a name, he truly understood the thing. But the stream kept flowing, the wind kept running, and the world was not locked inside its names.

This is "The name that can be named is not the constant name." Names are useful. They help us tell things apart, search for things, and remember things. But a name is not the whole thing.

### Page 15

This is also "The Dao that can be spoken is not the constant Dao."

We can use words to come closer to the Dao, but we cannot finish it with one sentence. The true Dao is still in the growth of all things, in the flowing of water, and in the coming and going of wind.

The door opens.

Beyond the door, there is still a road.

Farther down the road, there are many, many wonders that cannot be fully spoken.

So when we read Chapter One, we can first remember the sentence Ahe wrote:

Names are doors, not houses.

After the door opens, we must keep looking inside.
