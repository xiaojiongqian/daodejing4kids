# 第001章：名字不是全部

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名，天地之始；有名，万物之母。

故常无欲，以观其妙；常有欲，以观其徼。

此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄。

玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第2页

老子一开口就说了一句很特别的话：可以用话说出来的"道"，就不是那个永远不变的、真正的"道"了；可以取一个名字叫出来的"名"，就不是那个永远不变的、真正的"名"了。字和它要装的东西，不是同一件事。就像"爱"——你说得出这个字，但妈妈抱着你时那种温暖，比字大得多。就像"勇敢"——你写得出这两个字，但站在跳台上心跳咚咚响、最后还是跳下去了，字装不下。老子不是叫我们丢掉文字，只是轻轻提醒：字像一扇门。门帮你走进去，但门不是屋子。走进去了，还要继续往里看。

### 第3页

在天地刚开始的时候，什么都还没有名字。没有"天"和"地"，没有"山"和"水"——但世界已经在。这就是"无名，天地之始"。后来人们开始取名字：日、月、春、秋。有了名字，万物才能被分辨、记住——这就是"有名，万物之母"。

所以老子说：常放下"我全知道了"的心，就能看见深层的奇妙；心里一直装着"我摸到的才对"，就只会看到一小块边边。"无"和"有"看起来相反，其实是同一个源头出来的——就像黑夜和白天，像在对立，却来自同一个太阳。这个源头太深了，老子给它起了一个字："玄"。玄之又玄——深了还可以更深。而这个最深最深的源头，是所有奇妙走进来的一扇门。

## 难词注解

### 第4页

**道（dào）**：本来指"路"。在《道德经》里，它是万物生长变化背后那个看不见却一直在的东西——像水往低处流的"方向"，像种子知道何时发芽的"规律"。**常（cháng）**：永远不变、一直如此的。能被人用话说尽的，就不是不变的"常道"。**名（míng）**：名字很管用，但不是事物本身——你叫"小禾"，可这两个字装不下你的笑声和做梦的样子。

**无（wú）**：看不见的东西，不是"什么都没有"，而是像空气——看不见，却一直都在。**有（yǒu）**：看得见、摸得着的存在。**天地之始**：天和地刚出现时，世界最初的模样。**万物之母**：比喻——人们给万物取名字，就像妈妈把孩子生出来，从此万物被"认得"了。

### 第5页

**欲（yù）**：心里带着预设的期待去看。老子说的"无欲"不是不要任何东西，而是放下"我早就知道""我说的才对"的心。**妙（miào）**：深处藏着的奇妙——不是一眼就能看到的。**徼（jiào）**：边、表面能看见的那一层；"妙"在里，"徼"在外。**玄（xuán）**：深、远、不容易看清，老子用它形容最深的源头。**众妙之门**：所有奇妙走进来的那扇门——理解了这个源头，就像推开一扇门，门外是说不完的惊奇。

## 正文

### 第6页

这天早晨，空空村来了一样从没出现过的东西。不是风——风只把树叶吹得沙沙响。不是雨——雨还没来呢。是一声低沉浑厚的"哞——呜——"，从村口大路传来。阿禾手里的本子震了一下，小兔弟弟的耳朵竖成筷子，小石头嘴里的半个地瓜差点掉了。阿禾第一个跑到村口，然后停住了——嘴张着，眼圆着，腿像被钉在地上。一个灰色的、山一样巨大的身影，正从晨雾里慢慢走出来。

### 第7页

四条腿，每条都有老槐树的树干那么粗。两片大耳朵像灰被子在风里扇。一条长鼻子垂到地又弯起来，像有自己主意的灰蛇，鼻尖一动一动，闻了闻路边的小白花，又闻了闻阿禾晾在门口的鞋子。最吓人的是嘴角伸出两根白牙，弯弯的、尖尖的，闪象牙色光。阿禾见过牛、马、村长家的大黄狗——可从没见过这种东西。他翻开本子，手发抖。写下"大——"，又划掉。腿粗得像柱子，他改成"粗柱子怪"，合上本子，转身就跑。"有怪物！有粗柱子怪！"

### 第8页

小兔弟弟胆子小，远远绕到大家伙背后——灰灰的、毛毛的、肚皮鼓鼓的，一根细东西在屁股后面甩来甩去，像条粗绳子，左一下右一下，好像很快活。他跑回来，脸都白了："它有一条鞭子尾巴！比绳子粗，比蛇长——一甩就能把你卷上天！"小鸟飞出去，刚好看到那大家伙举起鼻子在闻树枝上的果子——红红的、软软的、弯弯伸上来，像一条没鳞的、从地里长出来的大蛇。她冲回来落在阿禾肩上，翅膀扇得像急雨："它有一条蛇！灰蛇，这么粗，这么长，从脸上长出来的！到处闻——下一个就是你的脑袋！"

"粗柱子。""鞭子尾巴！""灰蛇鼻子！""你说的不对！""你才不对！""你们都是瞎子吗？明明是蛇！"他们吵得那么大声，村口那只旧木桶都被震得嗡嗡响。

### 第9页

这时小石头来了。他做事最踏实，不跑不叫，走到大象侧面——看到两片灰色的东西扇过来又扇过去，薄薄的、宽宽的，像两片巨大的、自己会动的扇子。每扇一下，小草弯腰、雨滴飞起、小石子滚到一边。他双手叉腰走回来："你们吵什么？那明明是一对大扇子！"

"粗柱子腿！""鞭子尾巴！""灰蛇鼻子！""大扇子耳朵！"四个人你瞪我我瞪你，脸涨得通红。小兔的萝卜滚出来没人捡，小鸟气得差点叼走阿禾的头发，小石头说不过别人，蹲在地上画圈。"我就是看见了！""你看见的不对！""你才不对！"

### 第10页

这时村口传来轻轻的脚步声。老人的手杖点在地上——笃、笃——不慌不忙。他站定，看了看阿禾的本子、小兔竖成直角的耳朵、小鸟炸开的绒毛、蹲在地上画圈的小石头。然后他望向晨雾那边，望了很久。"你们都过去。一起再看一遍。"

四个人不服气地往前走。老人让他们每人绕一圈——从头到尾、从左到右、从腿到耳——认真看。阿禾看到了小石头说的"扇子"。小兔看到了阿禾说的"粗柱子"。小鸟看到了小兔说的"鞭子"。小石头看到了小鸟说的"蛇"。四个人站成一排，忽然不说话了。

### 第11页

晨光照在那大家伙身上。扇子耳朵轻轻扇动，吹起地上的小花；粗柱子腿稳稳站住，像四座小山；鞭子尾巴甩来甩去，赶走嗡嗡的虫子；蛇鼻子一卷一卷地卷起路边青草，送进嘴里。然后它微微抬起头，发出那声低沉的"哞——呜——"。那个声音不像怪物——它像远山那边，云被风推过山顶时发出来的。

小鸟第一个打破安静，轻轻飞到大象头上。大象没生气，扇了扇耳朵，像在打招呼。小兔深吸口气，伸手碰了碰象腿——毛毛的、温温的，像被太阳晒了一上午的石板。小石头摸了摸鼻子，软软的又有力，鼻尖轻轻卷住他手指，痒得他咯咯笑。阿禾站在旁边，心里有什么在一点一点打开。"它不是怪物。""它是一个……大……一个全部在一起的……"他不知道该叫什么。老人笑了笑："这种东西，在很远很远的地方才有。那里的人叫它——象。"

### 第12页

"象。"阿禾念了一遍。这个字不大也不长，却把粗柱子腿、扇子耳朵、蛇鼻子、鞭子尾巴——全装进去了。他又翻开本子，第一页上还留着被划掉的"粗柱子怪"。他在后面写：象。四个朋友各看见了一小部分，吵了架，又和好。现在我看见了它全部的样子——好大，好温柔。

老人不知何时走到身后，看了看阿禾写的字，轻轻点头。"你第一次看到的，也不全是错的。粗柱子——那是它的腿。你摸到了真的东西。只是你摸到的是它的一条腿，不是全部的象。""那每个人的答案，都有一点点对？""对。""也都不全对？""也对。"阿禾不太明白。老人笑着把拐杖往大路一指："腿是真的，耳朵是真的，鼻子和尾巴也都是真的。但如果每个人都只守着自己摸到的那一小块，把它当成全部——他们就在吵架了。"

### 第13页

太阳升到了头顶。阿禾和大象坐在一起，小鸟在象耳朵上打盹，小兔靠着象腿晒太阳，小石头在数脚趾甲。阿禾翻开新的一页，想写"象"——却想起了昨天：溪水绕过他贴的纸条哗啦啦往前流，风把纸条吹上天落到老人脚边。也想起刚才自己只看见腿，就喊了"粗柱子怪"。

"象"这个字，跟"溪水"、"风"一样——是一扇门。走进门，才能看见里面的东西。而里面的东西，比字大多了。他摸了摸大象的腿，温的。又把耳朵贴上去——肚子里传来低沉的咕噜噜，像远方的雷，从很深很深的地方传上来。他在纸上画了一圈纹路，又画了一扇小门。门开着，门外画了一条路。

## 回扣

### 第14页

夜里，月光把大象的影子拉得很长。大象在村口大树下站着睡着了——像一座闭着眼的小山，呼——吸，呼——吸，大耳朵随呼吸轻轻扇动。阿禾和小伙伴们坐在不远处。

"我们今天差点打了一架。"阿禾说。"因为我们每个人都只看见了它的一部分。"小兔说。小鸟把头埋进翅膀里："我当时真的以为是蛇。"小石头挠头："我当时真以为是扇子。""如果我们没有走那一圈，"阿禾慢慢说，"就永远不知道它是一头大象。"把所有人的一小片拼在一起，才拼出了真正的它。

### 第15页

阿禾翻开本子，把今天的事写成了短短几句话：今天来了一位新朋友，很大。大到我们每个人只看见它的一小片——我看见粗腿叫它"粗柱子怪"，小兔看见尾巴叫它"鞭子怪"，小鸟看见鼻子叫它"蛇怪"，小石头看见耳朵叫它"扇子怪"。但我们看的都是同一个它。把所有人的一小片拼在一起，才拼出了真正的大象。

他停了一下，在旁边加了一行：这大概就是"名可名，非常名"吧——我们起的每个名字都是真的，可没有一个名字能装下全部的它。

### 第16页

风从象耳边吹来，吹动了阿禾的本子。月光下，大象的轮廓安安静静——它不属于"粗柱子"，也不属于"蛇鼻子"，它是一个完整的、温柔的生命，比任何名字都大，比任何争吵都安静。

阿禾忽然想起老人讲过的另一个故事。

### 第17页

很久以前，东方有一位老子，骑着青牛出了函谷关。西方有一位佛陀，赤脚走出王宫，在菩提树下坐了下来。同一夜——希腊有一位叫苏格拉底的老人，拄着拐杖在雅典街头走来走去，向每一个路过的人问："你说你懂了——可你真的懂了吗？"这三个人从没见过面。话不一样，衣服不一样，走的路也不一样。可是他们都走到了同一座山的山顶上。老子说：这叫"道"。佛陀说：这叫"法"。苏格拉底说：这叫"善"。名字不一样——指向的，却是同一样东西。

### 第18页

这就是"此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄"。就像阿禾看见腿、小鸟看见鼻子、小兔看见尾巴——每个人的视角都有限，可完整的大象（或者叫它"道"），一直在那里。

风吹过来。月光亮了一点点。门开着。门外还有很长很长的路。路上，还有很多没被说出的名字——也有很多，永远不用名字就能感受到的奇妙。

# Chapter 001: Names Are Not Everything

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Dao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Dao.
The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name.

(Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth;
(conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

Always without desire we must be found,
If its deep mystery we would sound;
But if desire always within us be,
Its outer fringe is all that we shall see.

Under these two aspects, it is really the same; but as development takes place, it receives the different names.
Together we call them the Mystery.
Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

> — Translated by James Legge (1891), *The Texts of Taoism*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 39. Public domain.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Laozi opens with something startling: the Dao you can walk upon, the path you can follow — that is not the eternal, unchanging Dao. The name you can give something is not its real, eternal name. A word and the thing it points to are not the same. Take "love" — you can spell it, but the warm, safe feeling when your mother holds you is bigger than any letters. "Brave," too — you can write it, but the moment you stand on the high dive, heart thumping, and jump — words can't hold what happened inside you. Laozi is not saying throw words away. He's gently reminding us: words are doors. A door lets you enter — but the door is not the house. Walk through, and keep looking.

### Page 3

Before heaven and earth began, nothing had a name. No "sky," no "mountain," no "river" — yet the world was already here. That's "(conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator." Then people began naming things: sun, moon, spring, autumn. Now we could tell things apart and remember them. That's "(conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things." So Laozi says: let go of desire, of "I already know the whole picture," and you'll see life's deep mystery. But if desire always fills your mind — "my piece is the whole truth" — you'll only see the outer fringe. "Having no name" and "having a name" look like opposites — yet they come from one source. Like night and day: they seem to stand against each other, but both come from the same sun. This source is so deep that Laozi gives it one word: "the Mystery." Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 4

**trodden** — Walked upon, followed like a path. Legge's choice for 道可道: the Dao that you can follow like a road is not the true Dao.

**enduring and unchanging** — Legge's rendering of 常 (cháng): eternal, always true. The Dao you can fully explain is not the enduring Dao.

**name** — A label. Handy, but never the thing itself. Your name is useful — but those letters can't hold your laugh, your temper, or the face you make when you dream.

**Originator** — Legge's word for 天地之始: the source from which heaven and earth first came. The earliest dawn, before anything had a label.

**Mother** — Legge's rendering of 万物之母: the nourishing source of all things. When people started naming things, it was like a mother giving birth — each thing could now be "known."

### Page 5

**desire** — Legge's rendering of 欲 (yù): looking at something with a fixed idea already in your head. "Without desire" means letting go of "I already know" or "only my answer counts."

**deep mystery** — Legge's rendering of 妙 (miào): the hidden marvel you don't see at first glance. The treasure inside.

**outer fringe** — Legge's rendering of 徼 (jiào): the surface, the edge you see from outside. Deep mystery is within; outer fringe is the boundary.

**the Mystery** — Legge's rendering of 玄 (xuán): deep, far, hard to make out. Laozi's word for the deepest shared source.

**gate of all that is subtle and wonderful** — Legge's rendering of 众妙之门: the entrance. Once you glimpse the source, a door swings open — behind it, more wonder than words can tell.

## Main Text

### Page 6

That morning, something Kongkong Village had never seen before arrived. Not the wind — the wind only rustled leaves. Not the rain — rain hadn't come yet. A low, deep, rumbling "Mooooo—" rolled in from the main road. Ahe's notebook trembled; Little Rabbit's ears shot up like chopsticks; Little Stone nearly dropped his sweet potato. Ahe was first to the village gate, then froze — mouth open, eyes round, feet as if nailed to the ground. Out of the morning mist walked a gray, mountain-sized shape.

### Page 7

Four legs — each thick as the old locust tree. Two ears flapping like gray blankets. One nose so long it drooped to the ground, then curled up, like a gray snake with its own ideas — sniffing the white flowers, then Ahe's shoes drying by the door. From its mouth jutted two white tusks, curved and sharp, gleaming in the early light. Ahe had seen cows, horses, the village chief's big yellow dog — but never this. He opened his notebook. His hand shook. He wrote "Big—" and crossed it out. Those legs, thick as pillars — he wrote "Thick-Pillar Monster," slammed the notebook shut, and ran. "MONSTER! A Thick-Pillar Monster!"

### Page 8

Little Rabbit was too scared to get close. He circled way around to the back — gray, fuzzy, round belly, and a skinny thing swishing side to side like a thick rope, left-right, left-right, quite cheerful. He ran back, pale: "It has a Whip Tail! Thicker than rope, longer than a snake — one swish and you're in the sky!"

Little Bird flew out just as the creature lifted its nose to sniff a branch for fruit. To her, the nose curved up — pink, soft, wriggling — like a scaleless snake sprouting from the earth. She shot back to Ahe's shoulder, wings beating like leaves in a storm: "It has a SNAKE! A gray snake — THIS thick, THIS long — growing right out of its face! Sniffing everywhere — your head is next!"

"Thick Pillar." / "Whip Tail." / "Gray Snake Nose!" / "You're wrong." / "No, YOU'RE wrong!" / "Are you all BLIND?" They shouted so loud the old wooden bucket by the gate hummed.

### Page 9

Then came Little Stone — the steady one. No running, no yelling. He walked around to the creature's side and saw two gray flaps sweeping back and forth, thin and wide, like giant fans with their own motor. Each flap bent the grass, flung raindrops, sent pebbles rolling. He marched back, hands on his hips: "What are you fighting about? Those are Big Fans. Head-fans. One wave — WHOOMPH — big wind."

"Thick Pillar Legs!" / "Whip Tail!" / "Snake Nose!" / "Fan Ears!" Four red faces, four shouting voices. Little Rabbit's carrot rolled away and nobody cared. Little Bird nearly yanked out Ahe's hair. Little Stone squatted and drew circles in the dirt. "I SAW it!" — "You saw WRONG!" — "NO, YOU!"

### Page 10

Then — soft footsteps at the gate. Tap, tap. A walking stick, unhurried. The old man stopped and looked: Ahe's notebook, Little Rabbit's stiff right-angle ears, Little Bird's ruffled feathers, Little Stone drawing circles. He gazed toward the mist for a long moment. "All of you — go over there. Look again. Together."

Grumbling, the four moved forward. The old man made each one walk a full circle — head to tail, left to right, legs to ears — and really look. Ahe saw Little Stone's "fans." Little Rabbit saw Ahe's "thick pillars." Little Bird saw Little Rabbit's "whip." Little Stone saw Little Bird's "snake." They stood in a row. Nobody spoke.

### Page 11

Morning light fell across the creature. Fan-ears flapped softly, puffing little flowers into the air. Pillar-legs stood steady as four hills. Whip-tail swished away buzzing bugs. Snake-nose curled around tufts of roadside grass and tucked them into its mouth. Then it lifted its head and let out that low, deep "Mooooo—." It was no monster's cry — it was like cloud passing over a distant mountain.

Little Bird broke the silence, fluttering onto its head. The creature didn't mind — it flapped an ear, like waving hello. Little Rabbit touched its leg: fuzzy, warm, like stone that had sunned all morning. Little Stone touched the nose — soft and strong, the tip curling around his finger, tickling him until he giggled. Ahe stood nearby, something inside him slowly opening. "It's not a monster… It's a… one big, whole…" He didn't have the word. The old man smiled: "In a land far away, they call it: Elephant."

### Page 12

"Elephant." Ahe said the word. It was not big, but it held all of it — the pillars, the fans, the snake, the whip — all of it in one word. He opened his notebook. "Thick-Pillar Monster" was still there, crossed out on the first page. He wrote: Elephant. Four friends each saw one small piece. We argued. Then we made up. Now I've seen the whole of it. Huge. And gentle.

The old man had come up behind him. He looked at the page and nodded. "What you first saw wasn't all wrong. Thick pillar — that was its leg. You touched something real. Just — one leg, not the whole elephant." / "So everyone's answer has a little bit of right?" / "Yes." / "And a little bit of not-quite-right?" / "Also yes." Ahe looked puzzled. The old man pointed his stick down the road: "The leg was real. The ear — real. The nose and tail — real. But if each person clings to their one tiny piece and calls it the WHOLE — you get a fight."

### Page 13

The sun climbed overhead. Ahe sat beside the elephant. Little Bird dozed on an ear, Little Rabbit leaned against a sun-warmed leg, Little Stone counted toenails. Ahe opened a fresh page. He wanted to write "Elephant" — but his mind drifted to yesterday: the stream flowing right past his label, shuala-la; the wind snatching his paper and dropping it at the old man's feet. And just now — seeing only legs and shouting "Monster."

"Elephant," like "Stream" and like "Wind," is a door. Walk through it — then you begin to see. And what's inside is so much bigger than the word. He put his hand on the elephant's leg. Warm. He leaned in — a low rumble deep in its belly, like faraway thunder, coming from somewhere very far down. On the page, he drew winding lines. Then a small door, open. Outside it — a road.

Night came. Moonlight stretched the elephant's shadow long. The elephant slept standing under the big tree — a hill with its eyes closed. Huuuu-haaaa, each breath moving those huge ears just a little. Ahe and his friends sat nearby.

"We almost got into a real fight today," Ahe said. "Because each of us had only seen one part," said Little Rabbit. Little Bird tucked her head under her wing: "I really thought it was a snake." Little Stone scratched his head: "I really thought it was a fan." / "If we'd never walked that whole circle," Ahe said slowly, "we'd never have known it was an elephant." Only by fitting everyone's pieces together did the real creature appear.

### Page 15

Ahe opened his notebook and wrote: Today a new friend arrived — so big that each of us could only see one piece. I saw thick legs and called it "Pillar-Monster." Little Rabbit saw the tail and called it "Whip-Monster." Little Bird saw the nose and called it "Snake-Monster." Little Stone saw the ears and called it "Fan-Monster." But we were all looking at the same friend. Only by putting our pieces together did we get the real Elephant.

He paused, then added: Maybe this is what "the name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name" means. Every name we gave was true — but no single name could hold all of it.

### Page 16

Wind stirred across the elephant's ears and rustled Ahe's pages. In the moonlight, the elephant sat quietly — it didn't belong to "Thick Pillar" or "Snake Nose." It was a whole, gentle life — bigger than any name, quieter than any argument.

And Ahe remembered another story the old man had told him.

### Page 17

Long ago, in the East, an old sage named Laozi rode a green ox through a mountain pass and vanished. In the West, a prince named Siddhartha walked barefoot from his palace and sat beneath a Bodhi tree. On that very same night — so the story goes — an old man named Socrates walked the streets of Athens, leaning on his stick, asking everyone he met: "You say you understand — but do you really?" These three never met. Their words were different, their clothes were different, the roads they walked were different. Yet all three reached the peak of the same mountain. Laozi called it "Dao." The Buddha called it "Dharma." Socrates called it "the Good." Different names. Same mountain.

### Page 18

That is what Chapter One means: "Under these two aspects, it is really the same; but as development takes place, it receives the different names. Together we call them the Mystery." Ahe saw legs. Little Bird saw a nose. Little Rabbit saw a tail. Each view was limited. But the whole Elephant — call it Dao — was there the whole time.

The wind blew. The moonlight brightened, just a little. The door stands open. Beyond it, a long, long road. And on that road — many names not yet spoken, and many wonders that need no name at all.
