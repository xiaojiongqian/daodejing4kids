# 第000-1章：一个爱看水的老人

## 第1页

这一天，阿禾没有急着翻开他的"名字本"。他坐在村口的大树下，望着远山发呆。

老人拄着拐杖走过来，坐在他旁边。两个人安安静静地坐了一会儿。

"老师，"阿禾忽然问，"你教我们的那些道理——'道可道，非常道'——是谁第一个说出来的？"

老人把手杖横在膝上，望着远山，好像在看一个很远很远的地方。

"是一个爱看水的老人，"他说，"他的名字叫老子。"

## 第2页

"老子？"小兔弟弟不知什么时候凑了过来，"这个名字好奇怪。是因为他很老吗？"

老人笑了。"有人说，老子姓李，名叫李耳。'老子'是大家对他的尊称——就像我们叫'老师'一样，意思是'有智慧的老先生'。"

"李耳？这个耳朵的'耳'？"阿禾在本子上写了一个字。

"对。传说他的耳朵特别大，所以取名叫'耳'。古人觉得大耳朵是聪明的象征——耳朵大，听得远，装得下很多声音。"

小兔摸了摸自己的耳朵，有点得意。

老人接着说："也有人说，他叫老聃。'聃'也是耳朵大的意思。不过，关于他，史书上记的很少，后人讲的故事很多。有些是真的，有些是后人加上去的。就像一个发光的人，后来的人一层一层地给他加了更多光。"

## 第3页

"那他活在什么时候？"阿禾问。

"很久很久以前——比秦始皇修长城还要早三百多年。大约两千五百年前。"

小石头正在数石子，听到这个数字，石子从手指缝里掉了下去。

"那是一个很吵的时代。"老人慢慢说，"到处在打仗，路上跑着兵车，田里长着兵器。诸侯国你争我抢，今天你打我，明天我打你。老百姓住在晃来晃去的地方，不知道明天住的地方还属不属于同一个国家。"

"那不是很可怕吗？"小兔把耳朵垂了下来。

"是很可怕。可是，很奇怪——最吵的年代，也出了最多安静地思考的人。"

## 第4页

老人在地上画了一条线。

"中国那个时候叫东周，东周又分成两段：前面叫春秋，后面叫战国。老子活在春秋的尾巴上。"

他在线上画了很多小圈。"这些是当时的诸侯国。有齐、楚、燕、韩、赵、魏、秦……不下几十个。每个都说自己是对的，每个都想让别人听自己的。礼法被撕来撕去，约定被踩在脚下，有本事的人到处跑，没本事的人被扔在一旁。"

"像我们那天吵架的样子？"小鸟站在树枝上，歪着头。

"像。只不过他们不是吵大象叫什么——他们吵天下该归谁管。"

## 第5页

"那老子是做什么的？"阿禾问。

"他在周朝的国家图书馆当管理员。那时候的书不像我们现在的纸本书，是一片一片的竹简——把竹子削成细长的条，用绳子串起来，在上面刻字或写字。很重，一大堆竹简也写不了多少内容。"

老人拿起一根小树枝，在地上画了一片竹简的样子。

"老子的工作，就是守着这些竹简。有人来查资料，他帮忙找；没有人来的时候，他坐在那里看。他看了很多很多书——历史、诗词、天象、礼法、战争的记载、祖先的传说——什么都看。"

## 第6页

"整天坐着看书，不闷吗？"小鸟问。

"不闷。"老人摇摇头，"看书只是他一半的工作。另一半的时间，他看窗外。"

"窗外有什么好看的？"

"有一棵树。有一条小河。有走过的路人、飞奔的马、吵架的官员、讨饭的老人。老子不光看书——他还看水。"

"看水？"

"看水。"老人站起来，走到旁边的小溪边。阿禾和小伙伴们跟了过去。

"你们看这水，"老人指着溪水，"它从来不急着跑，可是石头挡不住它。遇到大石头，它绕着走；遇到悬崖，它就往下跳；冬天结冰，春天又流动。它从来不跟谁打架，可是谁也不能让它停下。"

阿禾盯着溪水看了很久。水哗啦啦地流，什么话也没说。

## 第7页

"老子看了一辈子水。"老人回到树下，接着说，"他发现，全世界最软的东西——水——反而能磨穿最硬的石头。把一块大石头扔进河里，水不急也不气，一天一天地从石头上面流过。十年、百年、千年……石头被磨成了圆圆的卵石，水还在流。"

"他还看树。"小鸟说。

"对。大树刚长出来时只是一颗小芽，软得一指就能掐断。可是它慢慢长——一年、十年、百年——长到几个大人手拉手都抱不住。强大的东西，常常是从最柔软的小东西开始的。"

阿禾在本子上写了一个字：水。又写了一个字：树。他忽然觉得，这两个字好像会动。

## 第8页

老人顿了顿，声音放轻了一些。

"关于老子的出生，有一个很神奇的故事——但你们要记住，这是传说，不是历史事实。"

"什么是传说？"小兔问。

"传说是后人加上去的特别故事，为了让一个人显得更了不起。就像给真实的人披上一件发光的袍子。"

"快讲快讲！"小鸟在树枝上跳了两下。

"好。据说，老子的母亲怀他，一怀就怀了八十一年。"

"八十一年？！"大家同时喊了出来。小石头的手指停在半空中——他刚数到四十七。

"对，八十一年。等到他出生的时候，胡子头发全都白了，所以大家叫他'老子'——'老'就是年纪大的意思，他一出生就像个老公公。又因为他生在一棵李树下面，就姓了'李'。"

## 第9页

小兔想了想，忽然笑了："一个长着白胡子、白头发的小婴儿——那也太好笑了！"

老人也笑了："对，这件事本身就很好笑。所以后来的人说这是传说，不是历史。"

"可是，"阿禾慢慢说，"这个故事好像也在讲一个道理。"

"什么道理？"

"像水，像种子发芽——最厉害的东西，好像在很早很早的时候就已经准备好了？"

老人看了阿禾一眼，眼睛亮了一下。"你有点明白了。传说不一定是真的，但传说里面常常藏着人们想说的道理。老子这个人的思想太深了，后人不知道怎么形容，就给了他一个很奇特的出生故事，好像在说：他来的时候，就已经带着很久很久的智慧了。"

## 第10页

"老子见过别的大人物吗？"小石头问。

"见过。历史上记着一件很有趣的事：孔子去拜访过老子。"

"孔子？！"大家都认识这个名字。

"对，就是那个后来被所有人尊敬的孔夫子。他比老子小一些，当时已经很有名气了。孔子听说周朝图书馆里有一位非常有智慧的老人家，就特意赶去请教。"

"他们说了什么？"

"史书上只记了很少的几句。孔子问了关于'礼'的问题——礼，就是人们相处的规矩。老子的回答大概意思是：你问的那种规矩，制定它的人骨头早就烂成土了，只剩下几句话飘在风里。"

## 第11页

"哇，"阿禾吸了口气，"老子说话，比我们老师直接多了。"

"孔子没有生气吗？"

"没有。孔子回去以后，对自己的学生说了一番非常有意思的话。他说：'鸟，我知道它会飞；鱼，我知道它会游；地上的走兽，我知道它会跑。会跑的可以张网来捉，会游的可以放线来钓，会飞的可以用箭来射。至于龙——我不知道它怎么乘着风云飞到天上。我今天见到老子，他就像一条龙。'"

大家安静了一会儿。阿禾想象一条龙在云里穿行——看得见一点鳞片，却看不清全身。

"龙是什么样子呢？"小兔轻声问。

"没有人真正见过龙的全部——就像没有人能用一个名字装下'道'的全部。"

## 第12页

老人停了一下，把拐杖换了一只手。

"老子在图书馆待了很多年。书看够了，水看够了，人也看够了。他看着周朝一天比一天乱，王子们争来抢去，诸侯们你攻我伐，礼法名存实亡。"

"那他怎么办？"阿禾问。

"他决定走。"

"走？"

"对。离开。不争了，也不留了。据说他骑了一头青牛——青，是一种灰蓝灰蓝的颜色——慢慢地往西边走。"

"为什么要往西边走？西边有什么？"

"不知道。可能他自己也不知道。他只是想离开这个太吵太乱的地方，往安静的地方走。往山的另一边走。"

## 第13页

阿禾在本子上画了一头牛。又把它涂成灰蓝色的。

"青牛是什么牛？和水牛一样吗？"

"古书上没写得很清楚。有人说青牛就是水牛，也有人说是一种颜色特别的牛。都不重要。重要的是那个画面。"

老人在空中画了一条线："一个白头发白胡子的老人，骑着一头缓缓走路的牛，往西边的山里去。他不急，不忙，牛走多快他就多快。牛停下来吃草，他就坐在路边看云。"

"为什么要骑牛，不骑马？"

"马跑得太快了。"老人笑了一下，"一个急着离开的人骑马，一个不急的人骑牛。"

小石头插嘴："那就是说，老子其实也不是很急着离开？"

"你问到了。他不逃，他只是走。"

## 第14页

太阳开始往西斜了。大树的影子慢慢拉长。阿禾看着老人——看着他的皱纹、白头发、安静放在膝上的手。

"老师，"他忽然问，"你见过老子吗？"

老人哈哈笑了起来。笑声很轻，像风吹树叶。

"傻孩子，老子是两千五百年前的人。我不可能见过他。"

"那你怎么知道你给我们讲的这些——水啦、树啦、不要吵架啦——就是他说的？"

老人把拐杖往地上一点。

"好问题。这也是我明天要跟你们讲的——他写下来的那些字，是怎么从两千五百年前，一路传到今天，传到我嘴里，再传到你耳朵里。"

## 第15页

傍晚的风吹过来，带着炊烟的味道。小鸟飞回树上的窝。小兔站起来拍拍屁股。小石头把地上的石子排成一条弯弯的小路。

阿禾坐在原地，在本子上写了今天的日期。然后在下面写：

*老子——一个爱看水的老人。他的耳朵很大（可能）。他的母亲怀了他八十一年（这大概是传说）。他骑一头青牛往西边走了。他不逃，他只是走。*

他停了一下，又加了一行：

*他像一条龙——看得见鳞片，看不见全身。*

远山那边，云正在变颜色。灰蓝灰蓝的，像青牛的背。

# Chapter 000-1: An Old Man Who Loved to Watch Water

## Page 1

On this day, Ahe did not rush to open his "book of names." He sat beneath the big tree at the village entrance, gazing blankly at the distant mountains.

The old man came over leaning on his walking stick and sat down beside him. The two of them sat quietly for a while.

"Teacher," Ahe suddenly asked, "those ideas you teach us — 'The Dao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Dao' — who was the first person to say them?"

The old man laid his stick across his knees and looked toward the distant mountains, as if watching somewhere very far away.

"It was an old man who loved to watch water," he said. "His name was Laozi."

## Page 2

"Laozi?" Little Rabbit had come over without anyone noticing. "That's a funny name. Is it because he was very old?"

The old man smiled. "Some say Laozi's family name was Li, and his given name was Er — Li Er. 'Laozi' is a title of respect — like when you call me 'teacher.' It means 'the wise old master.'"

"Li Er — the 'Er' that means 'ear'?" Ahe wrote the character in his notebook.

"Yes. Legend says his ears were especially large, so he was named 'Er.' Ancient people thought big ears were a sign of wisdom — big ears hear more, carrying many sounds."

Little Rabbit touched his own ears, a little proudly.

The old man went on: "Some also say he was called Lao Dan. 'Dan' also means large ears. But the truth is — very little is written about him in the history books. What later people have told are mostly stories. Some are true, some were added later. It's like a person who shines — later generations kept adding more light, layer by layer."

## Page 3

"So when did he live?" Ahe asked.

"A very, very long time ago — more than three hundred years before the First Emperor built the Great Wall. About two thousand five hundred years ago."

Little Stone was counting pebbles. At this number, the pebbles slipped right through his fingers.

"It was a very noisy era," the old man said slowly. "War was everywhere. War chariots raced along the roads, weapons grew in the fields. The feudal lords grabbed and snatched — today this one attacked that one, tomorrow that one attacked this one. Ordinary people lived on ground that kept shifting — never knowing whether the place they slept tonight would still belong to the same country tomorrow."

"Wasn't that terrible?" Little Rabbit's ears drooped.

"It was. But here is the strange thing — the noisiest age also produced the most people who sat quietly and thought."

## Page 4

The old man drew a line on the ground.

"At that time, China was in the Eastern Zhou dynasty. And the Eastern Zhou was divided into two periods: the first called Spring and Autumn, the second called Warring States. Laozi lived at the tail end of Spring and Autumn."

Along the line he drew many small circles. "These were the feudal states of that time. Qi, Chu, Yan, Han, Zhao, Wei, Qin… dozens of them. Each one claimed it was right. Each one wanted everyone else to obey. Rituals and laws were torn this way and that, agreements were trampled underfoot. Clever people ran everywhere trying to sell their skills; ordinary people were thrown aside."

"A bit like that day we all argued?" Little Bird tilted her head on a branch.

"Yes — a bit. Except they weren't arguing about what the elephant should be called. They were arguing about who should rule everything under heaven."

## Page 5

"So what did Laozi do for a living?" Ahe asked.

"He worked as the keeper of the archives at the royal library of the Zhou court. In those days, books were nothing like the paper books we have now. They were made of bamboo slips — bamboo stalks cut into long thin strips, strung together with cord, with characters carved or written on them. Very heavy. An enormous pile of bamboo slips still could not hold very much content."

The old man picked up a small twig and drew the shape of a bamboo slip on the ground.

"Laozi's job was to look after these bamboo slips. When someone came to look something up, he helped them find it. When no one came, he sat and read. He read a great many books — histories, poems, records of the stars and weather, rites and laws, accounts of wars, legends of the ancestors — he read everything."

## Page 6

"Sitting all day reading — wasn't that boring?" Little Bird asked.

"No," the old man shook his head. "Reading was only half his work. The other half of the time, he watched what was outside the window."

"What's so interesting outside a window?"

"A tree. A little stream. Travelers passing by, horses galloping, officials quarreling, old beggars. Laozi didn't just read books — he also watched water."

"Watched water?"

"Watched water." The old man stood and went to the little stream nearby. Ahe and the others followed.

"Look at this water," the old man pointed at the stream. "It is never in a hurry, yet stones cannot stop it. Meeting a large boulder, it flows around. Coming to a cliff, it jumps straight down. Freezing in winter, flowing again in spring. It never picks a fight with anyone — yet no one can make it stop."

Ahe stared at the stream for a long time. The water flowed, *shuala-la*, saying nothing at all.

## Page 7

"Laozi watched water his whole life." The old man returned to the tree and went on: "He found that the softest thing in the world — water — can wear through the hardest stone. Throw a great rock into a river and water does not get anxious or angry. It just flows over the rock, day after day. Ten years, a hundred years, a thousand years… the rock gets worn into a smooth round pebble — and the water still flows."

"He also watched trees," Little Bird said.

"Right. A great tree starts as a tiny sprout — so soft you could pinch it in two with one finger. But it grows, slowly — one year, ten years, a hundred years — until several grown-ups linking hands cannot wrap around it. What becomes strong often begins as something very soft."

Ahe wrote one character in his notebook: Water. Then another: Tree. He suddenly felt as if these two characters could move.

## Page 8

The old man paused, lowering his voice a little.

"There is a very wondrous story about Laozi's birth — but you must remember: it is a legend, not historical fact."

"What's a legend?" Little Rabbit asked.

"A legend is an extra story added later, to make someone seem even more remarkable. Like draping a shining robe over a real person."

"Tell it! Tell it!" Little Bird bounced twice on her branch.

"All right. It is said that Laozi's mother carried him in her womb for eighty-one years."

"EIGHTY-ONE YEARS?!" everyone shouted at once. Little Stone's finger froze mid-air — he had just been counting to forty-seven.

"Yes, eighty-one years. And when he was finally born, his beard and hair were already completely white. That is why people called him 'Laozi' — 'Lao' meaning 'old' — he was like a little old grandpa the moment he was born. And because he was born beneath a plum tree — 'Li' — he took Li as his family name."

## Page 9

Little Rabbit thought about this, then burst out laughing. "A tiny baby with a white beard and white hair — that is way too funny!"

The old man laughed too. "Exactly. The story itself is quite funny. Which is why later people say: this is a legend, not history."

"But," Ahe said slowly, "the story seems to carry a truth too."

"What kind of truth?"

"Like water. Like a seed sprouting. The most powerful things — maybe they were already prepared a very, very long time ago?"

The old man looked at Ahe, and his eyes glinted. "You are beginning to understand. Legends are not necessarily true — but legends often carry within them truths that people wanted to pass on. Laozi's thought was so deep that later people, uncertain how to describe him, gave him a strange and wondrous birth story. It's as if to say: when he came, he was already carrying wisdom that had been gathering for a very long time."

## Page 10

"Did Laozi ever meet any other important people?" Little Stone asked.

"He did. The histories record one very interesting event: Confucius went to visit Laozi."

"CONFUCIUS?!" Everyone knew that name.

"Yes. The very same Master Kong who would later be honored by everyone. He was a bit younger than Laozi and already quite famous at that time. Confucius had heard that there was an exceedingly wise old gentleman in the Zhou royal library, so he made a special journey to seek his advice."

"What did they say to each other?"

"The history books record only a few lines. Confucius asked about 'rites' — the rules and customs of how people should behave. Laozi's reply was roughly this: the people who created the rites you're asking about — their bones have long since crumbled to dust. Only a few of their words remain, drifting in the wind."

## Page 11

"Wow," Ahe sucked in a breath. "Laozi was a lot more direct than our teacher."

"And Confucius didn't get angry?"

"No. After Confucius went back, he said something fascinating to his own students. He said: 'Birds — I know they can fly. Fish — I know they can swim. Beasts on the ground — I know they can run. What runs can be caught with a net. What swims can be caught with a line. What flies can be shot with an arrow. But as for the dragon — I do not know how it rides the wind and clouds into the sky. Today I met Laozi — and he is like a dragon.'"

Everyone fell quiet. Ahe imagined a dragon threading through the clouds — you could just make out a glint of scales, but never see the whole body.

"What does a dragon look like?" Little Rabbit whispered.

"No one has ever truly seen a dragon in full — just as no one can ever fit the whole of the Dao into a single name."

## Page 12

The old man paused, shifting his stick to the other hand.

"Laozi stayed many years at the library. He had read enough books. Watched enough water. Seen enough of people. He watched the Zhou court grow more chaotic by the day — princes grabbing and snatching, lords attacking and invading, rites and laws hollowed out to nothing."

"So what did he do?" Ahe asked.

"He decided to leave."

"Leave?"

"Yes. Go. No more struggling. No more holding on. It is said he mounted a blue-green ox — 'qing,' a color somewhere between gray and blue — and slowly headed west."

"Why would he go west? What was in the west?"

"No one knows. Perhaps he himself did not know. He simply wanted to get away from this place — too noisy, too chaotic — and walk toward somewhere quiet. Toward the other side of the mountains."

## Page 13

Ahe drew an ox in his notebook. Then he colored it gray-blue.

"What kind of ox was the blue-green ox? Like a water buffalo?"

"The old books are not clear. Some say a blue-green ox is a water buffalo. Others say it was an ox of an unusual color. It doesn't really matter. What matters is the image."

The old man traced a line through the air: "An old man with white hair and a white beard, riding an ox that walks at an unhurried pace, heading into the western mountains. He was not anxious, not hurried — however fast the ox walked, that was his speed. When the ox stopped to graze, he sat by the roadside and watched the clouds."

"Why ride an ox — why not a horse?"

"A horse goes too fast." The old man smiled. "Someone who is in a hurry to get away rides a horse. Someone who is not in a hurry rides an ox."

Little Stone broke in: "So — Laozi wasn't really in such a hurry to leave after all?"

"You've put your finger on it. He was not fleeing. He was simply leaving."

## Page 14

The sun began to slant westward. The tree's shadow stretched longer. Ahe looked at the old man — at his wrinkles, his white hair, his hands resting quietly on his knees.

"Teacher," he suddenly asked, "have you ever met Laozi?"

The old man laughed out loud — a soft laugh, like wind through leaves.

"Silly child. Laozi lived two thousand five hundred years ago. I cannot possibly have met him."

"Then how do you know that all these things you teach us — the water, the tree, not fighting over names — really come from him?"

The old man tapped his stick against the ground.

"That is an excellent question. And it is exactly what I will tell you about tomorrow — how those words he wrote traveled from two thousand five hundred years ago all the way to today, into my mouth, and from my mouth into your ears."

## Page 15

The evening wind came, carrying the smell of cooking smoke. Little Bird flew back to her nest in the tree. Little Rabbit stood up and brushed himself off. Little Stone arranged the pebbles on the ground into a winding path.

Ahe stayed where he was. He wrote today's date in his notebook. Then beneath it:

*Laozi — an old man who loved to watch water. His ears were very big (probably). His mother carried him for eighty-one years (this is likely a legend). He rode a blue-green ox westward. He was not fleeing — he was simply leaving.*

He paused, then added one more line:

*He was like a dragon — you could glimpse the scales, but never the whole body.*

Beyond the distant mountains, the clouds were changing color. Gray-blue, like the back of a blue-green ox.
