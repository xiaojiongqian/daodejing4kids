# 第003-1章：把闪亮的东西收一收

## 原文

### 第1页

不尚贤，使民不争；

不贵难得之货，使民不为盗；

不见可欲，使民心不乱。

### 第2页

是以圣人之治，虚其心，实其腹，弱其志，强其骨。

常使民无知无欲。使夫知者不敢为也。

为无为，则无不治。

## 释义

### 第3页

“不尚贤”不是不要有本领的人。它是说：不要把“最聪明”“最厉害”高高举起来，让大家为了名头争个不停。

真正的本领，应该用来帮事成，不是用来压人。

### 第4页

“不贵难得之货”不是讨厌好东西。它是说：不要把稀奇宝贝摆得闪闪发光，让人心里只剩想要。

欲望被天天挑动，手就容易伸向不该拿的地方。

### 第5页

“虚其心”是把心里的攀比、贪念和乱想放空一些。“实其腹”是让人吃饱、安稳、有力气生活。

“弱其志”不是没志气，而是让争强好胜的劲柔下来；“强其骨”是把身体和根基养结实。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**尚贤（shàng xián）**：把“贤能”当成招牌来崇拜。老子反对的是标榜名头，不是反对真实才能。

**争（zhēng）**：抢第一、抢名声、抢位置。心里只剩“我要赢”，就容易争。

### 第7页

**难得之货**：少见、贵重、让人想抢的东西。它也可以指权力、名声和特殊位置。

**可欲**：看见就被勾起欲望的东西。不是所有喜欢都错，错在被它牵着跑。

### 第8页

**虚其心**：让心不被杂念塞满。

**实其腹**：让基本生活安稳。

**无知无欲**：不是变笨，而是少一点机巧和贪求，守住朴素真实。

**为无为**：该做的做，不乱添自己的私心。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村要办秋日会。阿禾想挂三块大牌子：最聪明、最快、最会说话。

牌子刚写好，孩子们就围过来问：“我能不能得第一？”

### 第10页

小兔偷偷练跑步，小石头急着背故事，小鸟把嗓子喊哑了。大家越练越紧张，连饭都吃不香。

老人看了看牌子，说：“这些字太亮了，照得人心乱。”

### 第11页

阿禾又拿出一盒彩石，想摆在最高的桌子上。盒盖一开，红的像火，蓝的像夜空。

孩子们都伸长脖子，谁也听不见身边的人说话。

### 第12页

老人没有责备谁。他把彩石收进布袋，把桌子换成一张长饭桌。

桌上放着热粥、青菜和馒头。孩子们坐下来，肚子慢慢暖了，声音也慢慢轻了。

### 第13页

吃完饭，老人把木条、绳子和小锤子放在地上。没有第一名，也没有奖牌，只有一座要修的小桥。

小兔量绳，小鸟递木条，小石头敲钉。阿禾发现，没人抢名头，桥反而很快站稳了。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第三章说：不要用名声挑起争，不要用宝贝挑起贪，不要用欲望挑乱人心。

这不是把世界变空，而是把多余的火收一收。火小一点，饭才煮得熟，心也才坐得住。

### 第15页

阿禾在本子上写：让肚子饱一点，让骨头稳一点，让心里的“我要赢”少一点。

这样做事，不是没人做事；是事情自己顺了，人也安了。

# Chapter 003-1: Put the Shiny Things Away

## Original Text

### Page 1

Not to value and employ men of superior ability is the way to keep the people from rivalry among themselves; not to prize articles which are difficult to procure is the way to keep them from becoming thieves; not to show them what is likely to excite their desires is the way to keep their minds from disorder.

### Page 2

Therefore the sage, in the exercise of his government, empties their minds, fills their bellies, weakens their wills, and strengthens their bones.

He constantly (tries to) keep them without knowledge and without desire, and where there are those who have knowledge, to keep them from presuming to act (on it). When there is this abstinence from action, good order is universal.

> — Translated by James Legge (1891), *The Texts of Taoism*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 39. Public domain.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

"Not to value men of superior ability" does not mean rejecting capable people. It means not lifting "the smartest" or "the greatest" so high that everyone fights for the label.

Real ability should help the work, not press other people down.

### Page 4

"Not to prize articles difficult to procure" does not mean hating good things. It means not making rare treasures glitter so loudly that hearts can think of nothing else.

When desire is stirred every day, hands may reach toward what is not theirs.

### Page 5

"Empties their minds" means clearing away comparison, greed, and noisy schemes. "Fills their bellies" means helping people be fed, steady, and able to live.

"Weakens their wills" does not mean no courage; it softens the urge to win at any cost. "Strengthens their bones" means making life and body firm.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**value and employ men of superior ability** — Legge's phrase for 尚贤. Laozi warns against making talent into a public trophy, not against real skill.

**rivalry** — Fighting to be first, famous, or above others. When the heart only says "I must win," rivalry grows.

### Page 7

**articles difficult to procure** — Rare and costly things people may chase. They can also stand for power, rank, or special positions.

**excite their desires** — To make people want something so strongly that they forget what matters.

### Page 8

**empties their minds** — Clears the heart of clutter and restless wanting.

**fills their bellies** — Makes ordinary life steady and nourished.

**without knowledge and without desire** — Not stupidity; less scheming and less greed.

**abstinence from action** — Acting without selfish forcing.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Kongkong Village was preparing for the Autumn Gathering. Ahe wanted to hang three big signs: Smartest, Fastest, Best Speaker.

As soon as the signs were written, the children crowded around. "Can I win first place?"

### Page 10

Little Rabbit practiced running in secret, Little Stone rushed to memorize stories, and Little Bird shouted until her throat hurt. Everyone grew tense, and even dinner tasted flat.

The old man looked at the signs. "These words are too bright," he said. "They shine the heart into disorder."

### Page 11

Then Ahe brought out a box of colored stones for the highest table. When the lid opened, red flashed like fire and blue like night.

The children stretched their necks toward it. Nobody could hear the person beside them.

### Page 12

The old man blamed no one. He put the stones into a cloth bag and changed the high table into a long dinner table.

On it were warm porridge, greens, and buns. The children sat down; their bellies warmed, and their voices softened.

### Page 13

After dinner, the old man laid wood, rope, and a small hammer on the ground. No first place, no medal: only a little bridge to mend.

Little Rabbit measured rope, Little Bird passed wood, and Little Stone tapped nails. Ahe noticed that without chasing titles, the bridge stood steady very soon.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Three says: do not use fame to stir rivalry, treasures to stir greed, or desire to stir confusion.

This does not empty the world. It lowers the extra flame. With a gentler fire, rice can cook, and the heart can sit still.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: Let the belly be full, the bones be steady, and the thought "I must win" grow smaller.

Then people still work. The work simply becomes smoother, and people become more at ease.
