# 第003-2章：青牛和千里马

## 典故引入

### 第1页

传说老子离开周王室时，没有坐华丽的大车。他骑着一头青牛，慢慢走向函谷关。

青牛不耀眼，也不稀奇。可是那一路安静，正像老子说的“不贵难得之货”。

### 第2页

后来，关令尹喜请他留下话。老子才写下五千言，然后继续西去。

他留下的是一卷书，不是一串宝石；是一条路，不是一场炫耀。

### 第3页

汉文帝也有一个故事。有人献上一匹千里马，说它一天能跑很远。

文帝没有高兴地收下。他问：队伍一天只能走几十里，我一个人骑得再快，又能先到哪里去呢？

### 第4页

他把马退还，还给献马的人路费。又告诉四方：以后不要再送这种稀奇东西。

这不是不懂好马，而是不想让天下人都去追“难得之货”。

## 正文

### 第5页

空空村也来了一匹“风一样快”的小马。卖马人敲着铜铃，说谁买到它，谁就是全村最神气的人。

孩子们全挤到路边，连地上的豆苗被踩弯了也没人看见。

### 第6页

阿禾也心动了。他想象自己骑着小马冲过村口，大家都喊：“阿禾最厉害！”

这个念头一冒出来，他的心就像被铜铃拉着跑。

### 第7页

老人牵来村里的老牛。老牛走得慢，眼睛湿润，鼻子上还沾着草屑。

阿禾小声说：“它一点也不像宝贝。”老人笑了：“它昨天才把大家的米袋送回来。”

### 第8页

小马很快，却只能载一个人。老牛很慢，却能拉一车粮食。

阿禾摸摸牛背，忽然明白：有用的东西，常常不站在最高的台上。

### 第9页

卖马人又摇铜铃。小兔往前挤，小石头也往前挤，大家差点吵起来。

老人把铃声请远一些，又让孩子们去扶起豆苗。路边一下安静了。

### 第10页

傍晚，老牛拖着车，慢慢把木柴送到每家门口。没有掌声，也没有红绸。

可是每一家炉火都亮了。阿禾觉得，这比“最神气”暖多了。

### 第11页

第二天，阿禾把那盒彩石拿出来。它们还是很美，只是不再摆在最高处。

他把彩石分给大家压书角、标菜地、做路标。宝贝不被供起来，反而变得有用了。

### 第12页

老人说：“珍贵的东西可以用，但别让它牵着人心跑。”

阿禾点头。他想起青牛，也想起那匹被退回去的千里马。

## 回扣

### 第13页

“不贵难得之货”，不是把好东西都扔掉。它是提醒我们：不要把稀奇东西捧成唯一的目标。

一旦大家只看见宝贝，就容易忘记饭、路、树苗和身边的人。

### 第14页

“不尚贤”也是这样。真正有本领的人，要像青牛拉车、像老牛送柴，把事情做好。

如果只是被举到台上让人追逐，名头会变亮，事情反而会变乱。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：我不把最快的马放在心的正中央。我愿意看见慢慢走的牛，也看见每一袋米、每一束柴。

心不追着铃声跑，村子就能听见自己的脚步。

# Chapter 003-2: The Blue-Green Ox and the Thousand-Li Horse

## Allusion

### Page 1

Legend says that when Laozi left the Zhou royal court, he did not ride in a splendid carriage. He rode a blue-green ox slowly toward Hangu Pass.

The ox was not dazzling or rare. Yet that quiet road suited Laozi's teaching: do not prize difficult treasures.

### Page 2

Later, Yin Xi, Keeper of the Pass, asked Laozi to leave some words behind. Laozi wrote five thousand characters, then continued west.

He left a book, not a string of jewels; a way, not a display.

### Page 3

Emperor Wen of Han has another story. Someone offered him a horse said to travel a thousand li in a day.

The emperor did not simply rejoice. He asked: if my whole procession can only move a few dozen li, where could I go alone on the fastest horse?

### Page 4

He returned the horse and even paid the travel costs of the giver. Then he told the land not to send such rare gifts again.

He understood good horses. He simply did not want everyone chasing rare goods.

## Main Text

### Page 5

One day, a "wind-fast" pony came to Kongkong Village. The seller rang a bronze bell and said, "Whoever buys it will be the grandest person here!"

The children crowded by the road. Nobody noticed the bean sprouts being stepped flat.

### Page 6

Ahe's heart jumped too. He imagined riding through the gate while everyone shouted, "Ahe is the greatest!"

As soon as that thought appeared, his heart ran after the bell.

### Page 7

The old man led out the village ox. It walked slowly, with damp eyes and grass dust on its nose.

"It does not look like treasure," Ahe whispered. The old man smiled. "Yesterday it brought everyone's rice sacks home."

### Page 8

The pony was fast, but it could carry only one person. The ox was slow, but it could pull a cart of grain.

Ahe touched the ox's back and understood: useful things often do not stand on the highest platform.

### Page 9

The seller rang the bell again. Little Rabbit pushed forward, Little Stone pushed too, and everyone nearly argued.

The old man asked the bell to move farther away. Then he sent the children to lift the bean sprouts. The roadside grew quiet.

### Page 10

At dusk, the ox pulled a cart and delivered firewood to each door. There was no applause and no red silk.

But every stove was lit. To Ahe, that felt warmer than being called grand.

### Page 11

The next day, Ahe brought out the box of colored stones. They were still beautiful, but he no longer placed them on the highest table.

He shared them as book weights, garden markers, and little road signs. When treasure was not worshiped, it became useful.

### Page 12

The old man said, "Precious things may be used. Just do not let them pull the heart by a rope."

Ahe nodded. He thought of the blue-green ox, and of the thousand-li horse that was returned.

## Return to the Original

### Page 13

"Do not prize articles difficult to procure" does not mean throwing good things away. It means not lifting rare things into the one goal everyone must chase.

Once people see only treasure, they forget food, roads, seedlings, and the people beside them.

### Page 14

"Not to value superior ability" is similar. Real ability should work like the ox carrying grain or firewood: helping the matter become complete.

If a title is raised only for people to chase, the title grows bright and the work grows confused.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: I will not put the fastest horse at the center of my heart. I will notice the slow ox, every sack of rice, and every bundle of wood.

When the heart stops running after bells, the village can hear its own footsteps.
