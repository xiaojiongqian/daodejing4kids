# 第005-3章：中道的翅膀和安静的善

## 典故引入

### 第1页

希腊神话里，代达罗斯做了两副蜡和羽毛的翅膀，带儿子伊卡洛斯逃离迷宫。

起飞前，他叮嘱孩子：走中间的路。

### 第2页

飞太低，海水会打湿羽毛；飞太高，太阳会融化蜡。

中间不是平庸，而是让翅膀活下来的分寸。

### 第3页

伊卡洛斯起初听从父亲。后来他被飞翔迷住，越飞越高，像要把太阳抓在手里。

蜡化了，羽毛散开，他从高空坠入海中。

### 第4页

“守中”不是胆小。它是知道力量要走在哪条路上。

孩子的兴奋若没有中心，就会把自由变成坠落。

### 第5页

古印度的悉达多也走过极端。他曾是王子，享受过宫殿里的安乐。

后来他离开宫殿，又把自己逼进很苦很苦的苦行。

### 第6页

吃得太少，身体虚弱到心也昏沉。享乐不能给答案，折磨自己也不能给答案。

他终于明白：琴弦太松没有声，太紧会断。

### 第7页

在菩提树下，他不再追赶什么，也不再逃开什么。

他静静看呼吸，看念头来去，让心像风箱一样空而能应。

### 第8页

后来，人们把这条路叫作中道。它不是两边各取一点的凑合。

它是离开执著，让心回到能看清的地方。

### 第9页

特蕾莎修女在加尔各答照顾被遗弃的人。有人感激她，也有人怀疑她，贫病却每天都在眼前。

她没有先问：这个人值不值得？也没有先证明自己多么善良。

### 第10页

她给人洗伤口，喂水，握住临终者的手。她做的是很小的事，却一天一天做了很多年。

这不是拿仁慈做招牌，而是不让“我很仁慈”塞满心；心中有空，眼前的苦才被看见。

### 第11页

老子说“圣人不仁”，并不是不要慈悲。它提醒我们，别把慈悲变成偏私、表演或口号。

真正的善守中：不被称赞拉高，也不被怀疑拉低，只在该照顾处照顾。

### 第12页

她有时把路边快死的人抱回临终之家。那人身上有伤，有气味，许多人绕开走。

她没有先问这是不是伟大事业，只先让这个人被洗净、被盖上布、被当作人。

### 第13页

这不等于天地偏爱某一个人。正因为她不把善变成口号，心里才有空看见眼前这一位。

圣人不仁，是不被“我要证明我仁慈”牵走；守中，所以不被赞美和怀疑耗干。

## 回扣

### 第14页

伊卡洛斯提醒我们，太高太急会失去中心。悉达多提醒我们，享乐和苦行都可能让心偏到一边。

特蕾莎提醒我们：真正的善不把自己说满，而是在中心上做眼前该做的事。

### 第15页

第五章里的“中”，不是一条死线。它像飞行时的高度，像琴弦的张力，也像帮助别人时不被名声和情绪拉偏的手。

它让力量不偏、不炫、不枯竭。

### 第16页

当心守住中间，话会少而清，行动会轻而准。

天地像风箱，空处生风。人若留得住空，就不被偏私塞住，也能在复杂世界里生出路。

# Chapter 005-3: Wings of the Middle Way and Quiet Goodness

## Allusion

### Page 1

In Greek myth, Daedalus made two pairs of wings from wax and feathers. With them, he and his son Icarus could escape the maze.

Before they flew, he warned the boy: take the middle path.

### Page 2

Fly too low, and sea spray will wet the feathers. Fly too high, and the sun will melt the wax.

The middle is not dull. It is the measure that lets the wings survive.

### Page 3

At first, Icarus followed his father. Then flight delighted him, and he rose higher and higher, as if he could seize the sun.

The wax softened, the feathers scattered, and he fell from the sky into the sea.

### Page 4

"Keeping the center" is not fearfulness. It is knowing where strength can safely travel.

When excitement has no center, freedom can turn into falling.

### Page 5

In ancient India, Siddhartha also walked through extremes. He had been a prince and had known palace comfort.

Then he left the palace and pushed himself into severe hardship.

### Page 6

When he ate too little, his body weakened and his mind grew dim. Pleasure did not give the answer. Hurting himself did not give the answer either.

He came to understand: if a string is too loose, it makes no sound; if it is too tight, it breaks.

### Page 7

Under the Bodhi tree, he stopped chasing and stopped running away.

He quietly watched his breath and watched thoughts come and go. His heart became like a bellows: empty, ready, and responsive.

### Page 8

Later, people called this the Middle Way. It is not a compromise that takes a little from each side.

It is leaving attachment, so the heart can return to a place that sees clearly.

### Page 9

Mother Teresa cared for abandoned people in Kolkata. Some thanked her, and some doubted her, but sickness and loneliness were before her every day.

She did not first ask, "Is this person worth helping?" She also did not first prove how good she was.

### Page 10

She washed wounds, gave water, and held the hands of people near death. These were small acts, done day after day for many years.

This was not kindness used as a signboard. It did not let "I am kind" fill the heart; because there was space inside, the suffering before her could be seen.

### Page 11

When Laozi says, "The sage is not benevolent," he does not reject compassion. He warns us not to turn compassion into favoritism, performance, or slogans.

True goodness keeps the center: not lifted by praise, not lowered by doubt, caring where care is needed.

### Page 12

Sometimes she carried a dying person from the roadside into a home for the dying. The body had wounds and smell, and many people walked around it.

She did not first ask whether this was a great mission. She first made sure this person was washed, covered, and treated as human.

### Page 13

This does not mean heaven and earth favor one person. Because she did not turn goodness into a slogan, there was room in the heart to see this person before her.

The sage is "not benevolent" in this sense: not pulled by "I must prove I am kind." Keeping the center, the heart is not drained by praise or doubt.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Icarus reminds us that too high and too fast can lose the center. Siddhartha reminds us that pleasure and self-torment can both pull the heart to one side.

Mother Teresa reminds us: true goodness does not fill itself with talk; it does the present thing from the center.

### Page 15

The "center" in Chapter Five is not a dead line. It is like the right height in flight, the right tension in a string, and a helping hand not pulled away by fame or emotion.

It keeps strength from becoming partial, showy, or exhausted.

### Page 16

When the heart keeps the center, words become fewer and clearer, and actions become lighter and more exact.

Heaven and earth are like a bellows: wind is born in the hollow. If people can keep some hollow space, they will not be blocked by favoritism, and a path can open in a complicated world.
