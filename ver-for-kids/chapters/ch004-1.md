# 第004-1章：不满的井

## 原文

### 第1页

道冲而用之或不盈，渊兮似万物之宗；

挫其锐，解其纷，和其光，同其尘，湛兮似或存。

吾不知谁之子，象帝之先。

## 释义

### 第2页

“冲”不是普通的空洞。它是空着、通着，所以能装、能过、能生出用处。

老子说道像一个深深的器物，正因为没有被塞满，万物才有路从里面来来去去。

### 第3页

“渊兮似万物之宗”，是说道深得像不见底的水。万物好像都从那里来，又靠它继续生长。

它不站出来说“我是源头”。它只是深深地在，像井水在黑暗里不声不响。

### 第4页

“挫其锐”，是把太尖、太冲的劲收柔一点；“解其纷”，是把缠在一起的结慢慢松开。

“和其光”，不是把光熄灭，而是让光不刺眼；“同其尘”，是愿意走进普通生活里，不把自己摆得高高的。

### 第5页

最后老子说：我不知道道是谁的孩子，它好像在天帝之前就已经在了。

这不是让我们猜一个答案，而是提醒我们：道不像桌子、石头那样能被抓在手里。它更像让一切出现的深处。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**冲（chōng）**：空而通，不满不塞。像井口空着，水才能被打上来。

**不盈（bù yíng）**：不满出来，不把自己塞到没有空处。

**渊（yuān）**：很深的水。这里形容道深远难尽。

### 第7页

**宗（zōng）**：根本、祖先、源头。

**挫锐（cuò ruì）**：把尖锐的锋芒磨柔一点。不是没力量，而是不刺伤别人。

**解纷（jiě fēn）**：解开混乱和纠缠，像慢慢松开打结的线。

### 第8页

**和光（hé guāng）**：让自己的光柔和一些，不用亮光逼人。

**同尘（tóng chén）**：和普通人、普通生活在一起，不把自己摆得很高。

**湛（zhàn）**：清深、安静，好像一直在那里。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村有一口老井。井沿不高，井口黑黑的，低头望进去，好像什么也没有。

小兔问：“里面空空的，为什么大家每天还来打水？”

### 第10页

老人把木桶慢慢放下去，绳子吱呀吱呀响。过了一会儿，桶上来了，清水在里面晃着光。

阿禾趴在井沿上看，忽然明白：井口看起来空，正因为空，水才能被打上来。

### 第11页

小石头搬来几块石头，想把井填满一点。“这样它看起来就有东西了。”

老人拦住他：“填满了，它就不再是井了。有些用处，正是靠不满才留下来的。”

### 第12页

后来，孩子们抢着拉水桶。你往左，他往右，绳子很快打成一个硬结。

老人没有猛拽。他把结放在膝上，一圈一圈挑开。阿禾看着看着，发现越急，结越紧；手稳下来，线头才肯露出来。

### 第13页

太阳快落山时，井水照出一片柔柔的光。光不刺眼，尘土在光里慢慢飘。

阿禾觉得，道也许就像这口井：深深的，静静的，不说自己有多重要，却每天给村子水。

## 回扣

### 第14页

回看第四章：道“冲而不盈”，不是空无一物，而是不把自己塞死，所以用不尽。

“渊深如宗”，是说万物有看不见的根；“挫锐解纷”，是说真正的力量先减少伤害，再慢慢解开混乱。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：井不满，水才能来；手不急，结才能松；光不刺眼，尘土也能被照见。

道像深处的空，不占满世界，却让世界有路、有水、有光。

# Chapter 004-1: The Well That Is Not Full

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Tao is (like) the emptiness of a vessel; and in our employment of it we must be on our guard against all fulness. How deep and unfathomable it is, as if it were the Honoured Ancestor of all things!

### Page 2

We should blunt our sharp points, and unravel the complications of things; we should attemper our brightness, and bring ourselves into agreement with the obscurity of others. How pure and still the Tao is, as if it would ever so continue!

I do not know whose son it is. It might appear to have been before God.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

"Chong" is not ordinary emptiness. It is open and passable, so it can hold, let things through, and give rise to use.

Laozi says Dao is like a deep vessel. Because it is not stuffed full, the ten thousand things have room to come and go.

### Page 4

"Deep, as if the ancestor of all things" means Dao is like water with no visible bottom. The ten thousand things seem to come from it and keep growing through it.

It does not stand up and announce, "I am the source." It is simply, deeply there, like well water quiet in the dark.

### Page 5

"Blunt sharp points" means softening the urge to stab forward. "Unravel complications" means slowly loosening tangled knots.

"Temper brightness" does not put out the light; it makes light gentle. "Join the dust" means entering ordinary life instead of placing oneself high above it.

Laozi finally says he does not know whose child Dao is. This is not a riddle with one answer. It reminds us that Dao is not a table or stone we can hold; it is more like the depth that lets all things appear.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**emptiness of a vessel** — Legge's image for 道冲. An open hollow can hold and give.

**fulness** — Being so packed that nothing more can move. Too much fullness spills over.

**unfathomable** — Too deep to measure.

### Page 7

**Honoured Ancestor** — Legge's rendering for 万物之宗: the deep source or root of all things.

**blunt our sharp points** — Soften sharpness. Not weakness, but strength that does not wound.

**unravel the complications** — Loosen tangles, like a knot in a string.

### Page 8

**attemper our brightness** — Make one's light gentle and balanced.

**obscurity of others** — Legge's way of saying 同尘: living among ordinary dust and ordinary people.

**pure and still** — Clear, deep, and quiet, as if always there.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Kongkong Village had an old well. Its stone rim was low, and when you looked down into its dark mouth, it seemed to hold nothing.

Little Rabbit asked, "If it is empty inside, why does everyone come here every day?"

### Page 10

The old man lowered a wooden bucket, and the rope creaked. After a while, the bucket came up with clear water rocking in the light.

Ahe leaned over the rim and suddenly understood: the well seemed empty, and because it was empty, water could be drawn up.

### Page 11

Little Stone carried over several stones. He wanted to fill the well a little. "Then it will look as if something is inside."

The old man stopped him. "If you fill it, it will no longer be a well. Some uses remain because a thing is not full."

### Page 12

Later, the children argued over the water bucket. One pulled left, another pulled right, and soon the rope tightened into a hard knot.

The old man did not yank. He laid the knot on his knee and opened it loop by loop. Ahe watched and saw that the faster one pulls, the tighter the knot gets; when the hands grow steady, the loose end appears.

### Page 13

At sunset, the well water held a soft light. The light did not sting the eyes, and dust drifted slowly inside it.

Ahe felt that Dao might be like this well: deep, quiet, never saying how important it is, yet giving water to the village every day.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Looking back at Chapter Four: Dao is open and not full. This does not mean blank nothingness; it means not being stuffed shut, so its use does not run out.

"Deep as a root" means the ten thousand things have an unseen source. To blunt sharpness and untie tangles means true strength first reduces harm, then slowly opens confusion.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: A well is not full, so water can come. Hands do not hurry, so knots can loosen. Light does not sting, so even dust can be seen.

Dao is like depth with room inside. It does not fill the world with itself, yet it lets the world have roads, water, and light.
