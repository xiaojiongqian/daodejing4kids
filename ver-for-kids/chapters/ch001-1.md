# 第001-1章：名字不是全部

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道，名可名，非常名；

无名，天地之始，有名，万物之母。

### 第2页

故常无欲，以观其妙，常有欲，以观其徼；

此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄。玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第3页

“道可道，非常道”，不是叫我们闭上嘴，也不是说话没有用。

老子像是轻轻提醒：说得出来的路，多半已经被人走过、指过、讲过。真正的大道，比一句话更早，也比一句话更宽。

### 第4页

名字也很有用。我们叫“树”，就知道该往树下找阴凉；我们叫“水”，就知道可以洗手、浇菜、解渴。

可是树不会只剩一个“树”字。它还在吸水、长叶、落影子；水也不会只剩一个“水”字，它还在流、在亮、在变凉。

### 第5页

心里不急着抓答案，像把手掌松开一点，就容易看见细小的奥妙。

心里有愿望，也不一定坏。想喝水时，我们会看见杯口、河岸和水井的边。一个看见深处，一个看见边界；两边合起来，才更接近真实。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**道（dào）**：像路，又不只是路。它是万物能生长、变化、各归其所的深深根源。

**可道（kě dào）**：可以说出来、指给别人看的道理或道路。

**常道（cháng dào）**：一直如此、不会被一句话装完的大道。

### 第7页

**名（míng）**：给事物的叫法，像贴在门上的小牌子。

**无名（wú míng）**：还没有被分开、还没有被叫出名字的深处。

**有名（yǒu míng）**：事物显出形状以后，我们能分辨、能呼唤、能照看它。

### 第8页

**欲（yù）**：心里想要、想抓住、想马上知道答案的劲。

**妙（miào）**：很细很深的奥妙，像种子里还没展开的春天。

**徼（jiào）**：边界、边缘。像一条河的岸，让我们知道水流到哪里。

**玄（xuán）**：深远得一下子看不透，不是吓人的黑，而是越看越深。

## 正文

### 第9页

阿禾有一本“名字本”。新本子，蓝布封面，纸页还带着一点草香。

他想把空空村一样一样写进去。井、石桥、竹篱笆、豆苗、云。写完一行，他就在旁边画一个小方框，像把那样东西收好了。

### 第10页

风来了。竹叶沙沙响，晾衣绳轻轻一跳，阿禾赶紧写下“风”。

他把纸条系在风筝线上。纸条啪啦啪啦响了一阵，忽然被风卷走，翻过篱笆，落到溪水上，又顺着水弯走了。

### 第11页

阿禾又给一粒种子插了小牌：“种子”。

第二天早晨，泥土裂开，一点嫩绿探出来。小牌还端端正正站着，可牌子旁边的东西，已经不是昨天那颗圆圆的小种子了。

### 第12页

阿禾有点着急：“那名字是不是没用了？”

老人把一块写着“门”的小木牌挂到院门上，说：“门牌有用。找院子的人，看见它就知道到了。可是门牌不能替你闻见饭香，也不能替你看见院里的树。”

### 第13页

老人请阿禾把名字本合上，在溪边坐一会儿。

一开始，阿禾老想问：“这叫什么？那叫什么？”后来他慢慢不问了。他听见水碰石头，听见小虫落在叶尖，看见一小片阳光在水底晃来晃去。

### 第14页

太阳偏西时，阿禾又打开名字本。这一次，他写得慢多了。

写“豆苗”时，他顺手给豆苗浇了水。写“小溪”时，他看看有没有泥堵住水口。名字不再像小盒子，倒像一只提醒他照看的小手。

## 回扣

### 第15页

第一章说“名可名，非常名”。阿禾这才明白：名字能带我们走近世界，却不能替世界活着。

只抓着名字，就只看见纸条；把纸条放松一点，风还会吹，水还会流，嫩芽还会往上长。

### 第16页

“无名”和“有名”都重要。没有名字以前，万物已经在悄悄开始；有了名字以后，我们才学会分辨，也学会照看。

阿禾在本子上写：名字是门牌，不是整个院子。说得出的路，带我开始走；说不尽的道，还在前面。

# Chapter 001-1: Names Are Not Everything

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Tao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao. The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name.

(Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth; (conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

### Page 2

Always without desire we must be found,
If its deep mystery we would sound;
But if desire always within us be,
Its outer fringe is all that we shall see.

Under these two aspects, it is really the same; but as development takes place, it receives the different names. Together we call them the Mystery. Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

"The Tao that can be trodden" is not telling us to stop speaking. Words can help.

Laozi is gently saying: a path we can point to and fully explain is already a path people have walked and named. The living Dao is earlier than our words, and wider than them too.

### Page 4

Names are useful. When we say "tree," we know where to look for shade. When we say "water," we know what can wash hands, water beans, and cool a thirsty mouth.

But a tree is not only the word "tree." It is drinking, leafing, and casting shade. Water is not only the word "water." It is flowing, shining, and turning cool.

### Page 5

When the heart does not rush to grab an answer, it opens like a loosened hand. Then small wonders can be seen.

Having wishes is not always wrong. If we are thirsty, we notice the cup rim, the riverbank, and the well's edge. One way of seeing notices depth; another notices edges. Together, they come closer to what is real.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**Tao** — A way, but more than a road. It is the deep source by which things grow, change, and find their place.

**trodden** — Walked on, like a path under your feet.

**enduring and unchanging** — Lasting and not easily changed by our words or labels.

### Page 7

**name** — A word we use to call something, like a small sign on a door.

**Originator** — The one from which something begins.

**Mother** — Here, the source that nourishes and brings things into form.

### Page 8

**desire** — The pull of wanting, needing, or trying to grab an answer too quickly.

**deep mystery** — A wonder so quiet and deep that it cannot be seen all at once.

**outer fringe** — The edge of something, like the riverbank rather than the whole river.

**subtle and wonderful** — Fine, hidden, and full of quiet wonder.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Ahe had a "name notebook." It was new, with a blue cloth cover and pages that still smelled faintly of grass.

He wanted to write Kongkong Village into it, one thing at a time: well, stone bridge, bamboo fence, bean sprout, cloud. After each name, he drew a little box, as if the thing had been neatly put away.

### Page 10

The wind came. Bamboo leaves rustled, the clothesline jumped, and Ahe quickly wrote "wind."

He tied the paper to his kite string. It rattled for a while, then the wind rolled it over the fence and onto the stream, where it turned once and drifted away.

### Page 11

Then Ahe put a small sign beside a seed: "seed."

The next morning, the soil split and a point of green looked out. The sign still stood straight, but the thing beside it was no longer yesterday's round little seed.

### Page 12

Ahe grew worried. "Are names useless, then?"

The old man hung a small sign that said "gate" on the yard gate. "A gate sign is useful. A visitor sees it and knows he has arrived. But the sign cannot smell the rice for him, or see the tree in the yard."

### Page 13

The old man asked Ahe to close his notebook and sit by the stream.

At first Ahe kept wanting to ask, "What is this called? What is that called?" After a while, he stopped asking. He heard water touch stone, heard a tiny insect land on a leaf tip, and saw a patch of sunlight shaking on the streambed.

### Page 14

When the sun leaned west, Ahe opened his notebook again. This time, he wrote slowly.

When he wrote "bean sprout," he watered it. When he wrote "stream," he checked whether mud had blocked the flow. Names no longer felt like little boxes. They felt like small hands reminding him to care.

## Return to the Original

### Page 15

Chapter One says, "The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name." Ahe begins to understand: names bring us near the world, but they cannot live in the world's place.

If we clutch only the name, we see only the paper label. If we loosen our grip, the wind still blows, the water still flows, and the sprout keeps rising.

### Page 16

"No name" and "having a name" both matter. Before names, things are already quietly beginning. With names, we learn to tell things apart and care for them.

Ahe wrote in his notebook: A name is the sign on the gate, not the whole yard. The speakable path helps me begin; the unspeakable Dao is still ahead.
