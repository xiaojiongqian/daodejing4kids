# 第005-2章：冰原、飞船和手心里的水

## 典故引入

### 第1页

杰克·伦敦写过《生火》。一个淘金客带着狗走进育空荒野，冷得像整片天地都结了冰。

他觉得自己够聪明，不必听老人的劝告：这种天气，不要独自上路。

### 第2页

雪地没有骂他，也没有惩罚他。雪只是冷，河冰只是薄。

他一脚踏破冰，靴子湿了，才明白自然不会为了人的面子改变温度。

### 第3页

他必须马上生火。可是手指冻僵，火柴掉落；火好不容易燃起，又被树枝上震落的雪扑灭。

最后，他倒在雪地里。狗等了一会儿，转身向有火光和食物的人群走去。天地“不仁”，不是故意害他，只是按自己的规律运行。

### 第4页

如果他早一点守中，听一句劝，少一点逞强，路也许不会走到绝处。

在自然面前，聪明不是硬撑到底，而是知道边界在哪里。

### 第5页

1970年，阿波罗13号去月球途中，服务舱里的氧气罐爆炸。飞船漏氧，电力下降，离地球还很远。

如果恐慌塞满控制室，大家只会互相喊叫，办法就没有地方进来。

### 第6页

休斯顿的工程师把问题拆开：还剩多少电？多少氧？二氧化碳怎样过滤？哪些材料宇航员手边真的有？

一句一句问清楚，心中间就空出一点位置。

### 第7页

他们把胶带、纸板、塑料袋和零件摆在桌上，想办法把方形滤芯接到圆形接口上。

像风箱一样，中间要有空，风才有路；人也要留出一点安静，办法才会转弯。

### 第8页

宇航员在狭小的登月舱里熬了许久。登月舱原本不是给三个人住这么久的，现在却成了救生艇。

冷、困、害怕都在旁边推他们。他们不能被这些推走，只能把下一步算清楚，再做下一步。

### 第9页

最后，飞船回到地球。那次任务没有登上月球，却把“守中”的力量带了回来。

危急时，最重要的往往不是声音最大的人，而是谁还能稳住中心。

### 第10页

海伦·凯勒小时候看不见，也听不见。她像被关在没有窗的房间里，急得常常发怒。

安妮老师没有用一大堆道理压住她，只是一遍遍在她手心写字。

### 第11页

有一天，水泵的清水流过海伦的手。安妮在她另一只手心一笔一画拼写“水”。

那一刻，符号和世界接上了。海伦立刻想知道水泵、地面、老师的名字，一整片天地被打开。

### 第12页

这就是“多言数穷”的反面。不是永远不说，而是不把话说成噪声。

好话像清水，要流到孩子正在醒来的地方。一句话说中了，比十句解释更有力量。

## 回扣

### 第13页

冰原教人：天地不按人的骄傲改变温度。飞船教人：心若被恐慌塞满，办法就进不来。

手心里的水教人：一句话若落在中心，比许多解释更能打开世界。

### 第14页

第五章的风箱，不只是古代工具。它也是一种练心的方法：中间要空，来回要有节奏。

别把心塞满判断、恐惧和解释，风才进得来，办法也才出得去。

### 第15页

天地不偏私，所以我们要学会看清规律，不把愿望当规律。圣人不标榜，所以我们要少说空话，多做实事。

空不是逃避，守中也不是发呆；它是让心不被一边的情绪拖走。

### 第16页

它给生命留出路：该低头时低头，该行动时行动，该说一字时不说十句。

风从空处来。路也常从安静的中心打开。

# Chapter 005-2: The Ice Field, the Spacecraft, and Water in the Palm

## Allusion

### Page 1

Jack London wrote a story called "To Build a Fire." A prospector walked into the Yukon wilderness with a dog, where the cold seemed to freeze heaven and earth.

He thought he was clever enough. He did not need the old man's warning: in this weather, do not travel alone.

### Page 2

The snow did not scold him or punish him. Snow was simply cold, and river ice was simply thin.

When one foot broke through and his boot was soaked, he learned that nature does not change temperature for human pride.

### Page 3

He had to build a fire at once. But his fingers froze, the matches dropped, and when a fire finally started, snow shaken from the branches smothered it.

At last, he fell in the snow. The dog waited for a while, then turned toward people, fire, and food. Heaven and earth were not trying to hurt him; they were following their own patterns.

### Page 4

If he had kept the center earlier, listened to one warning, and shown less bravado, the road might not have ended there.

Before nature, intelligence is not stubbornness to the end. It is knowing where the boundary is.

### Page 5

In 1970, an oxygen tank in Apollo 13's service module exploded on the way to the moon. Oxygen leaked, power fell, and Earth was far away.

If panic had filled the control room, people would only have shouted over one another, and no path could enter.

### Page 6

The engineers in Houston divided the problem: How much power was left? How much oxygen? How could carbon dioxide be filtered? What materials did the astronauts truly have?

Question by question, a little space opened in the center of the mind.

### Page 7

They laid tape, cardboard, plastic bags, and spare parts on a table. They had to connect a square filter to a round opening.

Like a bellows, there had to be room in the middle for air to find a path. People, too, need a little quiet before a solution can turn a corner.

### Page 8

The astronauts endured many hours in the cramped lunar module. It was not built for three people to live in that long; now it had become a lifeboat.

Cold, fatigue, and fear pressed beside them. They could not let these things push them away; they had to calculate one clear step, then take the next.

### Page 9

At last, the spacecraft returned to Earth. The mission did not land on the moon, but it brought back the power of keeping the center.

In a crisis, the loudest voice is often not the most important. The one who can stay centered matters most.

### Page 10

When Helen Keller was little, she could not see or hear. She lived as if inside a room with no windows, and she often raged in frustration.

Her teacher, Annie Sullivan, did not press her down with many explanations. She spelled into Helen's palm again and again.

### Page 11

One day, water from a pump ran over Helen's hand. Annie spelled "water" stroke by stroke into Helen's other palm.

At that moment, signs joined the world. Helen immediately wanted the names of the pump, the ground, and her teacher. A whole world opened.

### Page 12

This is the opposite of "too many words run dry." It does not mean never speak. It means do not turn speech into noise.

Good words are like clear water. They must flow to the place where a child is waking. One right word can be stronger than ten explanations.

## Return to the Original

### Page 13

The ice field teaches this: heaven and earth do not change temperature for human pride. The spacecraft teaches this: if panic fills the heart, no solution can enter.

Water in the palm teaches this: one word that reaches the center can open more of the world than many explanations.

### Page 14

The bellows in Chapter Five is not only an ancient tool. It is also a way to train the heart: the middle must stay open, and movement must keep rhythm.

Do not stuff the heart with judgment, fear, and explanation. Then wind can enter, and a way can leave.

### Page 15

Heaven and earth are not partial, so we learn to see patterns clearly and not mistake wishes for patterns. The sage does not display kindness, so we learn to speak less empty talk and do more real work.

Emptiness is not escape. Keeping the center is not daydreaming; it keeps the heart from being dragged away by one-sided emotion.

### Page 16

It leaves a path for life: bow when it is time to bow, act when it is time to act, and speak one word when one word is enough.

Wind comes through empty space. A road often opens from the quiet center.
