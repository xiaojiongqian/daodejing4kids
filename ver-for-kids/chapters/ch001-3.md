# 第001-3章：不同名字，同一口泉

## 典故引入

### 第1页

很久以后，中国人讲过一个传说：老子在东方讲“道”，古印度的佛陀讲“法”，古希腊的苏格拉底追问“善”和“真”。

有些故事还把他们的出生说得很神奇。这样的说法，不能当成确凿历史；它更像一盏灯，照出人们心里的愿望。

### 第2页

人们为什么喜欢讲这样的传说呢？也许是因为，在很远很远的地方，不同的人都问过相近的问题。

世界从哪里来？人该怎样活？心怎样才不被贪、怕和争吵牵着走？他们用的字不一样，问的却都是很深的事。

### 第3页

老子说“道”，佛陀说“法”，苏格拉底一遍遍追问“什么是真正的好”。这并不表示三个人讲的是同一套话。

可是这些名字，都像手指，指向比日常小事更深的地方。手指不同，方向却可能相近。

### 第4页

所以读“同出而异名”时，不必急着把所有名字揉成一个名字，也不必急着说“只有我的名字才对”。

更好的办法，是先听一听：别人从哪里出发，为什么这样叫，又看见了什么。

## 正文

### 第5页

空空村有三条去泉边的小路。小兔家走竹影路，小鸟家走石阶路，小石头家走麦田路。

平时大家各走各的，倒也相安无事。直到有一天，小石头说：“我家的路最正，别的路都是绕路。”

### 第6页

小兔不服气：“竹影路才好，夏天一点也不晒。”

小鸟也急了：“石阶路最清楚，下雨天脚不会陷进泥里。”三个人你一句我一句，连水桶都忘在了井边。

### 第7页

阿禾拿出名字本，想写一块最大的牌子，把三条路全改成“去泉路”。

老人看了看他，又看了看三个孩子，说：“明天清早，你们各走自己的路，到泉边见。先别急着改名字。”

### 第8页

第二天，小兔从竹影里走来，鞋面沾着露水。她说，竹叶一路替她挡太阳。

小鸟沿石阶上来，听见石缝里的小水声。小石头穿过麦田，袖口带着麦芒香。三个人在泉边碰头，谁也没有走错。

### 第9页

阿禾说：“既然都到了同一口泉，那就改成同一个名字吧。”

老人摇摇头：“名字不是用来争高低的，也不是用来把差别抹平的。住在竹林边的人，需要竹影路；住在麦田边的人，需要麦田路。”

### 第10页

他们低头看泉水。泉眼很小，藏在两块石头中间，水却不停往外冒。

它流进小渠，浇过豆苗，进了水缸，又被舀进茶壶。人们叫它泉、溪、水、茶、汤。名字一路在变，清凉还是从那一处来。

### 第11页

阿禾在泉边搭了一扇小木门。门上挂三块牌：竹影路、石阶路、麦田路。

门中间空着，谁都可以走过去。小兔摸摸门框，说：“原来门不是拦人的。”小鸟接着说：“门是告诉人，从这里可以进去。”

### 第12页

后来，村里人都喜欢从那扇门经过。有人挑水，有人洗笔，有人坐在石头上听水声。

同一口泉，给每个人的用处不一样，给每个人的安静也不一样。它不争辩，也不要求大家只用一个名字叫它。

### 第13页

傍晚，阿禾问：“那‘玄之又玄’是不是说，泉水后面还有更深的地方？”

老人望着泉眼说：“你看见水从石缝冒出来，却看不见它在山腹里走过的暗路。知道还有看不见的深处，心就不会太满。”

### 第14页

阿禾在名字本最后留了一页空白。他没有写“我全懂了”。

他只画了一扇没有关上的门。门后面有一点水声，还有一条没有画完的小路。

## 回扣

### 第15页

第一章说“同出而异名”，不是叫我们把所有名字都抹掉。

名字不同，常常因为人站的位置不同，走来的路不同，要照看的事情也不同。重要的是别把自己的路牌，当成唯一的泉眼。

### 第16页

“玄之又玄，众妙之门”，像阿禾搭的那扇门。门上可以有牌子，门中间却要空着，人才能走进去。

语言把我们带到门口。再往里走，就要靠安静、观察，也靠一点谦卑。

### 第17页

阿禾写下：同一口泉，可以有许多条路；同一个深处，可以有许多名字。

我会记住自己的路，也给别人的路留一点位置。门开着，水声还在里面。

# Chapter 001-3: Different Names, One Spring

## Allusion

### Page 1

Long afterward, people in China told a legend: Laozi in the East spoke of Dao, the Buddha in ancient India spoke of Dharma, and Socrates in ancient Greece asked about goodness and truth.

Some stories even describe their births in wondrous ways. We should not treat that as certain history. It is more like a lamp, showing what people wished to understand.

### Page 2

Why did people like telling such a story? Perhaps because, in faraway places, different people asked similar deep questions.

Where does the world come from? How should a person live? How can the heart avoid being dragged around by greed, fear, and quarrels? Their words were different, but their questions went deep.

### Page 3

Laozi said "Dao." The Buddha spoke of "Dharma." Socrates kept asking what the truly good was. This does not mean the three taught the same lesson.

But these names are like fingers pointing beyond ordinary daily concerns. The fingers differ, yet they may point in a nearby direction.

### Page 4

So when we read "different names," we do not have to squeeze all names into one name. We also do not have to say, "Only my name is right."

A better way is to listen first: where did this person begin, why did he use this word, and what did he see?

## Main Text

### Page 5

Kongkong Village had three paths to the spring. Little Rabbit's family used Bamboo Shadow Path, Little Bird's family used Stone Step Path, and Little Stone's family used Wheat Field Path.

Most days, everyone simply walked from home. Then one day Little Stone said, "My family's path is the proper one. The others are just detours."

### Page 6

Little Rabbit would not agree. "Bamboo Shadow Path is best. It keeps the sun off you in summer."

Little Bird grew anxious too. "Stone Step Path is clearest. Your feet do not sink into mud when it rains." The three argued so hard that they forgot their water buckets by the well.

### Page 7

Ahe took out his name notebook. He wanted to make one large sign and rename all three paths "Spring Path."

The old man looked at him, then at the three children. "Tomorrow morning, each of you walk your own path and meet at the spring. Do not rename anything yet."

### Page 8

The next morning, Little Rabbit came through bamboo shadows with dew on her shoes. She said the leaves had shaded her all the way.

Little Bird came up the stone steps and heard tiny water sounds between the stones. Little Stone crossed the wheat field with the scent of grain on his sleeves. They met at the spring. None of them had gone wrong.

### Page 9

"Since they all arrive at one spring," Ahe said, "should we change them to one name?"

The old man shook his head. "Names are not for ranking paths, and they are not for wiping away differences. People near the bamboo need Bamboo Shadow Path. People near the wheat field need Wheat Field Path."

### Page 10

They bent down to look at the spring. The spring eye was small, hidden between two stones, but water kept coming.

It ran into a little ditch, watered bean sprouts, entered the water jar, and was ladled into a teapot. People called it spring, stream, water, tea, and soup. The names changed along the way, but the coolness came from one place.

### Page 11

Ahe built a small wooden gate beside the spring. On it he hung three signs: Bamboo Shadow Path, Stone Step Path, Wheat Field Path.

The middle of the gate was open, and anyone could pass through. Little Rabbit touched the frame. "So a gate is not only for stopping people." Little Bird added, "It can show where to enter."

### Page 12

After that, villagers liked passing through the gate. Some came to carry water, some to wash brushes, and some sat on the stones and listened.

The same spring gave different uses to different people, and a different quietness too. It did not argue, and it did not ask everyone to use only one name.

### Page 13

At dusk, Ahe asked, "Does 'the Mystery is the deepest' mean there is something deeper behind the spring?"

The old man looked at the spring eye. "You see the water coming out between stones, but you cannot see the hidden path it took inside the mountain. When the heart knows there is unseen depth, it does not become too full."

### Page 14

Ahe left the last page of his notebook blank. He did not write, "I understand everything."

He drew only a gate that had not been closed. Behind it were a little sound of water and a path not yet finished.

## Return to the Original

### Page 15

When Chapter One says "different names," it is not asking us to erase all names.

Names differ because people stand in different places, travel by different paths, and care for different things. What matters is not mistaking your own path sign for the only spring.

### Page 16

"Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful." It is like Ahe's gate. A gate may have signs on it, but its middle must stay open for people to pass through.

Language brings us to the gate. To walk farther in, we need quietness, observation, and a little humility.

### Page 17

Ahe wrote: One spring can have many paths. One depth can have many names.

I will remember my own path, and I will leave room for another person's path too. The gate is open, and the sound of water is still inside.
