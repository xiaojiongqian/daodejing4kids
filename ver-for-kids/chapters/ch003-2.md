# 第003-2章：青牛和千里马

## 典故引入

### 第1页

传说老子离开周王室时，没有坐华丽的大车。他骑着一头青牛，慢慢走向函谷关。

青牛不耀眼，也不稀奇。可是那条慢慢的路，很像老子说的“不贵难得之货”：不把闪亮的东西摆在心的中央。

### 第2页

到了关口，关令尹喜请他留下几句话。老子写下五千言，然后继续往西去。

他留下的是一卷书，不是一串宝石；是一条路，不是一场炫耀。

### 第3页

汉文帝也有一个故事。有人献上一匹千里马，说它一天能跑很远。

文帝没有马上收下。他问：我的车队一天只能走几十里，我一个人骑得再快，又能先到哪里去呢？

### 第4页

他把马退还，还给献马的人路费，又告诉四方：以后不要再送这种稀奇东西。

这不是不懂好马，而是不想让天下人都追着“难得之货”跑。

## 正文

### 第5页

空空村来了一个卖马人，牵着一匹“风一样快”的小马。他摇着铜铃喊：“谁买到它，谁就是全村最神气的人！”

孩子们全挤到路边，眼睛跟着小马转。地上的豆苗被踩弯了，也没人发现。

### 第6页

阿禾也心动了。他想象自己骑着小马冲过村口，大家都喊：“阿禾最厉害！”

这个念头刚冒出来，他的心就像被铜铃牵住，一路往前跑。

### 第7页

老人牵来村里的老牛。老牛走得慢，眼睛湿润，鼻子上还沾着草屑。

阿禾小声说：“它一点也不像宝贝。”老人摸摸牛背：“昨天大家的米袋，就是它一袋一袋拉回来的。”

### 第8页

小马很快，却只能载一个人。老牛很慢，却能拉一车粮食，也能把柴送到每家门口。

阿禾忽然明白：有用的东西，常常不站在最高的台上，也不摇铃叫大家来看。

### 第9页

卖马人又摇铜铃。小兔往前挤，小石头也往前挤，眼看就要吵起来。

老人请卖马人把铃声带远一些，又让孩子们去扶起被踩弯的豆苗。铃声一远，路边才慢慢安静。

### 第10页

傍晚，老牛拖着车，把木柴送到每家门口。没有掌声，也没有红绸。

可是每一家的炉火都亮了。阿禾觉得，这种暖，比“最神气”三个字踏实多了。

### 第11页

第二天，阿禾把那盒彩石拿出来。它们还是很美，只是不再摆在最高处。

他把彩石分给大家：大的压书角，小的标菜地，扁的放在路口当记号。宝贝不被供起来，反而帮上了忙。

### 第12页

老人说：“珍贵的东西可以用，也可以欣赏。只是别让它牵着人心跑。”

阿禾点点头。他想起老子的青牛，也想起那匹被汉文帝退回去的千里马。

## 回扣

### 第13页

“不贵难得之货”，不是把好东西都扔掉。它提醒我们：不要把稀奇东西捧成唯一的目标。

一旦大家眼里只剩宝贝，就容易忘记饭、路、树苗和身边的人。

### 第14页

“不尚贤”也是这样。真正有本领的人，不一定站在台上让人崇拜；他也可以像青牛拉车、像老牛送柴，把事情稳稳做好。

名头越亮，心越容易乱。事情越实在，村子越容易安。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：我不把最快的马放在心的正中央。我也要看见慢慢走的牛，看见每一袋米、每一束柴。

心不追着铃声跑，村子就能听见自己的脚步。

# Chapter 003-2: The Blue-Green Ox and the Thousand-Li Horse

## Allusion

### Page 1

Legend says that when Laozi left the Zhou royal court, he did not ride in a splendid carriage. He rode a blue-green ox slowly toward Hangu Pass.

The ox was not dazzling or rare. Yet that slow road matches Laozi's teaching: do not place shiny, hard-to-get things at the center of the heart.

### Page 2

At the pass, Yin Xi asked Laozi to leave some words behind. Laozi wrote five thousand characters, then continued west.

He left a book, not a string of jewels; a way, not a display.

### Page 3

Emperor Wen of Han has another story. Someone offered him a horse said to travel a thousand li in a day.

The emperor did not accept it at once. He asked: if my procession can only move a few dozen li a day, where could I go first by riding alone on the fastest horse?

### Page 4

He returned the horse, paid the giver's travel costs, and told the land not to send such rare gifts again.

He understood good horses. He simply did not want everyone chasing rare goods.

## Main Text

### Page 5

A horse seller came to Kongkong Village with a "wind-fast" pony. He rang a bronze bell and called, "Whoever buys it will be the grandest person in the village!"

The children crowded by the road, their eyes following the pony. Nobody noticed the bean sprouts being stepped flat.

### Page 6

Ahe's heart jumped too. He imagined riding through the village gate while everyone shouted, "Ahe is the greatest!"

As soon as that thought appeared, his heart seemed to be pulled by the bell and ran after it.

### Page 7

The old man led out the village ox. It walked slowly, with damp eyes and bits of grass on its nose.

"It does not look like treasure," Ahe whispered. The old man patted the ox's back. "Yesterday it brought everyone's rice sacks home, one by one."

### Page 8

The pony was fast, but it could carry only one person. The ox was slow, but it could pull a cart of grain and carry firewood to each door.

Ahe suddenly understood: useful things often do not stand on the highest platform or ring a bell for people to look.

### Page 9

The seller rang the bell again. Little Rabbit pushed forward, Little Stone pushed too, and everyone nearly argued.

The old man asked the seller to take the bell a little farther away. Then he sent the children to lift the bent bean sprouts. As the bell faded, the roadside slowly quieted.

### Page 10

At dusk, the ox pulled a cart and delivered firewood to each door. There was no applause and no red silk.

But every stove was lit. To Ahe, that warmth felt much steadier than being called grand.

### Page 11

The next day, Ahe brought out the box of colored stones. They were still beautiful, but he no longer placed them on the highest table.

He shared them out: large ones held book corners, small ones marked garden beds, flat ones became signs at the path. When treasure was not worshiped, it became helpful.

### Page 12

The old man said, "Precious things may be used and admired. Just do not let them pull the heart along."

Ahe nodded. He thought of Laozi's blue-green ox, and of the thousand-li horse Emperor Wen returned.

## Return to the Original

### Page 13

"Do not prize articles difficult to procure" does not mean throwing good things away. It means not lifting rare things into the one goal everyone must chase.

Once people see only treasure, they easily forget food, roads, seedlings, and the people beside them.

### Page 14

"Not to value superior ability" is similar. A truly capable person does not always stand on a stage to be admired; he may work like the ox carrying grain and firewood, making the matter steady.

The brighter the title, the easier the heart grows restless. The more real the work, the calmer the village becomes.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: I will not put the fastest horse at the center of my heart. I will also notice the slow ox, every sack of rice, and every bundle of wood.

When the heart stops running after bells, the village can hear its own footsteps.
