# 第005-1章：空空的风箱

## 原文

### 第1页

天地不仁，以万物为刍狗；
圣人不仁，以百姓为刍狗。
天地之间，其犹橐籥乎？
虚而不屈，动而愈出。
多言数穷，不如守中。

## 释义

### 第2页

这里的“不仁”，不是冷酷，也不是叫人没有爱心。王弼解释说，天地任万物自然生长、自然归去，不用人的喜欢和讨厌来安排一切。

雨落下来，不会先问哪朵花最乖；风吹过去，也不会只吹给谁一个人。

### 第3页

“刍狗”是古代祭礼里用草扎成的狗。祭礼前，人们郑重摆放它；祭礼结束后，它的用处完成了，就回到草木之中。

老子借这个比喻说：天地不把任何形状永远捧在手心，也不按人的偏爱来分配万物。

### 第4页

圣人也学天地，不把“我多仁慈”挂在嘴边。该扶的时候扶，该止的时候止，不让偏爱挡住整体。

真正的照顾，不是情绪一涌上来就乱答应、乱帮忙；它要看清事情怎样才能继续活下去。

### 第5页

“橐籥”像古代风箱，中间空空的。手一推一拉，风就送进炉膛，火也更旺。

河上公把这一章叫“虚用”。空不是没用，空让风有路；中间不被塞满，力量才能源源不断出来。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**仁**：这里不是日常说的善良，而是带着人情偏爱的“我想怎样对你”。

**刍狗**：用草扎成的祭礼之物。它不是被恨，只是不用人情去偏爱。

**橐籥**：古代风箱，靠中空和推拉送出风。

### 第7页

**虚而不屈**：空着，却不穷尽。像风箱中间空，才不会被堵住。

**动而愈出**：一动，作用反而更多。

**守中**：守住中心和分寸，不被话语、情绪和偏见拉偏。

## 正文

### 第8页

铁匠铺里有一只旧风箱。它肚子空空，木板被摸得发亮，手柄也磨得很光。

小徒弟问：“师父，空东西怎么能让炉火变旺？”

### 第9页

师父拉一下，风从管口冲进炉膛。火苗先低了一低，马上又亮起来。

他再推一下，炭火红得像星子。小徒弟睁大眼：这么大的风，竟是从空肚子里来的。

### 第10页

小徒弟拿来一团棉布，想塞进风箱。“塞满了，风就不会乱跑了吧？”

师父笑着摇头：“塞满了，它就不再会呼吸。”

### 第11页

傍晚下雨，雨点落在屋顶，也落在泥地、石头和野草上。它没有挑一朵最乖的花，只给那朵花水。

阿禾站在门口看了很久，忽然觉得：天地的不偏心，有时比偏爱更大。

### 第12页

第二天，孩子们争着解释风箱。有人说它神奇，有人说它普通，越说越急，声音挤成一团。

阿禾把手放在风箱边，等风吹过，才轻轻说：“它中间空，所以风能过。”

### 第13页

老人点点头：“话太多，会把意思挤没。话说到中心，心里反而有地方。”

孩子们安静下来，只听见风箱一推一拉，像天地在慢慢呼吸。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第五章不是说天地冷酷到不管万物。它说天地不是一本奖惩账本，不按私心给谁加糖、给谁下雨。

万物在规律里来去。懂这一点，人就少一点抱怨，多一点敬畏。

### 第15页

它也不是叫圣人不爱人。真正的爱，不忙着标榜自己，也不被偏私塞满。

像风箱守着中间的空，该动时动，该停时停。心中有空，风才不断；守住中心，话和行动才不乱。

# Chapter 005-1: The Empty Bellows

## Original Text

### Page 1

Heaven and earth do not act from (the impulse of) any wish to be benevolent; they deal with all things as the dogs of grass are dealt with.

The sages do not act from (any wish to be) benevolent; they deal with the people as the dogs of grass are dealt with.

### Page 2

May not the space between heaven and earth be compared to a bellows?

'Tis emptied, yet it loses not its power;
'Tis moved again, and sends forth air the more.

Much speech to swift exhaustion lead we see;
Your inner being guard, and keep it free.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Here, "not benevolent" does not mean cruel, and it does not tell us to have no love. Wang Bi explains that heaven and earth let things grow and pass according to their own nature, not according to human liking and dislike.

Rain does not first ask which flower is best behaved; wind does not blow for only one person.

### Page 4

"Straw dogs" were grass figures used in old rites. Before the rite, people placed them with care; when the rite was over, their use was complete, and they returned to grass.

Laozi uses this image to say that heaven and earth do not hold any one form forever in their hands, and they do not arrange the world by human favoritism.

### Page 5

The sage learns from heaven and earth. He does not hang a sign saying, "Look how kind I am." He helps when help is needed, stops when stopping is needed, and does not let favoritism cover the whole.

True care is not a rush of feeling that promises and interferes everywhere. It sees how life can continue.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**not benevolent** — Here this means not sentimental, not partial, and not ruled by favoritism.

**straw dogs** — Grass figures used in ancient rites. They are not hated; they are simply not favored by personal feeling.

**bellows** — An old tool that sends air into a fire.

### Page 7

**empty, yet not used up** — Empty space lets breath keep moving.

**moving, more comes out** — When the bellows moves, more air comes.

**keep the center** — Stay steady and balanced, without being pulled off course by words, fear, or bias.

## Main Text

### Page 8

In the blacksmith's shop, there was an old bellows. Its belly was hollow, its wood shone from years of touch, and its handle was smooth.

The apprentice asked, "Master, how can an empty thing make the fire stronger?"

### Page 9

The master pulled once, and wind rushed into the furnace. The flame dipped, then rose brighter.

He pushed again, and the coals glowed like stars. The apprentice stared: such a strong wind had come from a hollow belly.

### Page 10

The apprentice brought a wad of cotton and wanted to stuff it into the bellows. "If we fill it, the wind will not run around, right?"

The master smiled and shook his head. "If we fill it, it will no longer breathe."

### Page 11

At dusk, rain fell on the roof, the mud, the stones, and the wild grass. It did not choose the best flower and water only that one.

Ahe stood at the door for a long time and felt that heaven's not taking sides could be larger than favoritism.

### Page 12

The next day, the children argued about the bellows. One called it magic, another called it ordinary. Their words grew hurried and crowded together.

Ahe placed a hand beside it, waited for the wind, and then said softly, "It is hollow in the middle, so the wind can pass."

### Page 13

The old man nodded. "Too many words can crowd the meaning away. When words reach the center, the heart has room."

The children grew quiet. They only heard the bellows push and pull, like heaven and earth breathing slowly.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Five does not say that heaven and earth are cruel and careless. It says they are not a reward-and-punishment ledger, adding sugar here and rain there by private feeling.

All things come and go within patterns. Seeing this, we complain less and respect more.

### Page 15

It also does not tell the sage to stop loving people. True care does not rush to display itself, and it is not stuffed full of favoritism.

Like a bellows keeping its hollow center, it moves when needed and rests when needed. When the heart has room, the wind keeps coming; when the center is kept, speech and action do not scatter.
