# 第003-1章：把闪亮的东西收一收

## 原文

### 第1页

不尚贤，使民不争；

不贵难得之货，使民不为盗；

不见可欲，使民心不乱。

### 第2页

是以圣人之治，虚其心，实其腹，弱其志，强其骨。

常使民无知无欲。使夫知者不敢为也。

为无为，则无不治。

## 释义

### 第3页

“不尚贤”不是说有本领不好，也不是让大家都装笨。老子反对的是把“最聪明”“最厉害”做成高高的招牌，让人为了那个名头挤来挤去。

真正的本领应该让事情变好，而不是让人只想着压过别人。

### 第4页

“不贵难得之货”也不是讨厌好东西。稀奇的东西可以喜欢，可以使用，但不要把它摆得太高、照得太亮，让大家的心只剩一个“我要”。

心天天被勾着跑，手就容易伸向不该拿的地方。

### 第5页

“虚其心”，是把心里的攀比、贪念和小算盘清出一点空处。“实其腹”，是让人吃饱、睡稳、有力气过日子。

“弱其志”不是没志气，而是让争强好胜的劲柔下来；“强其骨”是把身体、规矩和生活的根基养结实。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**尚贤（shàng xián）**：把“贤能”当成招牌来崇拜。老子反对标榜名头，不是反对真实才能。

**争（zhēng）**：抢第一、抢名声、抢位置。心里只剩“我要赢”，就容易争。

### 第7页

**难得之货**：少见、贵重、容易让人想抢的东西。它也可以指权力、名声和特殊位置。

**可欲**：一看见就被勾起强烈欲望的东西。喜欢不一定错，被它牵着跑才会乱。

### 第8页

**虚其心**：让心不被杂念塞满。

**实其腹**：让基本生活安稳。

**无知无欲**：不是变笨，而是少一点机巧和贪求，守住朴素真实。

**为无为**：该做的做，不乱添自己的私心。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村要办秋日会。阿禾写了三块大牌子：最聪明、最快、故事讲得最好。

牌子还没挂上去，孩子们已经围过来问：“我能不能得第一？”“第一名有没有红绳子？”“要是我输了怎么办？”

### 第10页

小兔偷偷练跑步，越练越怕别人看见。小石头急着背故事，背到舌头打结。小鸟把嗓子喊哑了，晚饭也吃不香。

老人看了看那三块牌子，说：“这些字太亮了，把人的眼睛都照到名次上去了。”

### 第11页

阿禾又拿出一盒彩石，想摆在最高的桌子上。盒盖一开，红的像火，蓝的像夜空。

孩子们都伸长脖子，脚尖踮得高高的。小兔问能不能摸一下，小石头问谁能带回家，谁也没听见旁边有人说“让一让”。

### 第12页

老人没有责备他们。他把彩石收进布袋，又把最高的桌子搬走，换成一张长饭桌。

桌上放着热粥、青菜和馒头。孩子们坐下来，先是还盯着布袋，后来肚子暖了，手也不再往前伸。

### 第13页

吃完饭，老人把木条、绳子和小锤子放在地上。没有第一名，也没有奖牌，只有一座被雨冲歪的小桥。

小兔量绳，小鸟递木条，小石头敲钉。阿禾发现，没人抢着当“最厉害”的人，小桥反而很快站稳了。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第三章提醒我们：别用名声挑起争，别用宝贝挑起贪，别用欲望搅乱人心。

这不是把好东西都拿走，而是把多余的火收小一点。火太旺会烧焦饭；火稳一点，粥才会慢慢熟。

### 第15页

阿禾在本子上写：让肚子饱一点，让骨头稳一点，让心里的“我一定要赢”小一点。

这样不是没人做事。相反，大家不忙着争名头，手就能回到真正要做的事上。

# Chapter 003-1: Put the Shiny Things Away

## Original Text

### Page 1

Not to value and employ men of superior ability is the way to keep the people from rivalry among themselves; not to prize articles which are difficult to procure is the way to keep them from becoming thieves; not to show them what is likely to excite their desires is the way to keep their minds from disorder.

### Page 2

Therefore the sage, in the exercise of his government, empties their minds, fills their bellies, weakens their wills, and strengthens their bones.

He constantly (tries to) keep them without knowledge and without desire, and where there are those who have knowledge, to keep them from presuming to act (on it). When there is this abstinence from action, good order is universal.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

"Not to value men of superior ability" does not mean skill is bad, and it does not ask everyone to pretend to be foolish. Laozi is warning against turning "the smartest" or "the greatest" into a high signboard that people push and shove to reach.

Real ability should make the work better, not make people think only of standing above others.

### Page 4

"Not to prize articles difficult to procure" does not mean hating good things. Rare things may be enjoyed and used, but they should not be raised so high and made so bright that every heart says only, "I want that."

When the heart is pulled every day, the hand may reach toward what is not its own.

### Page 5

"Empties their minds" means clearing away some comparison, greed, and scheming. "Fills their bellies" means helping people eat, rest, and have strength for ordinary life.

"Weakens their wills" does not mean taking away courage; it softens the urge to win at any cost. "Strengthens their bones" means making the body, habits, and ground of life firm.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**value and employ men of superior ability** — Legge's phrase for 尚贤. Laozi warns against making talent into a public trophy, not against real skill.

**rivalry** — Fighting to be first, famous, or above others. When the heart only says "I must win," rivalry grows.

### Page 7

**articles difficult to procure** — Rare and costly things people may chase. They can also stand for power, rank, or special positions.

**excite their desires** — To make people want something so strongly that they forget what matters.

### Page 8

**empties their minds** — Clears the heart of clutter and restless wanting.

**fills their bellies** — Makes ordinary life steady and nourished.

**without knowledge and without desire** — Not stupidity; less scheming and less greed.

**abstinence from action** — Acting without selfish forcing.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Kongkong Village was preparing for the Autumn Gathering. Ahe wrote three big signs: Smartest, Fastest, Best Storyteller.

Before the signs were even hung, the children crowded around. "Can I win first?" "Will first place get a red cord?" "What if I lose?"

### Page 10

Little Rabbit practiced running in secret and grew afraid someone would see. Little Stone hurried to memorize stories until his tongue tangled. Little Bird shouted until her throat hurt, and even dinner tasted flat.

The old man looked at the three signs. "These words are too bright," he said. "They shine everyone's eyes onto ranking."

### Page 11

Then Ahe brought out a box of colored stones for the highest table. When the lid opened, red flashed like fire and blue like night.

The children stretched their necks and stood on tiptoe. Little Rabbit asked to touch one; Little Stone asked who could take one home. No one heard the person beside them say, "Please make room."

### Page 12

The old man blamed no one. He put the stones into a cloth bag, moved away the high table, and brought in a long dinner table.

On it were warm porridge, greens, and buns. The children sat down. At first they still watched the bag, but once their bellies warmed, their hands stopped reaching forward.

### Page 13

After dinner, the old man laid wood, rope, and a small hammer on the ground. No first place, no medal: only a little bridge bent by rain.

Little Rabbit measured rope, Little Bird passed wood, and Little Stone tapped nails. Ahe noticed that without anyone trying to be "the greatest," the bridge stood steady very soon.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Three reminds us: do not use fame to stir rivalry, treasures to stir greed, or desire to stir confusion.

This does not mean taking away all good things. It means lowering the extra fire. Too much flame burns the rice; a steady flame lets the porridge slowly cook.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote in his notebook: Let the belly be full, the bones be steady, and the thought "I must win" grow smaller.

Then people still work. In fact, when they are not busy fighting for titles, their hands can return to the thing that truly needs doing.
