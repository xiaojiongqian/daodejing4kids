# 第002-1章：一对一对出现

## 原文

### 第1页

天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已；皆知善之为善，斯不善已。

故有无相生，难易相成，长短相形，高下相倾，音声相和，前后相随。

### 第2页

是以圣人处无为之事，行不言之教。

万物作焉而不辞，生而不有，为而不恃，功成而弗居。夫唯弗居，是以不去。

## 释义

### 第3页

大家都说“这样才美”时，另一种样子很快就会被叫作“不美”。大家都说“这样才好”时，别的做法也容易被推到一边。

老子不是叫我们不要美，也不是叫我们不要做好事。他提醒我们：一旦只剩比较，心就会忙着分高低，忘了看事情本来的样子。

### 第4页

很多东西都是互相照出来的。没有短，就很难说什么叫长；没有低，也说不清什么叫高。

碗也是这样。碗壁摸得着，是“有”；中间空着，是“无”。正因为中间空，饭和汤才有地方住进去。

### 第5页

所以懂道的人做事，不是躲在旁边什么也不管。他会看清什么时候该帮，什么时候该等，什么时候别把自己的手伸得太满。

事情做好了，他也不急着把功劳抱在怀里。功劳一被抢着抱住，大家的心就又回到比较里；放开一点，好反而留得久。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**美（měi）**：让人喜欢、觉得好看的样子。

**恶（è）**：这里多指“不美”，不是骂人坏。

**善（shàn）**：好、合宜、做得对。

**不善（bù shàn）**：在比较里显出来的“不够好”，不等于没有价值。

### 第7页

**有无相生**：有和无互相显出对方。房间有墙，也要有空处，人才能住进去。

**长短相形**：长和短放在一起，才看得清。

**高下相倾**：高处和低处互相依着，才有高低。

### 第8页

**音声相和**：一个声音应着另一个声音，才成了音乐。

**无为**：不是不做事，而是不凭私心硬推、乱抢。

**不言之教**：少用话压人，多用行动给人看。

**弗居**：不把功劳占住不放。

## 正文

### 第9页

阿禾给小屋做书架，挑出两根竹条。他把一根举得高高的，说：“这根长，那根短。”

老人问：“如果桌上只有一根竹条，你还知道它长还是短吗？”阿禾低头看了看手里的竹条，半天没有答上来。

### 第10页

他们又看一只碗。阿禾摸摸硬硬的碗壁，说：“这里有东西。”

老人指着碗中间：“这里空着。可饭和汤，正是住在这个空里。要是把空处也塞满，碗就不能用了。”

### 第11页

小石头敲了一下木鼓，咚。小鸟吹了一声竹笛，呜。

分开听，只是一声响。等两声一前一后接上，院子里忽然有了一小段曲子。孩子们互相看了看，都笑了。

### 第12页

阿禾急着说：“我懂了，我来教你们！”他刚要站到凳子上，老人把鼓轻轻推到他面前。

老人没有讲大道理，只先听了一会儿，再敲一下；又等一会儿，再应一下。孩子们看着看着，声音慢慢低下来。

### 第13页

院角有几株豆苗，被风吹得歪来歪去。阿禾想把它们扶直，手伸到一半，又停住了。

他挪开旁边挡光的小石头，给泥土浇了一点水。豆苗没有立刻变直，可第二天早上，它们自己朝着太阳抬起了头。

## 回扣

### 第14页

回看这一章，老子让我们看见：世界不是孤零零的一件件东西，而常常是一对一对显出来的。

美和不美、有和无、难和易、长和短，都在互相照见。只盯住一边，心就容易变窄；看见另一边，事情就宽了。

### 第15页

“无为”也藏在这里：不抢着比较，不急着证明，不把自己摆到事情前面。该帮时帮，该等时等。

阿禾在本子上写：短让长显出来，空让碗能盛汤。做成一件事后，别忙着占住它，让好自己留下来。

# Chapter 002-1: Things Appear in Pairs

## Original Text

### Page 1

All in the world know the beauty of the beautiful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what ugliness is; they all know the skill of the skilful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what the want of skill is.

So it is that existence and non-existence give birth the one to (the idea of) the other; that difficulty and ease produce the one (the idea of) the other; that length and shortness fashion out the one the figure of the other; that (the ideas of) height and lowness arise from the contrast of the one with the other; that the musical notes and tones become harmonious through the relation of one with another; and that being before and behind give the idea of one following another.

### Page 2

Therefore the sage manages affairs without doing anything, and conveys his instructions without the use of speech.

All things spring up, and there is not one which declines to show itself; they grow, and there is no claim made for their ownership; they go through their processes, and there is no expectation (of a reward for the results). The work is accomplished, and there is no resting in it (as an achievement).

The work is done, but how no one can see;
'Tis this that makes the power not cease to be.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

When everyone says, "Only this is beautiful," another kind of beauty is quickly called "not beautiful." When everyone says, "Only this is good," other ways are pushed aside.

Laozi is not asking us to dislike beauty or stop doing good. He is warning us that when comparison takes over, the heart becomes busy ranking things and forgets to see them as they are.

### Page 4

Many things reveal each other. Without short, it is hard to say what long means; without low, high is not clear either.

A bowl shows this too. The wall you can touch is "being"; the hollow in the middle is "nonbeing." Because the middle is empty, rice and soup have somewhere to go.

### Page 5

So a person who understands Dao is not someone who hides away and does nothing. He sees when to help, when to wait, and when not to fill the matter with his own hands.

When the work is finished, he does not clutch the credit. Once credit is grabbed, everyone falls back into comparison. Let go a little, and the good can remain longer.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**beauty** — A form people enjoy or find lovely.

**ugliness** — Here, the "not beautiful" side created by comparison. It is not a word for shaming someone.

**skilful** — Able, good at doing something.

**want of skill** — The "not skilled enough" side that appears once a standard is set.

### Page 7

**existence and non-existence** — Being and nonbeing showing one another. A room has walls, but people live in the open space.

**length and shortness** — Long and short become clear only side by side.

**height and lowness** — High and low lean on each other.

### Page 8

**musical notes and tones become harmonious** — One sound answers another, and music appears.

**without doing anything** — Not doing nothing; not forcing from selfish will.

**without the use of speech** — Teaching more by example than by pressure.

**no resting in it** — Not sitting on your achievement as if it belonged only to you.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Ahe was making a shelf for his little house. He picked out two bamboo strips, held one high, and said, "This one is long, and that one is short."

The old man asked, "If there were only one strip on the table, would you still know whether it was long or short?" Ahe looked down at the bamboo in his hand and had no answer.

### Page 10

Then they looked at a bowl. Ahe touched the hard wall and said, "This part has something."

The old man pointed to the middle. "This part is empty. But rice and soup live in that emptiness. If the hollow were filled too, the bowl could not be used."

### Page 11

Little Stone tapped a wooden drum: dong. Little Bird blew a bamboo pipe: woo.

Separate, each was only a sound. When one followed the other, a tiny tune appeared in the yard. The children looked at one another and laughed.

### Page 12

Ahe rushed to say, "I understand! Let me teach everyone!" Just as he was about to climb onto a stool, the old man gently slid the drum toward him.

The old man did not give a lecture. He listened first, then tapped once; waited again, then answered once. As the children watched, their voices slowly softened.

### Page 13

In the corner of the yard, a few bean sprouts leaned in the wind. Ahe wanted to hold them straight, but his hand stopped halfway.

He moved a small stone that blocked the light and watered the soil. The sprouts did not straighten at once, but the next morning they had lifted their heads toward the sun.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Looking back, Laozi shows us that the world is not made of lonely, separate things. Often, things appear in pairs.

Beauty and not-beauty, being and nonbeing, hard and easy, long and short all reveal one another. If we stare at only one side, the heart narrows; when we notice the other side, the matter opens.

### Page 15

"Without doing anything" is here too: do not hurry to compare, prove, or put yourself in front of the matter. Help when help is needed; wait when waiting is needed.

Ahe wrote in his notebook: Short reveals long. Emptiness lets the bowl hold soup. When something is done well, do not rush to possess it. Let the goodness stay by itself.
