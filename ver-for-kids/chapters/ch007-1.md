# 第007-1章：把自己放在后面

## 原文

### 第1页

天长地久。
天地所以能长且久者，以其不自生，故能长生。
是以圣人后其身而身先；
外其身而身存。
非以其无私邪，故能成其私。

## 释义

### 第2页

第七章的重点，不是“我先吃亏，后来换奖赏”。

它说的是一条因果：如果只围着自己活，人就会处处抢位置、怕失去；不把自己当中心，反而能和更多人、更多事一起长久。

### 第3页

王弼解释“不自生”时说：只为自己生，就会和万物争。

天地不抢云、水、风、草木的位置，万物反而都能在天地里来去。它不把自己塞满，所以容得下万物，也显得长久。

### 第4页

河上公说，天地安静地施与万物，却不求回报。

不求回报，不是没有作用。正因为不把每一点作用都算成自己的功劳，作用才不会被私心耗尽。

### 第5页

圣人学的正是这个因果。“后其身”，是先让事情成、让众人安；大家信他，才自然把他放到前面。

“外其身”，是不让那一点得失管住自己。这样保存下来的，不只是身体，还有信誉、道路和更深的生命。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**长久**：很长很长地延续。这里指不靠争抢而能持续。

**不自生**：不只为自己活，不把自己当作所有事情的中心。

**长生**：在这里不是求仙不死，而是因不争而能长久。

### 第7页

**后其身**：把自己放在后面，先顾到事情和别人。

**身先**：反而走到前面，得到大家信任。

**外其身**：不把自己那一点得失看得最大。

**无私**：没有偏私的盘算，所以能成就更深的自己。

## 正文

### 第8页

夏天，空空村要给过路人搭一座凉棚。孩子们搬竹竿，老人们编草帘，地上堆着绳子和木桩。

阿禾被推出来当小队长。他心里有点高兴，也有点想站到最前面，让大家都看见。

### 第9页

太阳很大，大家都想先喝酸梅汤。阿禾刚伸手，老人把水勺递给他。

老人说：“你来分。”阿禾愣了一下。他想到：如果队长先给自己倒，大家看的就不是凉棚，而是桶里还剩多少。

### 第10页

他先给最小的孩子倒，又给搬竹竿的叔叔倒，再给额头冒汗的老人倒。

轮到自己时，桶底只剩一点点。一个小女孩把半杯推回来，小声说：“阿禾哥哥也热。”

### 第11页

午后忽然起风，草帘被吹得翻起来。阿禾本想躲到屋檐下，却看见棚角的竹绳快松了。

他知道自己一躲，别人也容易散开。于是他跑到棚边，踮脚拉住绳子。雨点打在脸上，手心也被磨红。

### 第12页

风停以后，凉棚没有倒。过路人坐在棚下，鞋边滴着雨水，脸上却有了笑。

大家商量怎么加固时，第一个转头看阿禾。不是因为他喊得最大，而是因为刚才需要有人站出来时，他在。

### 第13页

傍晚，阿禾抬头看天。天空盖住每一间屋子，却没有在云上写自己的名字。

他又低头看地。土地托住每一双脚，也没有问谁先夸它。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第七章不是教人用退后来换掌声。退后若只是手段，私心还是站在最前面。

老子说的因果更朴素：越把小小的“我”摆在中心，越容易争、怕、累；越先看见整体，自己反而越站得稳。

### 第15页

真正的“后”，让大家看见可以信任的人；真正的“外”，让害怕不再指挥行动。

阿禾写下：天空不抢位置，所以万物都在它下面生长。把自己放进更大的事情里，不是消失，而是和大家一起长久。

# Chapter 007-1: Putting Yourself Behind

## Original Text

### Page 1

Heaven is long-enduring and earth continues long. The reason why heaven and earth are able to endure and continue thus long is because they do not live of, or for, themselves. This is how they are able to continue and endure.

Therefore the sage puts his own person last, and yet it is found in the foremost place; he treats his person as if it were foreign to him, and yet that person is preserved. Is it not because he has no personal and private ends, that therefore such ends are realised?

## Interpretation

### Page 2

The point of Chapter Seven is not, "I lose first, then get a reward later."

It shows a cause and effect: if we live only around ourselves, we keep grabbing places and fearing loss; when we do not make ourselves the center, we can endure with more people and more life.

### Page 3

Wang Bi says this about "not living for themselves": if they lived only for themselves, they would struggle against all things.

Heaven and earth do not seize space from clouds, water, wind, or plants. All things can come and go within them. Because they do not fill everything with themselves, they can hold many things and so they endure.

### Page 4

Heshang Gong says heaven and earth quietly give to all things without asking for repayment.

Not asking for repayment does not mean having no use. Because they do not count every use as their own credit, their usefulness is not worn out by selfishness.

### Page 5

The sage learns this same cause and effect. To put himself last means first letting the matter be done and the people be safe; because people trust him, they naturally place him in front.

To treat himself as outside means not letting one bit of gain or loss rule him. What is preserved is not only the body, but trust, the road, and a deeper life.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**long-enduring** — Lasting for a very long time. Here it means lasting without fighting to possess.

**do not live for themselves** — They do not make themselves the center of everything.

**continue and endure** — Keep going and remain.

### Page 7

**puts his own person last** — Puts himself behind the task and the people.

**foremost place** — The front place, where trust gathers.

**foreign to him** — Not treated as the biggest concern.

**private ends** — Selfish aims or hidden personal gain.

## Main Text

### Page 8

In summer, Kongkong Village built a shade shelter for travelers. The children carried bamboo poles, the elders wove grass mats, and ropes and stakes lay on the ground.

Ahe was chosen as the small team leader. He felt pleased, and part of him wanted to stand in front where everyone could see him.

### Page 9

The sun was hot, and everyone wanted sour plum drink first. Just as Ahe reached for a cup, the old man handed him the ladle.

"You serve," said the old man. Ahe paused. He thought: if the leader poured for himself first, everyone would watch the bucket, not the shelter.

### Page 10

He poured first for the smallest children, then for the uncles carrying bamboo, then for the elders with sweat on their foreheads.

When his turn came, only a little remained. A small girl pushed back half her cup and whispered, "Brother Ahe is hot too."

### Page 11

In the afternoon, wind rose and lifted the grass mats. Ahe wanted to hide under the eaves, but he saw a bamboo rope loosening at the corner.

He knew that if he hid first, others might scatter too. So he ran to the shelter's edge and pulled the rope tight. Rain struck his face, and the rope reddened his palms.

### Page 12

When the wind stopped, the shelter still stood. Travelers sat beneath it with wet shoes, but their faces had softened.

When everyone discussed how to strengthen the shelter, they first looked at Ahe. Not because he had shouted the loudest, but because when someone was needed, he had been there.

### Page 13

At dusk, Ahe looked up at the sky. It covered every roof, yet wrote no name on the clouds.

Then he looked at the ground. It carried every pair of feet, yet did not ask who praised it first.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Seven does not teach people to step back in order to trade for praise. If stepping back is only a trick, private desire is still standing in front.

Laozi's cause and effect is simpler: the more the little "me" stands at the center, the more we fight, fear, and tire; the more we see the whole first, the more steady our own place becomes.

### Page 15

True "behind" lets people see someone trustworthy. True "outside" keeps fear from commanding the action.

Ahe wrote: The sky does not seize a place, so all things grow beneath it. Putting myself inside a larger matter is not disappearing; it is enduring together.
