# 第003-3章：金手指和放下的权杖

## 典故引入

### 第1页

希腊神话里有位迈达斯王，已经有许多财宝，却还想要更多。他许愿：碰到什么，什么就变成黄金。

愿望刚实现时，他高兴极了。石头、树枝、桌子，都在他手下变得金光闪闪。

### 第2页

可是他拿起面包，面包变成金子；端起水，水也变成金子。传说里，连他最爱的孩子跑来拥抱他，也变成了金色雕像。

他这才明白：最想要的东西，有时会把最需要的东西拿走。

### 第3页

另一个故事来自古罗马。辛辛纳图斯原本在田里耕作，国家遇到急难，人们请他出来掌权救城。

他把事情办完后，没有抱住权力不放，而是回到自己的农田。

### 第4页

很久以后，华盛顿也做过相似的事。他带兵打赢战争后交还军权，后来总统任期满了，也回到自己的庄园。

能拿起权力并不稀奇，能在该放下时放下，才难。

## 正文

### 第5页

阿禾听完迈达斯的故事，做了一个游戏。他把纸片剪成金币，贴在杯子、馒头和小椅子上。

一开始大家都笑。过了一会儿，小兔拿起馒头说：“金馒头不能吃呀。”

### 第6页

小鸟也说：“金杯子里没有水。”小石头抱着金椅子坐下去，纸片哗啦啦掉了一地。

阿禾看着满屋金光，忽然觉得眼睛累。东西太亮时，人反而看不清自己真正需要什么。

### 第7页

老人端来一碗热汤。碗没有金边，也没有花纹，只冒着白白的热气。

孩子们一口一口喝下去，肚子慢慢暖起来。桌边的金纸片卷起了角，没人在意它们了。

### 第8页

下午，他们又玩“村长椅”的游戏。谁坐上椅子，谁就可以发号施令。

小兔坐上去不肯下来，小石头也想坐，小鸟在旁边急得直跺脚。小小一把椅子，竟让院子吵成一团。

### 第9页

老人讲了辛辛纳图斯的故事。那个农夫拿起权力，是为了救人；事情一成，就回去扶犁。

阿禾摸摸椅背，觉得这把椅子忽然没有那么好玩了。

### 第10页

他坐上去，只说了一句话：“我们先修水沟。”说完，他就站起来，去拿小铲子。

小兔眨眨眼，也从椅子上跳下来。大家跟着去挖泥、搬石头、引水。

### 第11页

水沟通了，菜地喝到了水。没有人再问谁坐了多久村长椅。

阿禾发现，权杖如果只是拿来挥舞，会让人争；如果把它当工具，用完放下，事情就清楚了。

### 第12页

傍晚，孩子们把金币纸片收进盒子，把村长椅搬回屋檐下。

桌上只剩热汤、馒头和清水。它们不闪亮，却让大家安安稳稳。

## 回扣

### 第13页

第三章说“不见可欲，使民心不乱”。迈达斯的金手指让阿禾看见：欲望太亮，会把面包、水和拥抱都推远。

所以不是不能喜欢好东西，而是不要让“我想要”坐到心的正中央。

### 第14页

第三章也说“为无为”。辛辛纳图斯和华盛顿让阿禾看见：有时最好的做，是把该做的事做完，然后退一步。

不把权力和功劳抓在手里，事情反而能稳稳留给大家。

### 第15页

阿禾写下：金子不能替我喝水，椅子不能替我修沟。

我愿意让心空一点，肚子暖一点，骨头有力一点。这样，手里没有金光，也能把今天过好。

# Chapter 003-3: The Golden Touch and the Laid-Down Staff

## Allusion

### Page 1

In Greek myth, King Midas already had many treasures, but wanted more. He wished that everything he touched would turn to gold.

When the wish first came true, he was delighted. Stones, branches, and tables shone under his hand.

### Page 2

But when he picked up bread, it became gold; when he lifted water, it became gold too. In the story, even the child he loved became a golden statue when running to embrace him.

Only then did he understand: what we most want can sometimes take away what we most need.

### Page 3

Another story comes from ancient Rome. Cincinnatus was farming when the city faced danger, and the people asked him to take power and save it.

When the work was done, he did not cling to power. He returned to his field.

### Page 4

Much later, George Washington did something similar. After winning the war, he returned military power; after his terms as president, he went back to his estate.

Taking up power is not so rare. Laying it down at the right time is harder.

## Main Text

### Page 5

After hearing the Midas story, Ahe made a game. He cut paper into coins and pasted them on cups, buns, and little chairs.

At first everyone laughed. After a while, Little Rabbit picked up a bun and said, "A golden bun cannot be eaten."

### Page 6

Little Bird said, "A golden cup has no water." Little Stone tried to sit on a golden chair, and paper coins rustled all over the floor.

Ahe looked at the shining room and felt his eyes grow tired. When things glitter too much, people may stop seeing what they truly need.

### Page 7

The old man brought a bowl of hot soup. The bowl had no golden edge and no pattern, only white steam rising.

The children drank slowly, and their bellies warmed. The paper coins curled at the edge of the table, and no one cared about them anymore.

### Page 8

In the afternoon, they played "village-chief chair." Whoever sat on the chair could give orders.

Little Rabbit sat and would not get down. Little Stone wanted a turn, and Little Bird stamped in worry. One small chair had made the whole yard noisy.

### Page 9

The old man told the story of Cincinnatus. The farmer took power to save people; once the matter was finished, he went back to the plow.

Ahe touched the chair back. Suddenly the chair did not seem so fun.

### Page 10

He sat down and said only one thing: "Let us mend the water ditch first." Then he stood up and picked up a small shovel.

Little Rabbit blinked, then jumped down too. Everyone went to dig mud, move stones, and guide the water.

### Page 11

The ditch opened, and the vegetable beds drank. No one asked who had sat longest on the chief's chair.

Ahe saw that if a staff is only waved, people fight. If it is used as a tool and then put down, the work becomes clear.

### Page 12

At dusk, the children put the paper coins in a box and carried the chief's chair back under the eaves.

On the table were only soup, buns, and clear water. They did not shine, but they made everyone steady.

## Return to the Original

### Page 13

Chapter Three says, "not to show them what is likely to excite their desires." Midas's golden touch shows Ahe that desire can shine so brightly that bread, water, and embraces are pushed away.

This does not mean we cannot love good things. It means "I want" should not sit in the very center of the heart.

### Page 14

Chapter Three also teaches action without forcing. Cincinnatus and Washington show Ahe that sometimes the best doing is to finish what needs doing, then step back.

When power and credit are not clutched, the result can remain steady for everyone.

### Page 15

Ahe wrote: Gold cannot drink water for me, and a chair cannot mend a ditch for me.

I will keep my heart a little emptier, my belly warmer, and my bones stronger. Then even without glitter in my hands, I can live this day well.
