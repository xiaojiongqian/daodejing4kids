# 第001-2章：摸到的不是整头象

## 典故引入

### 第1页

古印度有一个流传很久的故事。城里来了大象，脚掌踩在地上，像小鼓一样咚咚响。

几位从没见过大象的人被带到院子里。他们眼睛看不见，只能伸出手，慢慢摸。

### 第2页

一个人抱住象腿，摸到又粗又硬的一截，说：“我知道了，大象像柱子。”

另一个人摸到尾巴，细细的，末梢还有一撮毛，就摇头说：“不对，大象明明像绳子。”

### 第3页

摸到耳朵的人说：“你们都错了，它像一把大扇子。”摸到鼻子的人吓了一跳，因为那东西会弯，会卷，还会呼出热气。

摸到象牙的人说像长矛，摸到肚皮的人说像一堵墙。每个人都摸得很认真，也都只摸到了一处。

### 第4页

他们越说越急，声音一声高过一声。谁也不是故意说假话，可谁都把自己摸到的一角，当成了整头大象。

看象的人说：“你们摸到的都是真的。只是大象比你们手里的那一小块大得多。”这句话，正好能帮我们读第一章的“妙”和“徼”。

## 正文

### 第5页

老人把这个故事讲给阿禾听。阿禾听完，立刻站起来：“我们也试试！”

他请小兔、小鸟和小石头闭上眼睛，走到村口那棵大榕树旁边。每个人只能摸一处，摸完就说它像什么。

### 第6页

小兔摸到粗树干，手臂张开也抱不住：“像一堵墙。”

小鸟摸到垂下来的气根，细细长长，扫过手背：“像一把旧扫帚。”小石头摸到树叶，笑了：“像好多小手掌。”

### 第7页

阿禾摸到树皮上的裂缝。裂缝弯弯曲曲，指尖一走进去，就像走进干掉的小河。

大家睁开眼，马上争起来。小兔说树就是墙，小鸟说树是扫帚，小石头说树是手掌，阿禾差点把“干河树”写进名字本里。

### 第8页

老人没有急着评谁对谁错。他请大家围着榕树走一圈。

他们看见树干撑住树冠，气根一条条垂进土里，叶子接住阳光，树皮护着里面的汁水。刚才每个人摸到的一处，都在这棵树上安了家。

### 第9页

“原来我们都没说错。”小兔小声说。

“也都没说完。”阿禾接了一句。他忽然明白，急着证明自己对的时候，眼睛虽然睁着，心却还只停在手摸到的那一块。

### 第10页

下午，孩子们在榕树下乘凉。小鸟说：“它叫榕树。”小兔说：“它还是一大片阴凉。”

小石头抬头看见鸟窝，说：“它是小鸟的屋顶。”阿禾看见坡边的土没有被雨冲走，说：“它也是泥土的手。”

### 第11页

名字越说越多，榕树却没有被说完。风一吹，叶子翻出一层一层绿光。

阿禾觉得，名字像许多小窗。每扇窗都能看见一点，可窗外的树，比任何一扇窗都大。

### 第12页

夜里，阿禾问老人：“道也像大象和榕树吗？我们摸到一点，就以为摸到了全部？”

老人说：“这个比方能送你到门口。可是比方不是道，故事也不是道。真到了门口，还要自己静一静，看一看。”

### 第13页

阿禾没有马上再问。他把手放在胸口，听见自己的心跳一下一下。

他还是不懂“道”到底是什么。奇怪的是，这一次，他不急着把“不懂”赶走了。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第一章说“常有欲，以观其徼”。带着问题、用途和想法去看，我们常常能看见边界。

摸到象腿、摸到树皮、看见树荫，这些都是真的。只是边界很有用，却不是全部。

### 第15页

“常无欲，以观其妙”，不是叫人没有好奇心。它是让心先松开一点，别急着把“我摸到了”说成“我全懂了”。

心一松，别人的发现能进来，事物自己的样子也能进来。奥妙常常就在这个空处露头。

### 第16页

阿禾在本子上写：我摸到的是真的，但不一定是全部。

只想赢的时候，我看见边缘；愿意听的时候，门会开大一点。道也许就在这些门外，又比所有门都更深。

# Chapter 001-2: What You Touch Is Not the Whole Elephant

## Allusion

### Page 1

An old Indian story tells of an elephant brought into a courtyard. Its feet thudded on the ground like small drums.

Several people who had never known an elephant were led close. They could not see, so they reached out and touched slowly.

### Page 2

One person wrapped his arms around a leg. It was thick and firm. "Now I know," he said. "An elephant is like a pillar."

Another touched the tail, thin with a tuft at the end. "No," he said. "An elephant is clearly like a rope."

### Page 3

The one who touched an ear said, "You are both wrong. It is like a big fan." The one who touched the trunk jumped, because that part bent, curled, and breathed warm air.

The one who touched a tusk said it was like a spear. The one who touched the belly said it was like a wall. Each person touched carefully, and each touched only one place.

### Page 4

Their voices rose. No one meant to lie, but each person called his one small part the whole elephant.

The keeper said, "What you touched is real. But the elephant is much larger than the piece in your hands." This helps us read Chapter One's mystery and outer fringe.

## Main Text

### Page 5

The old man told this story to Ahe. Ahe stood up at once. "Let us try too!"

He asked Little Rabbit, Little Bird, and Little Stone to close their eyes and stand beside the big banyan tree at the village gate. Each person could touch one place and say what it felt like.

### Page 6

Little Rabbit touched the thick trunk and could not wrap his arms around it. "It is like a wall."

Little Bird touched the hanging roots. They were long and thin and brushed her hand. "It is like an old broom." Little Stone touched the leaves and laughed. "Like many small hands."

### Page 7

Ahe touched the cracks in the bark. They twisted under his fingers like dried little rivers.

When everyone opened their eyes, they argued at once. Little Rabbit said the tree was a wall; Little Bird said it was a broom; Little Stone said it was hands. Ahe nearly wrote "dry-river tree" in his notebook.

### Page 8

The old man did not hurry to say who was right. He asked them to walk around the banyan.

They saw the trunk holding up the crown, the roots reaching into soil, the leaves catching sunlight, and the bark guarding the sap inside. Each part they had touched had a home in the tree.

### Page 9

"So none of us was wrong," Little Rabbit said softly.

"And none of us was finished," Ahe added. He suddenly understood: when he rushed to prove himself right, his eyes were open, but his heart stayed with only the part his hand had touched.

### Page 10

In the afternoon, the children sat in the banyan shade. Little Bird said, "It is called a banyan." Little Rabbit said, "It is also a great cool shade."

Little Stone looked up at a nest. "It is a roof for birds." Ahe saw how the slope had not washed away in the rain. "It is also the hand of the soil."

### Page 11

More and more names appeared, yet the banyan was not used up. Wind turned its leaves into layer after layer of green light.

Ahe felt that names were small windows. Each window showed something, but the tree outside was larger than any window.

### Page 12

That night, Ahe asked, "Is Dao like the elephant and the banyan? We touch one part and think we have touched the whole?"

The old man said, "This comparison can bring you to the gate. But a comparison is not Dao, and a story is not Dao. At the gate, you still have to grow quiet and look for yourself."

### Page 13

Ahe did not ask another question at once. He placed his hand on his chest and heard his heart beating.

He still did not know exactly what Dao was. Strangely, this time he was not in a hurry to chase the not-knowing away.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter One says that if desire is always within us, "its outer fringe is all that we shall see." With questions, uses, and wishes, we often see edges.

Touching the elephant's leg, touching the tree bark, noticing the shade: all of these are real. But an edge, useful as it is, is not the whole.

### Page 15

"Always without desire" does not mean having no curiosity. It means loosening the heart before saying, "I touched it, so I understand all of it."

When the heart loosens, another person's discovery can enter, and the thing itself can enter too. Wonder often appears in that open space.

### Page 16

Ahe wrote in his notebook: What I touched is real, but it may not be all.

When I only want to win, I see the edge. When I listen, the gate opens wider. Dao may be outside these gates, and deeper than all of them.
